Abstract: High-capacity associative memories based on Kernel Logistic Regression (KLR) are known for their exceptional performance but are hindered by high computational costs. This paper investigates the compressibility of KLR-trained Hopfield networks to understand the geometric principles of its robust encoding. We provide a comprehensive geometric theory based on spontaneous symmetry breaking and Walsh analysis, and validate it with compression experiments (quantization and pruning). Our experiments reveal a striking contrast: the network is extremely robust to low-precision quantization but highly sensitive to pruning. Our theory explains this via a “sparse function, dense representation” principle, where a sparse input mapping is implemented with a dense, bimodal parameterization. Our findings not only provide a practical path to hardware-efficient kernel memories but also offer new insights into the geometric principles of robust representation in neural systems. 

Key Words: kernel associative memory, quantization robustness, holographic representation, Walsh analysis, representational duality 

1. Introduction 

Associative memory models, epitomized by the Hop-field network, have experienced a renaissance with the integration of modern machine learning techniques. In particular, our recent work demonstrated that Ker-nel Logistic Regression (KLR) learning can drasti-cally enhance storage capacity and robustness, achiev-ing performance far exceeding classical limits [1, 2]. These “kernelized” memories operate by embedding patterns into a high-dimensional feature space, cre-ating deep and sharp attractor basins. Furthermore, we have theoretically established that this high perfor-mance originates from a geometric self-organization mechanism termed Spectral Concentration [3]. In the broader context of deep learning, model com-pression has become a critical research frontier. With the advent of Large Language Models, techniques like 4-bit or even 1-bit quantization (e.g., BitNet [4]) are revolutionizing how we deploy massive models. Whether such extreme compression is applicable to kernel-based associative memories, however, remains an open question. Addressing this question is crucial because the high performance of KLR-trained Hopfield networks comes at a significant cost. The retrieval process in kernel networks involves computing weighted sums over all stored patterns using high-precision dual vari-ables, leading to significant computational and mem-ory overheads. This poses a major bottleneck for deployment on resource-constrained edge devices or neuromorphic hardware, where memory bandwidth and energy efficiency are critical. 2. Related Work 

Our work on compressing kernel associative memo-ries connects to three broader research areas: low-precision deep learning, models of distributed repre-sentation, and the neurobiology of synaptic precision. 

2.1 Low-Precision Deep Learning 

The push for low-precision inference is well-established in deep learning. Binarized Neural Net-works have demonstrated that weights can be reduced to 1-bit without catastrophic loss of accuracy for com-plex tasks [5, 6]. More recently, the success of 1.58-bit quantization in Large Language Models (e.g., Bit-Net [4]) has highlighted the surprising effectiveness of extreme compression. Our work extends these find-ings from feedforward networks to the domain of re-current, kernel-based associative memories, showing that similar robustness can be achieved. 

2.2 Holographic and Distributed Representa-tions 

The concept of a “holographic” representation, where information is distributed rather than localized, has a rich history. It aligns with paradigms like Holo-graphic Reduced Representations [7] and the broader framework of Vector Symbolic Architectures [8, 9]. In these models, information is encoded holistically across high-dimensional vectors, enabling robust su-perposition and retrieval. In the context of this paper, we define a representa-tion as “holographic” if it exhibits two key properties: (1) information is distributed across all parameters, making the system sensitive to sparsity, and (2) the system is robust to local perturbations of those param-eters, such as low-precision quantization. Our findings suggest that KLR learning naturally discovers a cod-ing scheme that satisfies these properties to maximize capacity. 

2.3 Biological Plausibility of Low-Precision Synapses 

Physiologically, our results resonate with the princi-ples of neural computation in the brain. Biological systems are known to operate efficiently with noisy and imprecise components. Seminal work by Bartol et al. [10] provided nanoconnectomic evidence that hippocampal synapses likely operate with very low precision (approximately 4.7 bits). This suggests that nature, like our KLR model on the Ridge, prioritizes robust, low-precision distributed storage over high-precision localized information.

3.4 The Key Question 

The opposing responses to these two compression methods present a puzzle. If the network is robust to the massive distortion of quantization, why is it so fragile to the seemingly milder intervention of pruning? Answering this question requires a deeper theoretical analysis of the geometric structure of the learned representation, which we will develop in the following section. 

4. Theoretical Analysis of Representation Geometry 

In this section, we analyze the geometric structure of the learned representation from complementary view-points. Section 4.1 provides an effective dynamical interpretation for the emergence of a bimodal weight distribution through symmetry breaking. Section 4.2 examines the functional structure in the input space via Walsh influence analysis, clarifying the distinction be-tween functional sparsity and dense parameterization. Based on these results, Section 4.3 summarizes the re-sulting duality of representation. Finally, Section 4.4 quantitatively validates the proposed geometric pic-ture by analyzing the scaling behavior of performance degradation under quantization.

This corresponds to a symmet-ric state where the model has no preference for any particular feature direction. 2. 4.2 Holographic vs. Sparse Influence: A Walsh Analysis 

Our results reveal a fundamental property of the mem-ory representation on the Ridge, which our previous work identified as a critical state of Spectral Concen-tration [3]. The failure of sparsity pruning (Fig. 2) motivates an investigation into the information structure of the learned representation. To explore this quantitatively, we employ Walsh Analysis, a set of Fourier-analytic tools for analyzing Boolean functions [12]. We treat the update function ℎ 𝑗 (𝒔 ) for a single output neuron 

𝑗 as a function mapping {− 1, 1}𝑁 → R and compute the influence of each input neuron 𝑠 𝑖 . The influence 

𝐼 𝑖 (ℎ 𝑗 ) measures the probability that flipping input 𝑠 𝑖 

changes the sign of the output ℎ 𝑗 , thus quantifying its functional importance. We compared the influence distribution for the stan-dard KLR model ( 𝐿 2 regularization) with a model trained with 𝐿 1 regularization (Lasso), which explic-itly promotes sparsity. To isolate the effect of inter-neuron couplings, we analyzed the “cross-influence” by excluding the dominant self-influence term ( 𝑖 = 𝑗 ). The results are shown in Fig. 3. Contrary to a naive holographic hypothesis (which would predict a uniform, flat distribution), both mod-els appear to exhibit a sparse influence structure, in-dicating that the KLR network performs a form of feature selection. However, a crucial quantitative dif-ference reveals the underlying nature of the 𝐿 2-model. The 𝐿 2-regularized model (the left panel of Fig. 3) retains a richer, denser tail of small cross-influences, resulting in a Gini coefficient of 𝐺 ≈ 0.305. In con-trast, the 𝐿 1-model (the right panel of Fig. 3) exhibits a much more unequal distribution with a significantly higher Gini coefficient of 𝐺 ≈ 0.347. This result sug-gests that while the KLR input mapping has sparse characteristics, its underlying parameterization is fun-damentally more distributed (holographic), explaining its sensitivity to pruning. 

4.3 Geometric Interpretation: The Duality of Representation 

This subsection integrates the preceding analyses into a unified geometric interpretation of the learned repre-sentation. Combining the analyses of the weight dis-tribution and input influence, our results point towards a dualistic view of the KLR memory representation: 

• Parameter Space (Digital & Dense): The weights 𝑨 self-organize into a bimodal, digital-like structure. This representation appears to be dense (not sparse), which could be the source of its fragility to pruning. However, its intrinsic discreteness makes it extremely robust to quanti-zation. 

• Input Space (Sparse Influence): The function computed by the network appears to be sparse in its input dependencies. This suggests that the network learns to focus on a subset of informa-tive features, a characteristic of effective feature learning. This proposed duality offers a resolution to the ap-parent contradiction. The network may not be naively holographic. Instead, it seems to employ a sophisti-cated strategy: it learns a sparse mapping in the input space (for efficiency) but implements it using a dense, distributed, and robust parameterization in the feature

4.4 Scaling Law of Quantization Error 

This subsection does not introduce a new theoretical mechanism. Instead, it provides a quantitative valida-tion of the geometric interpretation developed in the preceding subsections by examining how performance degradation scales with quantization strength. Theoretically, under a local quadratic approxima-tion of the loss landscape, the degradation is expected to scale quadratically with the quantization step size 

Δ (see Appendix A for a heuristic derivation). To test this hypothesis, we measured the degradation of the Stability Margin for a range of quantization bit depths 

𝑘 ∈ { 16 , 12 , 10 , 8, 7, 6, 5, 4, 3, 2}, both on and off the Ridge. While we focus on the stability margin as a sensitive probe, similar trends were observed in other performance metrics (not shown). The results for the “On Ridge” regime are shown in Fig. 4. Note that data points are only visible where the degradation is positive; for higher bit depths, the degradation was negligible (zero or negative) and thus omitted by the log scale. The visible points are con-sistent with a power-law relationship. A linear fit on the logarithmic data yields a slope of approximately 0.80, establishing the empirical scaling relation: Degradation ∝ ( Δ2)𝛽 , with 𝛽 ≈ 0.80 . (4) Although this exponent deviates slightly from the ideal quadratic prediction ( 𝛽 = 1), the presence of a robust power-law scaling confirms that the performance de-grades in a predictable and graceful manner. In con-trast, for the “Off Ridge (Local)” regime, the degra-dation was negligible across all bit depths, consistent with the flatter energy landscape in this region (see Ap-pendix C for the detailed plot). We conjecture that this deviation from the ideal exponent arises from finite-size effects and higher-order nonlinearities in the attractor geometry that are not captured by the simple quadratic approxima-tion. Furthermore, the highly skewed spectrum of the Fisher information matrix, as reported for deep networks [13], suggests that the parameter space is strongly anisotropic. In such a geometry, quantiza-tion noise may be preferentially absorbed along flat directions associated with small eigenvalues, result-ing in less severe degradation than predicted by the quadratic approximation. A more precise theoreti-cal characterization of this exponent is left for future work. 

5. Experimental Validation 

The theoretical analysis in the previous section pro-posed a dualistic nature of the KLR representation: a bimodal structure in the parameter space and a sparse influence in the input space. In this section, we pro-vide further experimental evidence to validate these theoretical claims. 

5.1 Preservation of Basin Depth under Quanti-zation 

Our theory suggests that the bimodal, digital-like na-ture of the weights should make the attractor land-scape robust to quantization. To test this, we eval-uated the noise robustness of the quantized network. Figure 5 compares the bit-wise recall accuracy of the full-precision (Float32) and the 2-bit quantized mod-els as a function of input noise. Remarkably, the 2-bit model’s performance closely tracks the baseline, maintaining high accuracy even under significant cor-ruption (e.g., >85% accuracy at 20% noise). This confirms that quantization does not destroy the deep attractor basins, a direct consequence of the system’s intrinsic digital nature proposed in Sec. 4.1.

6. Discussion 

Our comprehensive analysis, from empirical observa-tion to theoretical modeling and validation, reveals a sophisticated information processing strategy in KLR-trained networks. The present results are obtained near the Ridge of Optimization, which we view as a structurally relevant regime for high-capacity kernel models; nevertheless, compression behavior outside this regime remains an open question. 

6.1 The Duality of Representation 

The central theoretical finding of this paper is the dual nature of the memory representation. The Walsh anal-ysis (Sec. 4.2) demonstrated that the network learns a sparse input mapping, effectively performing feature selection. In stark contrast, the parameter representa-tion in the feature space is dense and holographic, as shown by its sensitivity to pruning (Sec. 3.3). This 

7duality suggests an optimal trade-off: the network identifies a small set of critical features for efficiency, but encodes them in a distributed, fault-tolerant man-ner for robustness. This principle may extend beyond associative memory to general-purpose neural com-putation. 

6.2 Spectral Bias and Memory Stability 

Recent studies by Karakida et al. [13, 14] have iden-tified a universal structural property of deep neural networks: their Fisher information matrices exhibit highly skewed spectra dominated by a small number of large eigenvalues. Although such spectral structure is sometimes described as “pathological,” our results suggest an alternative interpretation in the context of associative memory. We hypothesize that KLR learning near the Ridge accentuates this intrinsic spectral bias to an extreme degree. By exploiting the dominant spectral mode, the system can efficiently form deep and stable attractor basins, while the concurrent suppression of the bulk spectrum reduces inter-pattern interference. From this perspective, a spectral bias that is detrimental in other learning contexts instead serves as a key advantage for memory storage. This functional property may also reflect a physiological principle underlying ro-bust, high-capacity memory. This interpretation mo-tivates the biological perspective discussed in Sec. 6.4. 

6.3 The Ridge as a Critical “Sweet Spot” 

Our scaling analysis (Sec. 4.4) and distance-to-ridge analysis (Sec. 5.3) provide a more nuanced picture of the Ridge of Optimization. It is not simply the point of maximum stability, but a critical operating point balancing multiple trade-offs. The power-law scaling confirms that the landscape is geometrically smooth, allowing for graceful degradation. However, its close proximity to the capacity cliff reveals that the system self-organizes to the brink of instability to maximize its computational capability, a characteristic feature of Self-Organized Criticality [15]. This positions the Ridge as an optimal “sweet spot” that maximizes per-formance while maintaining a safe margin from catas-trophic failure. Practically, this critical state can be identified by monitoring geometric metrics like Pin-nacle Sharpness [3], or more simply, by observing the emergence of a bimodal weight distribution during training. 

6.4 Revisiting Biological Plausibility 

The holographic and low-precision nature of the KLR representation resonates strongly with principles of neural coding in the brain. Biological systems must operate robustly with noisy, low-precision synaptic components [16]; for instance, hippocampal synapses are estimated to have a precision of only a few bits [10]. Our results show that the combination of a dense, dis-tributed code (holographic) and a low-precision, dig-ital format (2-bit quantization) provides a powerful solution. While the KLR learning algorithm itself is not biologically plausible, the resulting geometric structure, namely a bimodal, fault-tolerant weight dis-tribution, may represent a convergent solution that both artificial and biological learning systems have discovered. 

6.5 Practical Implications for Hardware 

The collective findings of this study provide aclear blueprint for hardware-efficient implementation. Specifically, the combination of extreme quantiza-tion robustness and a dense holographic representa-tion allows for significant optimization. The robust-ness to 2-bit quantization allows for a 16 times reduc-tion in memory footprint compared to standard 32-bit floating-point storage. For example, a network with 

𝑁 = 1000 and 𝑃 = 3000 would normally require ≈

12 MB of memory, but with 2-bit quantization, this shrinks to a mere 0.75 MB, enabling the entire model to fit within the on-chip SRAM of low-power edge devices. Furthermore, the retrieval computation can be sig-nificantly accelerated. By quantizing weights to 1-bit or 2-bit, the expensive floating-point multiply-accumulate operations can be replaced by bitwise XNOR and population count operations. Such binary operations are theoretically 58 times faster on CPUs and highly efficient on FPGAs [5]. While sparsity is often sought for acceleration, our results suggest that for high-capacity associative memory, low-precision dense computation is the superior strategy, offering both capacity retention and massive speedups. Beyond speed and memory, quantization offers sig-nificant energy savings. A 32-bit floating-point multi-plication consumes approximately 3.7 pJ (picojoules) of energy on a 45 nm process, whereas a binary XNOR operation consumes less than 0.1 pJ [17]. For large-scale associative memory with millions of parame-ters, this translates to orders of magnitude reduction in power consumption, crucial for battery-operated edge AI. 

6.6 Limitations and Future Work 

The present study focuses primarily on the recall of random binary patterns. How the structural correla-tions present in real-world data (e.g., images or lan-

8guage) might alter the “duality” principle proposed here remains a subject for future investigation. Fur-thermore, our analysis is limited to post-training quan-tization; exploring the potential of quantization-aware training within this geometric framework is another important direction. 

7. Conclusion 

In this work, we have provided a comprehensive geo-metric theory for the remarkable compression robust-ness of high-capacity KLR-trained Hopfield networks. Our analysis revealed that the network’s contrasting re-sponses to quantization and sparsity originate from a dualistic representation: a dense, bimodal structure in the parameter space and a sparse functional mapping in the input space. Theoretically, we explained the emergence of this structure through a spontaneous symmetry breaking mechanism. Empirically, we established the robust-ness of this geometry through a quantitative scal-ing analysis, which revealed a predictable power-law degradation. Our findings characterize the “Ridge of Optimization” not merely as a high-performance re-gion, but as a critical “sweet spot”: a geometrically stable state that self-organizes to the brink of the ca-pacity limit. This principle of “sparse function, dense representation” at the edge of stability may offer a new perspective on achieving robustness and efficiency in both artificial and biological neural systems. Finally, our findings overturn the assumption that kernel methods are computationally too expensive for edge deployment. By leveraging low-precision quan-tization, massive speedups and memory reduction are achievable without sacrificing storage capacity. This work establishes KLR-trained Hopfield networks as a prime candidate for next-generation neuromorphic hardware, bridging the gap between high-dimensional theoretical models and their practical, efficient imple-mentation.
