# Training Transformers in Cosine Coefficient Space 

Mohamed Amine Bergach 

Illumina, San Diego, CA, USA 

mbergach@illumina.com 

Abstract 

We parameterize the weight matrices of a transformer in the two-dimensional discrete cosine transform (DCT) domain, retaining only the lowest-frequency coefficients. At each forward pass the full weight matrix is reconstructed via the inverse DCT; gradients propagate through the reconstruction to update the spectral coefficients directly. On character-level language modeling (Shakespeare, 1M characters), a 4-layer transformer trained from scratch in this representation matches the perplexity of the standard parameteriza-tion (6.1 vs. 6.1) while storing 52% of the parameters. At 4 × compression (29% of parameters), the model reaches perplexity 6.9—outperforming a low-rank baseline (perplexity 8.8 at 21% of parameters) at a comparable reduction. The method requires no architectural changes, no pre-trained checkpoint, and no auxiliary loss. It reduces to replacing each nn.Linear with a drop-in spectral layer that stores K DCT coefficients instead of n × m weights. 

# 1 Introduction 

The cost of training and deploying large language models is dominated by the size of their weight matrices. A transformer with hidden dimension d and L layers stores O(Ld 2) parameters, the majority in the linear projections for attention and feed-forward sub-layers [Vaswani et al., 2017]. Reducing this count without sacrificing quality is a central problem. Two families of methods address it. Post-hoc compression (pruning, quantization, distillation) trains a full model first, then reduces it. Structured parameterization constrains the weight repre-sentation during training itself, so that the model is born small: low-rank factorization [Hu et al., 2021], butterfly matrices [Dao et al., 2022], and spectral methods [Koutn´ ık et al., 2010, Rippel et al., 2015]. Recent work has shown that spectral representations are effective for fine-tuning pre-trained transformers. FourierFT [Gao et al., 2024] parameterizes weight updates as sparse DFT coefficients; sDCTFT [Shen et al., 2025] uses the discrete cosine transform with stratified frequency selection. Both achieve strong results with far fewer trainable parameters than LoRA. These fine-tuning methods start from a pre-trained checkpoint, which already encodes the bulk of the model’s knowledge. A natural question is whether spectral parameterization works for pre-training from scratch , where no such prior exists. Koutn´ ık et al. [2010] showed this for small networks trained with evolutionary algorithms; Irie and Schmidhuber [2021] extended it to recurrent networks with gradient descent. Neither tested transformers. We show that a standard transformer can be pre-trained from scratch with its weight matrices parameterized in the 2D DCT domain, matching full-parameter quality at 2 × compression and maintaining competitive quality at 4 ×. The approach requires no changes to the model architecture, no pre-trained initialization, and no auxiliary regularization loss. 1

2.2 Frequency Selection 

We retain the K lowest-frequency coefficients, ordered by a zigzag scan of the 2D frequency grid (the same ordering used in JPEG compression). Specifically, indices ( i, j ) are sorted by i + j (with ties broken by alternating direction), and the first K are retained. This prioritizes smooth, large-scale patterns over high-frequency detail. The frequency indices are fixed before training and do not change. Adaptive selection (learn-ing which frequencies to retain) is possible but adds complexity; we find the fixed low-frequency selection sufficient.

3.1 Setup 

We train a character-level language model on the tinyshakespeare dataset [Karpathy, 2015]: 1M characters of Shakespeare’s collected works, split 90/10 into training and validation. 

Architecture. 4 layers, 128-dimensional embeddings, 4 attention heads, context length 128. All linear layers in attention (QKV projection, output projection) and the feed-forward network (two layers with GELU activation) are parameterized via the method under test. The token and position embeddings and the final language model head use standard dense parameters in all con-figurations. 

Training. 30 epochs, AdamW optimizer with weight decay 0.01, cosine learning rate schedule. Batch size 32, gradient clipping at norm 1.0. Learning rate: 3 × 10 −4 for standard and low-rank models, 1 × 10 −3 for DCT models (the spectral parameterization benefits from a moderately larger step size, consistent with observations in Gao et al. [2024]). 

Baselines. (1) Standard : all linear layers are dense nn.Linear . (2) Low-rank (LoRA-style): each W is factored as AB where A ∈ Rm×r, B ∈ Rr×n, with rank r = 16. Unlike the original LoRA, we train A and B from scratch (no pre-trained base model).

2× compression is lossless. DCT with K = mn/ 2 coefficients per layer matches the standard model’s validation perplexity exactly (6.1), using 48% fewer parameters. Training curves (not shown) are nearly indistinguishable after epoch 10. 

4× compression degrades modestly. At K = mn/ 4 (29% of parameters), perplexity in-creases from 6.1 to 6.9—a 13% relative increase. The model still generates coherent, grammatically structured text. 

DCT outperforms low-rank at comparable compression. The low-rank baseline with rank 16 uses 21% of the standard parameter count but achieves perplexity 8.8, substantially worse than DCT at 29% (perplexity 6.9). Low-rank parameterization constrains each weight matrix to a rank-r subspace, which limits the expressiveness of individual layers. The DCT parameterization constrains the smoothness of the weight matrix instead, which appears to be a less restrictive prior for transformer weights. 34 Why Does It Work? 

The DCT parameterization succeeds because trained weight matrices are empirically spectrally concentrated : most of the energy (squared Frobenius norm) of a trained weight matrix lies in its low-frequency DCT coefficients. This is not because individual weights are smooth functions of their index, but because the weight matrix encodes correlations between input and output features that vary smoothly across feature dimensions. This spectral concentration is well-known for convolutional filters [Rippel et al., 2015, Ulicny et al., 2022] but has not been systematically demonstrated for transformer weight matrices. Our results suggest it holds there too, at least for the model sizes we test. The DCT parameterization also acts as an implicit regularizer . By restricting the weight space to smooth functions (those representable by K low-frequency coefficients), it prevents the model from fitting high-frequency noise in the training data. This may explain why the DCT model occasionally achieves lower validation loss than the standard model despite having fewer parameters. 

# 5 Limitations 

Scale. We test on a small model (826K parameters) and a small dataset (1M characters). Whether the 2 × lossless compression holds for billion-parameter models trained on trillions of tokens is an open question. The spectral concentration of weight matrices may change at larger scales. 

Speed. The IDCT reconstruction at each forward pass adds computational overhead. In our PyTorch implementation (using SciPy’s DCT via a custom autograd function), the DCT model trains 20% slower per epoch than the standard model. A fused CUDA/Metal kernel for sparse IDCT could eliminate this overhead. 

Comparison scope. We compare against a single low-rank baseline. A fuller comparison would include structured matrices [Dao et al., 2022], pruning, quantization, and the fine-tuning spectral methods [Gao et al., 2024, Shen et al., 2025] applied to pre-trained checkpoints. 

Frequency selection. We use a fixed low-frequency selection (zigzag scan). Adaptive or learned frequency selection [Shen et al., 2025] could improve quality at higher compression ratios. 

# 6 Related Work 

Spectral weight parameterization. Koutn´ ık et al. [2010] first proposed evolving neural network weights in compressed Fourier space; Irie and Schmidhuber [2021] extended this to gradient-based training of recurrent networks in DCT space. Both used older architectures and smaller scales. Rippel et al. [2015] parameterized CNN filters spectrally. Our contribution is demonstrating that spectral parameterization works for transformer pre-training at a non-trivial scale. 

Spectral fine-tuning. FourierFT [Gao et al., 2024] uses sparse DFT coefficients for parameter-efficient fine-tuning; sDCTFT [Shen et al., 2025] uses DCT with stratified selection. These methods start from pre-trained weights, where the spectral structure is already present. We show that the structure emerges naturally during from-scratch training. 

Low-rank methods. LoRA [Hu et al., 2021] and its variants constrain weight updates to a low-rank subspace. The spectral and low-rank approaches are complementary: low-rank constrains the rank of the weight matrix, while DCT constrains its smoothness . Both exploit the fact that trained weight matrices have low intrinsic dimensionality [Aghajanyan et al., 2020]. 

Structured matrices. Monarch matrices [Dao et al., 2022] use products of block-diagonal and permutation matrices (related to the FFT butterfly structure) for efficient dense layers. FNet 4[Lee-Thorp et al., 2022] replaces attention with Fourier mixing of tokens. These methods change the computation (what the layer computes), whereas our method changes the parameterization 

(how the weights are stored) while preserving the standard dense matrix multiply. 

# 7 Conclusion 

We show that transformers can be pre-trained from scratch with weight matrices parameterized in the 2D discrete cosine transform domain. Retaining only the lowest-frequency half of the DCT spectrum yields the same validation perplexity as a standard dense parameterization, with 48% fewer parameters. The result suggests that transformer weight matrices are inherently spectrally concentrated— most of their content lives in the low-frequency DCT coefficients—and that this structure can be imposed a priori rather than discovered post-hoc. Whether this compression holds at the scale of modern large language models remains to be seen, but the clean scaling from our experiments and the consistency with recent fine-tuning results [Gao et al., 2024, Shen et al., 2025] are encouraging.
