# Abstract 

We present PolarQuant , a post-training weight quanti-zation method for large language models (LLMs) that ex-ploits the distributional structure of neural network weights to achieve near-lossless compression. PolarQuant operates in three stages: (1) block-wise normalization to the unit hypersphere, (2) Walsh–Hadamard rotation to transform coordinates into approximately Gaussian random variables, and (3) quantization with centroids matched to the Gaus-sian distribution. Our ablation reveals that Hadamard rota-tion alone accounts for 98% of the quality improvement ,reducing Qwen3.5-9B perplexity from 6.90 (absmax Q5) to 6.40 ( ∆ = +0 .03 from FP16), making it practically lossless without any calibration data . Furthermore, Po-larQuant functions as an effective preprocessing step for downstream INT4 quantizers: PolarQuant Q5 dequantized and re-quantized by torchao INT4 achieves perplexity 6.56 versus 6.68 for direct absmax INT4, while maintaining 43.1 tok/s throughput at 6.5 GB VRAM. Code and models are publicly available. 

# 1 Introduction 

The deployment of large language models on consumer and edge hardware demands aggressive weight compression. A 9-billion parameter model in FP16 requires approximately 18 GB of GPU memory, exceeding the capacity of most consumer GPUs. Quantization to 4 bits reduces this to roughly 5–6 GB, enabling inference on devices ranging from NVIDIA RTX desktop GPUs to Apple Silicon lap-tops. The simplest and most widely deployed quantization scheme is absmax (absolute maximum) quantization [ 10 ], which linearly maps values in [−α, α ] to integer codes, where α = max |wi|. While computationally trivial, abs-max is provably suboptimal when the weight distribution is non-uniform: it wastes precious codebook entries on rarely occurring outlier magnitudes, and concentrates quantiza-tion errors in the high-density central region. More sophisticated approaches, such as GPTQ [ 8] and AWQ [ 11 ], mitigate this problem through calibration-based Hessian corrections or activation-aware scaling, respec-tively. NormalFloat (NF4) [ 7] takes a distributional ap-proach, designing codebooks optimal for normally dis-tributed weights. QuIP# [ 6] applies random incoherence processing to improve quantization bounds. Recently, Tur-boQuant [ 2] demonstrated that normalizing and rotating KV cache vectors via Hadamard transforms yields Gaus-sian coordinates amenable to optimal quantization. In this work, we adapt and extend the polar quantiza-tion idea from KV cache compression to weight compres-sion. Our key insight is that after block-wise normaliza-tion and Hadamard rotation, LLM weight blocks are well-approximated by i.i.d. standard normal random variables, for which the MSE-optimal quantizer is analytically known via the Lloyd–Max algorithm [ 13 , 14 ]. This yields a quan-tization scheme that is: 1. Near-lossless. Hadamard rotation alone reduces quan-tization error by 98%, bringing Q5 perplexity within 

+0 .03 of FP16 (6.40 vs 6.37) without any calibration data. 2. Simple. The core algorithm is a single matrix multipli-cation ( H128 ˆbi) per block, with no gradient computa-tion, no iterative optimization, and no calibration data. The Hadamard matrix is deterministic and self-inverse. 3. Composable. PolarQuant serves as a preprocessing step compatible with any downstream quantizer. We demonstrate that PolarQuant Q5 dequantized weights, when re-quantized by torchao INT4 [ 16 ], yield 0.12 points lower perplexity than direct absmax INT4 at identical speed and memory. 4. Efficient. The Hadamard matrix is its own inverse (HH ⊤ = I), requiring no additional storage. Dequan-tization adds approximately 8 seconds at model load time, with zero runtime overhead. We evaluate PolarQuant on Qwen3.5-9B [ 17 ] and demonstrate state-of-the-art perplexity–compression trade-offs. Combined with AWQ, PolarQuant achieves a per-plexity of 6.43 on WikiText-2 [ 15 ], just +0 .06 above FP16, 1

## 2.2 GPTQ 

GPTQ [ 8] performs layer-wise quantization using approxi-mate second-order (Hessian) information, iteratively quan-tizing columns and compensating the error in remaining columns via the optimal brain surgeon framework. GPTQ achieves strong results but requires calibration data and is computationally expensive for large models. 

## 2.3 AWQ: Activation-Aware Weight Quanti-zation 

AWQ [ 11 ] observes that a small fraction of weight channels disproportionately affect output quality because they cor-respond to large activation magnitudes. AWQ computes per-channel scaling factors from calibration activations, protecting important channels from quantization error. We show that AWQ complements PolarQuant: AWQ operates on channels while PolarQuant operates on blocks , and their combination yields near-lossless compression. 

## 2.4 NormalFloat (NF4) 

Dettmers et al. [ 7] introduced NormalFloat, a data type with quantization levels uniformly spaced in the quan-tile domain of the standard normal distribution. NF4 is information-theoretically optimal when each quantization bin has equal probability. PolarQuant differs in two key ways: (1) we use Lloyd–Max centroids that minimize MSE rather than maximize entropy, and (2) we explicitly trans-form weights to Gaussian via Hadamard rotation rather than assuming Gaussianity a priori. 

## 2.5 QuIP and QuIP# 

QuIP [ 5] introduced incoherence processing for weight quantization, showing that random orthogonal rotations improve quantization bounds. QuIP# [ 6] extended this with randomized Hadamard transforms (RHT) and lattice code-books for 2-bit quantization. While PolarQuant shares the use of Hadamard transforms, our approach differs in two key ways: (1) QuIP# applies rotations to the entire weight matrix columns (inter-block), while PolarQuant applies rotations within each block of 128 elements (intra-block); and (2) QuIP# uses incoherence to bound worst-case er-ror, while PolarQuant uses normalization and rotation to transform the distribution to Gaussian. 

## 2.6 QuaRot and SpinQuant 

QuaRot [ 3] demonstrated that applying Hadamard rota-tions to hidden states, activations, and KV cache removes outliers without changing model output, enabling outlier-free 4-bit inference. SpinQuant [ 12 ] further showed that 

learned rotation matrices outperform fixed Hadamard rota-tions by up to 16 points on zero-shot tasks. Both methods apply rotations between layers (requiring graph surgery to absorb rotations into adjacent linear layers), while Polar-Quant applies rotations within blocks of each weight ten-sor independently, requiring no modification to the model graph and enabling use as a simple preprocessing step. 

## 2.7 TurboQuant 

TurboQuant [ 2] applies the polar quantization framework to KV cache compression during inference, normalizing KV vectors, applying random rotation, and quantizing the resulting near-Gaussian coordinates with optimal cen-troids. TurboQuant proves information-theoretic lower bounds and achieves near-optimal distortion rates. Our work adapts this approach from KV cache to weight com-pression, demonstrates its effectiveness as a preprocessing step for downstream INT4 quantizers, and provides the first ablation quantifying the individual contributions of rotation (98%) versus optimal centroids (2%) at Q5.

## 3.5 PolarQuant + AWQ 

AWQ [ 11 ] and PolarQuant operate on orthogonal axes: AWQ rescales channels to protect activation-sensitive weights, while PolarQuant normalizes and rotates blocks to enable optimal quantization. Their combination is straight-forward: 1. Compute AWQ per-channel scales s from calibration data. 2. Multiply weights by the scales: W′ = W · diag( s).3. Apply PolarQuant to W′.4. At dequantization, apply inverse AWQ: ˆW = ˆW′ ·

diag( s−1).This combination reduces the perplexity delta from 

+0 .52 (PolarQuant Q5 alone) to just +0 .06 (PolarQuant Q5 + AWQ), as shown in section 4.

When torchao subsequently re-quantizes this intermediate representation to INT4 using group-wise absmax, it starts from a better approximation of W. The group-wise absmax scale factors computed on ˆWBF16 are more representative of the true weight dis-tribution, because PolarQuant has already removed the outlier-induced distortion. This is not double quantization in the traditional sense (where both quantization steps are lossy and errors com-pound). Rather, PolarQuant acts as a denoising step: the Q5 quantization and dequantization effectively project the weights onto a representation that is more amenable to subsequent absmax INT4 quantization.

## 4.1 Setup 

Model. We evaluate on Qwen3.5-9B [ 17 ], a recent 9-billion parameter language model with a hybrid DeltaNet + MoE architecture, chosen for its strong baseline quality and architectural diversity. 

Hardware. Primary experiments are conducted on an NVIDIA RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell GPU with 96 GB VRAM. Cross-platform experiments use an Apple Mac mini M4 with 16 GB unified memory. 

Evaluation. We report perplexity on WikiText-2 [ 15 ]using a sliding window of 2048 tokens with stride 512, masking the first 1536 context tokens in each window. This methodology follows the standard established by prior quantization work [ 8, 11 ]. All perplexity numbers are deterministic and reproducible. 

Speed measurement. Throughput is measured as the average of 3 runs of 100 generated tokens, after a warmup run, in tokens per second (tok/s). 

Baselines. We compare against: (1) FP16, the full-precision reference; (2) torchao INT4 [ 16 ] with group-wise absmax quantization (group size 128); and (3) BitsAnd-Bytes NF4 [7], the NormalFloat 4-bit format.

# 5 Analysis 

## 5.1 Why PolarQuant Improves Downstream INT4 

The central finding of this work is that PolarQuant Q5 dequantized weights, when re-quantized by torchao INT4, yield better perplexity than direct absmax INT4. We offer the following explanation. Torchao uses group-wise absmax INT4 quantization with group size 128. For each group of 128 weights, it computes s = max |wj | and maps all values to 4-bit in-tegers using this scale. The quality of this quantization depends critically on the dynamic range of each group: groups with outliers have large s and poor resolution for the majority of values. As our ablation (section 4.4) reveals, the dominant mech-anism is Hadamard rotation, which accounts for 98% of the quality improvement. The rotation transforms the weight distribution from a heavy-tailed, non-uniform shape to an approximately Gaussian one. In the Gaussian regime, the dynamic range of each 128-element group is tightly con-centrated around √2 ln 128 ≈ 3.1 standard deviations, so the group-wise absmax scale factor closely tracks the true data range. Without rotation, outlier weights inflate the absmax scale, wasting resolution on the tails. The PolarQuant Q5 dequantized weights have under-gone this distributional normalization. When torchao sub-sequently re-quantizes to INT4, it encounters a weight distribution that is more homogeneous across groups, with fewer outliers and more consistent dynamic range. The result is that each INT4 group achieves better utilization of its 16 available levels. In essence, PolarQuant acts as a distributional regular-izer : Hadamard rotation homogenizes the weight distribu-tion, producing groups that are more amenable to uniform (absmax) quantization. 

## 5.2 The Double Quantization Paradox 

One might expect that applying PolarQuant at a lower bit width (e.g., Q3) before torchao INT4 would yield even better results, since the distributional regularization would be stronger. Surprisingly, the opposite is true: • Mixed-bit PolarQuant (Q3 for gate/up projections) + torchao INT4: PPL 7.25 ( worse )• Uniform PolarQuant Q5 + torchao INT4: PPL 6.56 ( bet-ter )The explanation is that Q3 dequantization is too lossy :with only 8 centroids, the dequantized weights lose critical fine-grained information that torchao’s INT4 quantizer cannot recover. The optimal operating point requires that 6the PolarQuant preprocessing preserves enough signal for the downstream quantizer to exploit. At Q5 (32 centroids), the dequantized weights retain sufficient information for torchao to produce a high-quality INT4 representation. This establishes a principle for cascaded quantization: 

the preprocessing quantizer must operate at a sufficiently high bit width to preserve the information needed by the downstream quantizer. In our setting, Q5 is the empirically optimal intermediate bit width for a Q5 →INT4 cascade. 

## 5.3 Computational Overhead 

PolarQuant dequantization adds approximately 8 seconds to model load time on the RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell. This is a one-time cost. At inference time, the compu-tational path is identical to standard FP16 or INT4 infer-ence, with zero additional overhead. The dominant cost during dequantization is the batched matrix multiplication 

H128 ˆbi for each block; our implementation uses PyTorch’s 

torch.matmul , which achieves 25 × faster execution than a naive fast Walsh–Hadamard transform (FWHT) im-plementation by leveraging optimized cuBLAS GEMM kernels. 

# 6 Conclusion 

We have presented PolarQuant, a simple and effective weight quantization method that achieves near-lossless compression by exploiting the Gaussian structure of Hadamard-rotated weight blocks. Our ablation reveals that the Hadamard rotation is the essential component, ac-counting for 98% of the quality improvement at Q5. Our key contributions are: 1. Near-lossless compression. PolarQuant Q5 achieves perplexity 6.39 on Qwen3.5-9B ( ∆ = +0 .02 from FP16) without any calibration data, requiring only a single Hadamard matrix multiplication per block. 2. Rotation is the key. Our ablation reveals that Hadamard rotation alone accounts for 98% of the qual-ity improvement (PPL 6.90 → 6.40), while Lloyd–Max centroids provide only marginal additional benefit at Q5. This finding simplifies the method to its essential component: a deterministic orthogonal rotation. 3. Improved INT4 inference. When used as a prepro-cessing step, PolarQuant Q5 + torchao INT4 achieves perplexity 6.56 versus 6.68 for direct absmax INT4, at identical throughput (43.1 tok/s) and near-identical memory (6.5 GB). 4. Cross-platform deployment. PolarQuant runs on both NVIDIA GPUs and Apple Silicon, achieving 19.7 tok/s on a Mac mini M4 at 4.8 GB. PolarQuant is compatible with any downstream quan-tizer (torchao, GGUF, MLX) and requires no calibration data for its core algorithm (AWQ is the only component that uses calibration). The method adds no runtime over-head and only a few seconds of one-time dequantization cost at model load. 

Limitations and future work. PolarQuant assumes that Hadamard-rotated weight blocks are well-approximated by i.i.d. Gaussians, which may not hold for all architectures. The current implementation does not exploit inter-block correlations. Future work could extend PolarQuant to activation quantization, explore vector quantization with Gaussian codebooks, and investigate the theoretical limits of cascaded quantization pipelines. # B Mixed-Bit Allocation Strategy 

Inspired by Unsloth Dynamic 2.0, we investigated a mixed-bit strategy that assigns different bit widths to different tensor types based on quantization sensitivity: Table 6: Mixed-bit allocation by tensor type. 

Tensor Type Bits Rationale 

MLP gate/up projections 3 Most robust to quantization MLP down projection 4 Slightly more sensitive Attention Q/K/V 5 Moderate sensitivity Attention O/output proj 6 High sensitivity Embedding 5 Safe LM head 6 Nearly lossless Norms, biases, routing FP16 Must stay full precision 

This achieves a ∼3.7-bit average with quality comparable to uniform Q5 at smaller storage. However, as noted in section 5.2, using this mixed-bit scheme as preprocessing for torchao INT4 degrades quality (PPL 7.25) because the Q3 layers lose too much information. The mixed-bit strategy is therefore recommended only for direct PolarQuant inference (without downstream re-quantization).
