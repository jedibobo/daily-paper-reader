Abstract 

Numerous tensor accelerator designs have been proposed in academia and industry, yet most lack well-documented ISAs and compiler backends. As a consequence, the majority of new designs are only evaluated on a handful of operators or synthetic kernels. Recent work (e.g., TAIDL and the ACT ecosystem) has shown that given a tensor-level ISA specification, complete software stacks including test oracles and compiler backends can be automatically generated. However, writing such specifications remains a manual, expert-driven process. We present ATLAAS, the first end-to-end MLIR-based pipeline that lifts RTL-extracted accelerator semantics to TAIDL-like tensor ISA specifications. Building on prior architecture-level model ex-traction that produces bit-level LLVM IR, ATLAAS introduces an 8-pass MLIR semantic lifting pipeline that progressively recovers high-level tensor structure, including MAC idioms, saturation se-mantics, multi-dimensional buffer organizations, and data layout transformations. The pipeline emits specifications in the TAIDL for-malism, immediately enabling automatic software stack generation through the ACT ecosystem. We evaluate ATLAAS on Gemmini, a systolic-array accelerator, extracting all hardware instructions semantics across 127 MLIR files. The lifting pipeline collapses the extracted bit-level MLIR by up to 92.9% on processing elements (686 → 49 lines, of which only 17 encode the tensor computation core) and by 24–34% on con-troller modules, with the residue being irreducible control logic (FSMs, address computation, and instruction muxing). ATLAAS discovers hardware features omitted from the hand-written ref-erence, including multi-bank DMA configuration, pooling engine semantics, and im2col hardware support. Correctness is validated through Z3 SMT equivalence proofs. We further confirm gener-ality on TVM’s VTA tensor processor, where the same pipeline lifts all four datapath modules without accelerator-specific changes, with extracted semantics formally verified against the underlying RTL. By feeding the extracted specification into the ACT compiler framework, ATLAAS enables an automated path from RTL to a performance-competitive compiler backend, eliminating the need to manually write tensor-level ISA semantics.

1 Introduction 

The rapid growth of deep learning workloads has driven a prolifera-tion of specialized tensor accelerators in both academia and industry. Designs such as Gemmini [ 13 ], MAERI [ 24 ], SIGMA [ 38 ], Eyeriss [ 6 ], NVDLA [ 8 ], and FEATHER [ 43 ] explore diverse microarchitectural innovations—systolic arrays, reconfigurable interconnects, flexible dataflow engines—to deliver orders-of-magnitude improvements in performance and energy efficiency over general-purpose pro-cessors. Meanwhile, a small number of platforms backed by major vendors, notably Google’s TPU [ 23 ] with XLA [ 39 ], Intel AMX [ 19 ]with oneDNN [ 35 ], and NVIDIA GPUs with CUDA/cuDNN [ 7, 32 ], enjoy mature compiler toolchains that connect them seamlessly to frameworks like PyTorch [ 36 ], JAX [ 4 ], and TensorFlow [ 1]. The contrast is stark: as Table 1 summarizes, accelerators with mature, end-to-end compiler support number fewer than five, while over a dozen open-source designs lack any usable compiler backend. These accelerators can only be exercised through hand-written kernel libraries that cover a narrow set of operators and miss cross-layer fusion opportunities, leaving the vast majority of proposed hardware unable to run real ML models end-to-end. The root cause of this software deficit lies upstream of the com-piler: the ISA semantics of most accelerators have never been for-mally defined. In the CPU world, the ISA serves as the foundational contract between hardware and software; for accelerators, how-ever, ISA information is typically scattered across RTL source code, informal documentation, or exists only in the minds of hardware de-signers [ 17 , 18 ]. Figure 1 summarizes this landscape: the top-down ACT ecosystem assumes a hand-written TAIDL specification, while the bottom-up ILA framework produces only scalar-level models, leaving no automated path from RTL to tensor-level semantics. Recent work has made significant strides in closing this gap from ISA specification downward. TAIDL [ 21 ] introduced a domain-specific language for defining tensor accelerator ISAs, and ACT [ 22 ]showed that compiler backends can be automatically generated from such specifications (§2.2). However, this pipeline has a critical prerequisite: someone must first write the TAIDL specification by hand—a process that requires deep understanding of the acceler-ator’s architectural state, instruction encoding, and tensor-level semantics. This manual specification bottleneck is well-documented: writ-ing formal accelerator models has been characterized as “tedious and error-prone” [ 50 ] and as “a significant burden for accelerators which may not have the volume or life-span compared to proces-sors” [ 49 ]. On the hardware side, RTL designs iterate rapidly; on the software side, TAIDL and ACT automate everything down-stream of the ISA spec but cannot function without one. Prior efforts such as ILA [17] and 3LA [16] formalized accelerator inter-faces but still require hand-authored models, and LLM-based EDA agents [ 27 , 37 , 47 , 53 ] that depend on machine-readable hardware semantics [ 15 , 33 , 48 ] cannot operate when such semantics do not exist. In this paper, we present ATLAAS, the first end-to-end MLIR-based pipeline that lifts RTL-extracted accelerator semantics to TAIDL-like tensor ISA specifications. Building on prior architecture-level model extraction [ 49 , 50 ] that produces bit-level MLIR from flattened RTL, ATLAAS employs an 8-pass MLIR semantic lifting pipeline that progressively recovers high-level tensor structure— from bit-manipulation canonicalization through idiom detection and loop reconstruction to structured metadata emission—bridging the abstraction gap between gate-level hardware and the tensor-level semantics required by tools like TAIDL and ACT (Figure 2). We evaluate ATLAAS on two open-source accelerators: Berke-ley’s Gemmini (systolic array, 16 ×16, INT8) and TVM’s VTA. The lifting pipeline achieves up to 92.9% reduction on compute-dominated modules, discovers hardware features omitted from hand-written references, and generalizes across both accelerators without mod-ification. All lifted semantics are formally verified equivalent to the RTL via Z3 [ 9] SMT proofs. Fed into ACT, the extracted specifi-cation yields a compiler backend that achieves a geometric mean speedup of 1.014 × over hand-written Gemmini kernels on work-loads including ResNet-50 and MobileNet. By filling the frontmost gap in the RTL-to-framework pipeline, ATLAAS enables an automated path from RTL to ML framework integration, as illustrated in Figure 1. 2 Background 2.1 Hardware-Software Interfaces in Tensor Accelerators 

Tensor accelerators occupy a fundamentally different point in the design space than scalar or vector processors. Rather than exe-cuting fine-grained instruction streams over flat register files and cache-coherent memory, they expose coarse-grained operations that consume and produce multi-dimensional tensors and replace implicit caches with explicitly managed on-chip memories (scratch-pads, accumulator banks). Gemmini [ 13 ], Berkeley’s open-source systolic-array generator, is representative: it attaches to a Rocket core [ 3] via the Rocket Custom Coprocessor (RoCC) interface [ 2]with non-standard RISC-V custom instructions, and its programmer-visible state consists of a row-addressed scratchpad and an accumu-lator. Its ISA exposes only configuration, DMA data-movement, and matrix-multiply execution instructions—every operation simulta-neously specifies what to compute, where operands live in a private memory hierarchy, and how data should be staged. This distinctiveness places heavy demands on compiler infras-tructure. ML frameworks feed tensor IR (StableHLO, Linalg, TOSA) into backends such as XLA [ 39 ], TVM [ 5 ], or IREE [ 42 ], which must lower high-level operations to the accelerator’s native instruc-tions. Doing so correctly requires tiling and fusing loop nests to fit scratchpad capacity, scheduling explicit DMA transfers, allocat-ing scratchpad regions, and orchestrating double-buffering—all of which presuppose a precise model of what each instruction does to which piece of tensor state. Consider lowering a single XLA dot 𝐶 =𝐴 ·𝐵 for matrices exceed-ing Gemmini’s 16 ×16 tile. The backend must emit a tiled sequence: 

config_ex /config_ld /config_st to set dataflow and stride pa-rameters; mvin /mvin2 to stage tiles of 𝐴 and 𝐵 into the scratchpad; 

preload followed by compute_preloaded (or compute_accumulated )to fire the systolic array for each output tile; and mvout to drain results. Generating this sequence requires the backend to know, for every instruction, which tensor tile it touches, which scratchpad region it occupies, and how its output composes with subsequent operations. This is precisely where most accelerators fall short. Mapping ten-sor IR to an instruction sequence presupposes a machine-readable 

ISA that specifies both the binary encoding and the tensor-level se-mantics of each instruction. For Gemmini, semantics live informally in prose documentation, the Chisel RTL, and a hand-written Spike functional model; most open-source accelerators offer only RTL and perhaps a driver. Building a backend today therefore remains largely an exercise in manual reverse-engineering of the RTL .

2.2 Automated Compiler Generation and the Specification Bottleneck 

Recent work has shown that the main barrier to end-to-end software support for tensor accelerators is not only backend engineering effort, but the lack of a precise, machine-readable Instruction Set Architecture (ISA) specification. TAIDL [ 21 ] (Tensor Accelerator ISA Definition Language) addresses this problem by providing a domain-specific language for describing accelerator data models and instruction semantics at the tensor level. A key design choice in TAIDL is that instruction behavior is expressed using tensor op-erators close to XLA High Level Optimizer (XLA-HLO) [ 39 ], such as reshape , transpose , convert , dot , and add . This abstraction level is high enough to capture programmer-visible tensor seman-tics compactly, while remaining close enough to tensor compiler intermediate representations to support downstream automation. A representative TAIDL specification therefore looks less like mi-croarchitectural pseudocode and more like a tensor transformation. In published TAIDL examples [ 20 ], even more complex instructions such as Intel Ad-vanced Matrix Extensions (Intel AMX) [ 19 ] tdpbusd are modeled by composing layout transformations ( reshape , transpose ), type conversion, tensor contraction, accumulation, and write-back in the same style. Built on top of such specifications, ACT [ 22 ] (Accelerator Com-piler Toolkit) automates what comes after the ISA description. Given a TAIDL specification, ACT automatically generates compiler back-ends by combining equality saturation for instruction selection with constraint-programming-based memory allocation. The result is an ISA-driven compilation flow in which much of the backend logic is synthesized rather than handwritten. 2.3 RTL Extraction and MLIR-Based Semantic Lifting 

A natural baseline for recovering ISA semantics from hardware is architecture-level model extraction from RTL. The relevant starting point is the autoGenILA line of work [ 49 , 50 ], which first identifies the architectural state variables (ASVs) that persist across instruc-tions and then derives the corresponding state update functions for each instruction directly from RTL. This removes the need to manually write an Instruction-Level Abstraction (ILA) [17] model and yields an architecture-level interface that is consistent with the hardware implementation by construction. In ATLAAS’s workflow, autoGenILA serves exactly this role: it is the extraction backend that converts accelerator RTL into per-instruction semantic functions, providing the raw material for later lifting. The problem is that these extracted models are still too low-level for tensor compiler generation. autoGenILA captures hardware behavior faithfully, but the output remains a flattened, bit-level scalar model in which high-level tensor intent is obscured by the structure of the datapath [ 49 , 50 ]. As illustrated in Figure 2, a single Gemmini processing element may initially appear as hundreds of primitive operations—dominated by sign-extension chains, trun-cations, bitwise composition, and scalar arithmetic—before later passes recover the underlying dot + add + clamp semantics. Such an output is appropriate for bit-accurate reasoning, but it is not yet suitable for TAIDL [ 21 ], which requires tensor-visible seman-tics: buffer reads and writes, structured layout transformations, multiply-accumulate patterns, reductions, and saturation behavior. In other words, autoGenILA solves the extraction problem, but not the semantic abstraction problem. This is where MLIR (Multi-Level Intermediate Representation) becomes the key enabling substrate. The advantage of MLIR is not any hardware-specific frontend, but its support for multi-level, progressive abstraction within one compiler framework [ 26 ]. In ATLAAS’s implementation, the extracted semantics are material-ized as low-level MLIR in the arith and memref dialects, and are progressively raised through structured passes that leverage MLIR’s dialect-aware rewriting infrastructure [26, 30]. More importantly, MLIR provides the right semantic stepping stones between bit-level arithmetic and tensor-level intent: arith 

for scalar operations, memref for explicit memory accesses, scf for structured control flow, and linalg for tensor computations [ 28 –30]. Because MLIR supports rewrite-driven and partially legalized transformations, ATLAAS can progressively raise the representa-tion rather than jumping from RTL-level behavior to tensor seman-tics in one step (Section 3). 

3 ATLAAS Pipeline 

ATLAAS transforms an unannotated RTL design into a TAIDL spec-ification through three stages (Figures 1 and 3): Stage 1 (§3.1) sym-bolically extracts per-instruction behavior from the RTL into bit-level MLIR; Stage 2 (§3.2) progressively lifts that bit-level represen-tation to tensor-level semantics via an eight-pass rewrite pipeline; 

Stage 3 (§3.3) assembles the lifted MLIR into a TAIDL specification with XLA-HLO-style semantics. The entire pipeline takes a direc-tory of SystemVerilog files and a top-module name as input, and produces a complete TAIDL specification consumable by the ACT ecosystem. 

3.1 Stage 1: RTL-to-MLIR Extraction 

Stage 1 builds on autoGenILA [ 49 , 50 ], which symbolically unrolls a Verilog design for each (instruction, architectural state variable) pair, producing a function that maps current state and inputs to next state—provably bit-equivalent to the RTL by construction. However, autoGenILA’s LLVM IR backend destroys two structural properties that tensor-level reasoning requires. First, conditional updates are lowered into branches and phi nodes, erasing the if/else structure that encodes hardware mode selection. Second, RTL signal names— which identify whether an input carries activations, weights, or an accumulator—are discarded during lowering. ATLAAS replaces the LLVM IR backend with an MLIR code generator that preserves both. Conditional updates become scf.if 

regions; memory accesses remain as indexed memref loads; and original RTL signal names are attached as structured metadata that downstream passes consult to classify arguments semantically. A second design choice packs the time series of each input signal across unroll cycles into a single memref argument rather than flattening them into hundreds of independent scalars—this grouping is what later enables loop reconstruction (§3.2) to recover MAC chains as indexable reductions. Two properties make the pipeline robust. An instruction that fails to match a template simply receives no annotation, and Stage 3 falls back to opaque semantics rather than producing incorrect TAIDL. Second, the pipeline is accelerator-agnostic: the structural cues it relies on— sign-extension chains, MAC fan-in, fixed-point clamp idioms, accu-mulator def–use chains—are properties of common RTL synthesis patterns, not of any particular ISA. The same binary lifts Gemmini’s PE and DMA controllers, and VTA’s TensorGemm and TensorAlu without modification. On the running Gemmini PE example, the eight passes reduce 686 lines of bit-level MLIR to 49, of which only 17 encode the computation core clamp(dot(%A,%B)+%C) .

3.3 Stage 3: TAIDL Assembly 

Stage 3 assembles the annotated MLIR from Stage 2 into a complete TAIDL specification (Listing 1). It first merges the per-(instruction, ASV) functions back into per-instruction groups, then dispatches on the recognized tensor operation through two paths. The compute path maps each tensor op to an XLA-HLO template: dot_product 

expands to convert + dot + add (with optional clamp ), pool to reduce(max) ,Conference’17, July 2017, Washington, DC, USA Gao et al. 

and im2col_matmul to reshape + dot + add ; when the RTL encodes an activation function, the corresponding HLO operation is in-serted (e.g., maximum(result, 0) for ReLU). The DMA path clas-sifies memory-port roles (DRAM address vs. scratchpad address) from the annotated metadata and emits a load or store body ac-cordingly. For CISC-style loop macros that orchestrate sequences of primi-tive instructions behind a single invocation (e.g., Gemmini’s loop_ws ), ATLAAS recovers the primitive instruction vocabulary and loop-bound configuration registers from the controller’s RTL, then com-poses the primitive tensor operation over the extracted bounds into a whole-program HLO body. Instruction ordering constraints—e.g., that compute_preloaded may only fire after preload —are recov-ered by analyzing FSM register updates across instruction groups and folded into the specification as structured metadata. 

4 Evaluation 

We evaluate ATLAAS along four axes: (§4.2) how effectively the lifting pipeline compresses bit-level MLIR into tensor-level seman-tics; (§4.3) whether the lifted semantics are provably equivalent to the RTL; (§4.4) how the automatically extracted specification com-pares to a hand-written reference; and (§4.5) whether the extracted specification can drive end-to-end compiler backend generation.

A natural question is why we do not directly compare compiler backends generated from the two specifications. The reason is that the TAIDL MICRO 2025 artifact targets a functional simulator , not a compiler backend. Its specification describes how individual hard-ware instructions move data between scratchpad and accumulator at per-tile (16 ×16) granularity—exactly the level of detail a cycle-accurate or functional simulator needs to replay an instruction trace. However, a compiler backend requires a different abstraction: per-kernel granularity that maps an entire HLO dot or convolu-tion onto the hardware. The MICRO 2025 artifact’s per-instruction patterns are too granular for ACT’s instruction selection, which expects CISC-level macro-instructions (e.g., loop_ws matching a whole matrix multiply, loop_conv_ws matching a whole convolu-tion). 4.5 End-to-End Compiler Backend Generation 

To validate that ATLAAS’s extracted specification is sufficient for practical use, we feed it into the ACT compiler framework [ 22 ]to automatically generate a Gemmini compiler backend, and com-pare the generated code against hand-written kernels on realistic workloads. 

Methodology. We take ATLAAS’s extracted TAIDL specification and pass it to ACT, which generates an XLA-compatible compiler backend using equality saturation for instruction selection and constraint programming for scratchpad memory allocation. Com-pleting the path from specification to executable binary required engineering contributions to ACT: HLO frontend support for JAX-produced operations (e.g., convolution , reduce_max ), enode-level preconditions for correct convolution dispatch, and memory alloca-tor support for multi-layer chains. We then reimplement the bench-mark suite from gemmini-rocc-tests [ 44 ]—the official bare-metal test suite provided by the Gemmini team—in JAX [ 4], compiling through the ACT-generated backend. For each benchmark, we com-pare the Spike cycle count of the ACT-compiled binary against the hand-written C kernel from gemmini-rocc-tests, ensuring identical workload parameters: layer shapes, weight-stationary dataflow, ac-tivation functions, scaling, and batch sizes. The benchmark suite spans MLP, convolutional, and transformer workloads ranging from 2-layer networks (1,501 cycles) to full ResNet-50 (5,544,077 cycles) and MobileNet (3,408,414 cycles). 

Results. Table 5 and Figure 5 report the results. Across all seven benchmarks, the automatically generated backend achieves a geo-metric mean speedup of 1.014 × over hand-written kernels, with per-benchmark speedups ranging from 0.981 × to 1.049 ×. The small MLP benchmarks (mlp2, mlp3) show the ACT backend running slightly faster ( 1.047 –1.049 ×), reflecting minor tiling differences in the generated code. The transformer benchmark shows the only slowdown ( 0.981 ×), attributable to per-tile configuration overhead in the generated instruction sequence. On the large workloads that dominate real deployment—ResNet-50 (49 conv layers) and MobileNet (52 layers)—the speedup is 1.000 ×.This result demonstrates that ATLAAS, combined with ACT, en-ables a fully automated path from RTL to a functional, performance-competitive compiler backend. 

5 Related Work 

Formal accelerator interfaces and model extraction. Instruction-Level Abstraction (ILA) [ 17 ] established a software-visible specifi-cation framework for accelerators and processors, while the sub-sequent autoGenILA work [ 49 , 50 ] showed how architecture-level models can be derived automatically from RTL designs. 3LA [ 16 ] fur-ther demonstrated how such formal interfaces can support application-level validation of accelerators. ATLAAS builds directly on this line of work but differs in its target abstraction: rather than stopping at scalar architectural state updates or validation-oriented interfaces, it lifts extracted semantics to tensor-visible instruction behavior suitable for software-stack generation. 

Tensor-accelerator specification and backend automation. TAIDL [ 21 ]introduced a domain-specific language for expressing tensor-accelerator ISA semantics, and ACT [ 22 ] showed that compiler backends can be generated automatically from such specifications. These efforts automate the downstream path from ISA description to tooling but assume that a tensor-level specification already exists. ATLAAS addresses this missing front-end step by recovering TAIDL-like semantics from RTL-derived low-level models. 

MLIR and hardware IR semantics. At the compiler-infrastructure level, our work is enabled by MLIR [ 26 ] as a progressive abstrac-tion framework, and is adjacent to hardware-oriented MLIR efforts such as CIRCT [ 11 ]. Recent work on K-CIRCT [ 52 ] and first-class verification dialects for MLIR [ 12 ] further highlights the value of giving hardware and compiler IR dialects explicit semantics. AT-LAAS differs from these efforts in emphasis: rather than defining semantics for an existing IR or hardware dialect, it uses MLIR as the staging ground for semantic lifting from extracted hardware behavior to tensor-level ISA descriptions. 

Top-down accelerator programming and generation. ATLAAS should be distinguished from top-down frameworks such as HeteroCL [ 25 ], Calyx [ 34 ], and Exo [ 18 ]. Those systems help users design, program, or compile for accelerators from higher-level descriptions, whereas ATLAAS is a bottom-up approach that starts from an existing RTL implementation and reconstructs the software-visible tensor inter-face. In that sense, ATLAAS also has some affinity with semantic lifting and raising work in binary analysis [ 10 ], but its end goal is not generic decompilation to compiler IR; it is recovery of struc-tured tensor semantics that can be consumed by accelerator-specific tooling. 

6 Conclusion 

We presented ATLAAS, the first end-to-end pipeline that automati-cally lifts RTL-extracted accelerator semantics to tensor-level ISA specifications, addressing the manual specification bottleneck that has prevented automated compiler generation tools from reaching the vast majority of tensor accelerator designs. By introducing an 8-pass MLIR semantic lifting pipeline—spanning bit-manipulation canonicalization, MAC pattern detection, control-flow specializa-tion, saturation recovery, loop reconstruction, linalg lifting, and structured metadata emission—ATLAAS bridges the abstraction gap between gate-level hardware behavior and the tensor-level semantics required by specification languages like TAIDL. ATLAAS: Automatic Tensor-Level Abstraction of Accelerator Semantics Conference’17, July 2017, Washington, DC, USA 

Evaluated on Gemmini and VTA, ATLAAS processes 156 MLIR files totaling over 1.2 million lines of bit-level IR, achieving up to 92.9% reduction on compute-dominated modules and recovering hardware features—multi-bank DMA configuration, pooling engine semantics, and im2col support—that were absent from hand-written references. All lifted semantics are formally verified equivalent to the underlying RTL via Z3 SMT proofs, and the same pipeline gen-eralizes across accelerator architectures without modification. By feeding the extracted specification into ACT, ATLAAS enables a fully automated path from RTL to a performance-competitive com-piler backend, achieving a geometric mean speedup of 1.014 × over hand-written kernels on workloads up to ResNet-50 and MobileNet. More broadly, ATLAAS closes the frontmost gap in the RTL-to-framework pipeline: the step from hardware design to machine-readable ISA semantics that has historically required deep architec-tural expertise and manual effort. With this gap filled, the complete automated path—RTL through ATLAAS to TAIDL specification, then through ACT to an XLA-compatible compiler backend—is now possible, lowering the barrier for new accelerator designs to achieve full software-stack integration.
