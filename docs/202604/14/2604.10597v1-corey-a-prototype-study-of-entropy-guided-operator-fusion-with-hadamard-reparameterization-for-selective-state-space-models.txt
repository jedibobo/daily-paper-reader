# Abstract 

State Space Models (SSMs), represented by the Mamba family, provide linear-time sequence modeling and are attractive for long-context inference. Yet practical deployments remain memory-bandwidth limited because selective state updates are often decomposed into fragmented kernels with repeated intermediate ten-sor materialization. We present COREY, a prototype framework that combines memory-aware operator fusion with Hadamard-based feature reparameterization. Activation entropy, estimated with fixed-width histograms, is used as a runtime scheduling statistic to place fusion boundaries and choose tile sizes. To regularize heavy-tailed activations, we absorb normalized Hadamard transforms into linear projections, preserving functional equivalence while reducing peak-coordinate concentration. In a controlled prototype study over heavy-tailed SSM activations, COREY consistently reduces proxy latency, improves throughput, and lowers DRAM traffic relative to unfused and fixed-depth baselines. Low-bit results are reported only through a hand-crafted stability proxy and are intended as diagnostic evidence rather than checkpoint-level quality claims. Code repository: https://github.com/mabo1215/COREY_Transformer. git .

# 1 Introduction 

Transformer attention has strong modeling capacity but incurs quadratic complexity O(L2) in sequence length L [Vaswani et al., 2017]. Modern selective SSMs provide linear complexity O(L),making them promising for long-sequence and streaming workloads [Gu and Dao, 2023]. Yet asymptotic gains do not automatically translate into hardware efficiency. In current GPU deployments, selective scan pipelines often execute as multiple pointwise and reduction-like kernels with frequent global-memory traffic for intermediate states. This turns the critical path into a memory-bound workload. Prior fusion studies indicate that aggressive fusion and memory-aware scheduling can reduce materialization overhead and improve effective arithmetic intensity.

A second challenge is activation outliers in SSM internals, especially output channels with heavy-tailed statistics. These outliers can destabilize low-bit quantization and reduce the safe depth of fusion due to amplified numeric sensitivity. Quantization-oriented SSM work suggests that Hadamard transforms can smooth activation distributions and reduce outlier concentration. This paper unifies these observations into a single framework, COREY: entropy-guided operator fusion with fused Hadamard reparameterization. We use entropy as a direct, runtime-friendly signal for deciding fusion boundaries and tile granularity, bridging distribution shaping and systems optimization. In the present paper, we study this mechanism as a controlled prototype rather than as a deployment-grade fused-kernel implementation, and we reserve hardware claims for future checkpoint-level validation. Despite the close connection between outlier suppression and deep fusion, prior treatments often dis-cuss these issues separately. Our formulation makes the link explicit: Hadamard-based redistribution smooths the activation distribution, raises empirical entropy, and thereby expands the safe operating region for aggressive fusion and low-precision execution. 

Contributions. (1) We introduce an entropy-driven criterion for dynamic fusion boundary selection in SSM kernels. (2) We design a fused Hadamard layer that embeds forward/inverse transforms into projection blocks while preserving functional equivalence. (3) We connect entropy gain with outlier suppression and quantify how Hadamard mixing contracts peak-coordinate magnitude, which is directly relevant to clipping-limited low-bit execution. (4) We validate the resulting design with a reproducible prototype study and checkpoint-harness sanity checks, while explicitly separating these results from deployment-grade hardware and perplexity claims. 

# 2 Related Work 

Efficient long-context modeling. Transformers remain the dominant sequence architecture but require quadratic attention cost in context length [Vaswani et al., 2017]. A parallel line of work seeks linear-time alternatives via recurrent and state-space formulations. Selective SSMs, including Mamba-style designs, show competitive quality with hardware-friendly recurrence [Gu and Dao, 2023], while structured state space duality further tightens the conceptual link between SSM and Transformer families [Gu and Dao, 2024]. 

Operator fusion and memory-aware scheduling. On modern GPUs, many sequence kernels are bottlenecked by memory movement rather than FLOPs. Prior systems work demonstrates that deeper fusion and memory-aware tiling can reduce intermediate materialization and improve arithmetic intensity. Our method differs by introducing a distribution-aware signal (entropy) to decide fusion boundaries dynamically, instead of relying only on static kernel graphs or heuristic depth limits. FlashAttention [Dao et al., 2022] and Triton [Tillet et al., 2019] demonstrate the impact of memory-aware tiling and fused kernels on Transformer workloads; our work extends this tiling philosophy to SSMs with an entropy-driven boundary criterion. Unlike arithmetic-intensity-only scheduling, the proposed signal can react to prompt-dependent activation shifts and to the distributional flattening induced by Hadamard mixing. In that sense, COREY uses entropy as the bridge variable between activation geometry and fusion feasibility rather than as a generic profiling surrogate. 

SSM quantization and Hadamard smoothing. Recent SSM quantization studies indicate that activation outliers are a key failure mode under low-bit inference, and that Hadamard rotation can smooth heavy-tailed statistics [Chiang et al., 2024, Xu et al., 2025]. We build on this finding but move beyond pure quantization: we use entropy increase after Hadamard rotation as a scheduling signal that directly controls fusion depth and tile selection. 3.2 Entropy-Driven Fusion Strategy 

For candidate fusion region R, we compute a fusion score: 

S(R) = α eH(R) + β fAI (R) − γ fM (R), (4) where eH, fAI , and fM are normalized entropy, arithmetic intensity, and memory-traffic estimates. 3.3 Fused Hadamard Layer 

Let H be a normalized Hadamard matrix with H⊤H = I. For linear projection y = W x , we insert a transform pair: 

y = W H ⊤Hx = W ′ ˆx, ˆx = Hx, W ′ = W H ⊤. (5) 3This preserves exact functional equivalence while operating in a rotated basis. We fuse Hx , projection, and adjacent elementwise operators into a single kernel to avoid additional memory passes. Beyond outlier suppression, the rotation enlarges the safe region for aggressive fusion by redistributing heavy-tailed energy across channels. In our setting, the entropy increase after reparameterization is therefore not just a quantization artifact; it is a direct systems signal that the surrounding operators can be fused more deeply without destabilizing low-precision execution. 

3.4 Entropy-Tiling Co-Design 

Entropy is also used to modulate tiling decisions. High-entropy regions support larger tiles because activation mass is more evenly distributed and resource use is more predictable; low-entropy regions benefit from smaller tiles that reduce register pressure and guard against over-fusion. In practice, our implementation treats entropy, arithmetic intensity, and memory traffic as coupled signals: the scheduler first forms candidate fusion groups, then assigns more aggressive tile shapes only when the resulting group remains within register and shared-memory limits. The same score can be viewed as a constrained partitioning objective over contiguous operator regions. In the current system, we use the thresholded rule above at runtime and defer the optimization view, dynamic-programming formulation, and adaptive-threshold details to the appendix.

# 5 Experimental Setup 

5.1 Implementation Model and Baselines 

Our evaluation is a controlled prototype study rather than a full end-to-end Triton benchmark. We instantiate the scheduler on heavy-tailed synthetic activations, validate the exactness of Hadamard weight absorption, and measure latency, throughput, DRAM bytes per token, and quality-drop proxies across multiple sequence lengths and precision settings. This setup is intended to isolate the interaction between entropy-guided scheduling, outlier smoothing, and memory traffic without conflating the analysis with backend-specific kernel engineering. 

Prototype execution environment. All experiments are executed on a single workstation running Windows 11 with an Intel Core i9-13900K CPU and 64 GB RAM. The prototype pipeline uses Python 3.11, PyTorch 2.3, and NumPy 1.26. Because no GPU kernel is invoked, latency and through-put figures reflect Python-level timing of the simulated scheduler and are not directly comparable to production Triton benchmarks. Unless otherwise noted, each reported point aggregates 5 repeated runs from the prototype experiment pipeline. We log entropy before and after Hadamard reparameterization, outlier ratios, validated projection error, average fusion depth, latency, throughput, and DRAM bytes per token. Where variance is available from the exported repeated-run traces, we report mean ± standard deviation. In the prototype study, quality drop is a synthetic degradation proxy rather than a checkpoint-level perplexity or task-accuracy metric. Concretely, it combines a precision-dependent quantization sensitivity term, an over-fusion penalty that increases when deep fusion is attempted in low-entropy regions, and a Hadamard stabilization bonus proportional to entropy gain and outlier reduction. Because this proxy encodes the proposed mechanism directly, it is suitable only as an internal scheduler diagnostic; it is not used as a substitute for perplexity or downstream task accuracy. Lower values are better, and a value of zero indicates that the simulated schedule incurred no measurable proxy degradation relative to the unfused FP16 reference. 

5.5 Protocol for Pretrained SSM Evaluation 

To bridge the prototype study and deployment-oriented validation, we define a real-model benchmark protocol on three pretrained selective SSMs: Mamba-370M, Mamba-1.4B, and Mamba-2.8B. We target long-context evaluation on WikiText-103 [Merity et al., 2017], PG19, and LongBench [Bai et al., 2023], and measure perplexity, tokens per second, latency in ms/token, DRAM bytes/token, and energy in J/token when hardware telemetry is available. We compare against native PyTorch inference and entropy-guided execution under matched tokeniza-tion and decoding settings. Precision settings include FP16, W8A8, and W4A8 when the checkpoint and backend support them. Full harness details and deployment-oriented implementation notes are deferred to the appendix. Because the current repository contains a controlled scheduler prototype rather than full checkpoint-level deployment code, we do not claim pretrained-model gains in the present results section. The checkpoint evidence is nevertheless materially broader than a smoke-test-only sanity pass: in the repaired WSL2 fast-path environment we now execute the same four-task LongBench subset with 20 samples per task on both Mamba-370M and Mamba-1.4B, attach both WikiText-103 and PG19 side perplexity, run a bounded benchmark-only pass on Mamba-2.8B, and add one fair external baseline on Pythia-410M [Biderman et al., 2023] under the same four-task script. The remaining gap is therefore no longer raw checkpoint reachability, but broader task coverage and method-versus-baseline execution on real COREY/static-fusion backends. 

5.6 LongBench-Oriented Evaluation Protocol 

Within the pretrained-model stage, LongBench [Bai et al., 2023] serves as the primary long-context reasoning suite because it spans retrieval, multi-hop reasoning, narrative understanding, and long-document summarization. Our intended comparison uses identical tokenization and decoding settings across native inference, static fusion, and COREY, and reports both task accuracy and systems metrics. This split is important: even if a fused backend improves latency and throughput, it must preserve answer quality on long-context tasks to count as a valid deployment improvement. Accordingly, the LongBench protocol is paired with WikiText-103 [Merity et al., 2017] perplexity checks. The former measures downstream long-context utility; the latter detects subtle regressions that may not surface in task averages alone. We therefore treat task accuracy, perplexity, latency, TPS, and DRAM bytes/token as a joint evaluation bundle rather than isolated metrics. The evaluation harness is designed so that systems metrics and task-quality metrics are collected from the same run. At present, it has been exercised through a local smoke-test backend, through a repaired WSL2 fast-path matrix covering Mamba-370M and Mamba-1.4B on a common four-task LongBench subset with 20 samples per task, through one matched external Pythia baseline, and through a real selective-scan Triton timing probe in the same WSL2 CUDA 12.8 stack. Additional harness details and deployment-oriented Triton integration notes are deferred to the appendix.

# 7 Ablation Studies 

7.1 Entropy Threshold τ

We sweep τ from conservative to aggressive fusion. Very low thresholds over-fuse and can hurt low-bit quality; very high thresholds under-fuse and leave memory savings unrealized. Mid-range thresholds produce the best latency-quality trade-off. 

7.2 Tiling Policy 

We compare static tiling against entropy-guided tiling. Entropy-guided tiling selects smaller tiles in low-entropy segments (to avoid register pressure and instability) and larger tiles in high-entropy segments (to maximize data reuse). The current prototype now exports a per-tile surrogate runtime trace, including tile-level entropy, memory, compute, latency, and cumulative group-runtime estimates. In the matched-depth comparison, this surrogate trace shows that entropy-guided scheduling retains a lower mean group-runtime estimate than arithmetic-only matched scheduling in every bucket, with reductions of 0.53 ms in the short bucket and 1.07 ms in the ultra-long bucket; Appendix A summarizes representative short- and ultra-long traces. These traces remain prototype-level surrogates rather than real GPU kernel timings, so Table 6 still reports only the depth and latency consequences that are stable enough for the main text. 

7.3 Quantization Bit Width 

We evaluate FP16, W8A8, and W4A8. Gains from entropy guidance persist across precisions in the prototype cost model, while the low-bit proxy margin over static fusion is most visible at lower bit widths where outlier sensitivity is strongest. These low-bit trends should be read as diagnostic evidence only. 

7.4 Sequence-Length Stratification 

We report ablation trends by context strata (1k–2k, 4k–8k, 16k–32k, 64k). Entropy-guided scheduling provides increasing speedup as sequence length grows, confirming that our strategy targets the dominant long-context bottlenecks.

# 9 Broader Impact 

If the scheduling ideas explored here transfer to real fused kernels, lower memory traffic per gener-ated token could reduce the energy cost of long-context SSM inference and modestly improve the environmental footprint of serving these models. That said, the present paper only establishes this effect in a controlled prototype study, so any real energy claim remains provisional until hardware power measurements are collected. More generally, improved inference efficiency can lower deployment cost for beneficial long-context applications such as document analysis and scientific assistance, but it can also reduce the barrier to scaling surveillance or low-quality mass generation workloads. # 10 Conclusion 

Entropy increase after Hadamard reparameterization indicates stronger information diffusion and reduced outlier concentration. This enables deeper fusion and tighter clipping bounds within the prototype setting. By reformulating fusion scheduling as an entropy-regularized optimization problem and relating feasibility to explicit hardware constraints, we strengthen the argument that COREY is not merely heuristic but a structured systems method. Our controlled prototype study shows that this design consistently improves scheduling and memory-traffic proxies; deployment-grade systems or quality claims still require real fused-kernel benchmarks and checkpoint-level perplexity or task measurements. Cross-hardware validation on a remote RTX 3090 server confirms that checkpoint quality is platform-independent, and data-parallel sharding across two GPUs yields near-linear wall-clock speedup without any change to per-sample accuracy. Future accelerator co-design can expose native fast Hadamard primitives and fusion-aware local-memory scheduling interfaces, allowing runtime entropy signals to tune tile shape and fusion depth. The immediate next milestone is to extend the now three-checkpoint benchmark matrix from bounded smoke coverage to broader LongBench sample counts, harmonized prompt lengths, additional language-model datasets, and method-versus-baseline comparisons on real checkpoints.

Cross-hardware reproducibility on RTX 3090. To validate that the above WSL2+RTX 3070 scores are not artefacts of a specific machine, we re-ran the identical Mamba-370M four-task 20-sample suite on a remote server equipped with four NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPUs (24 GiB each, CUDA 12.1, PyTorch 2.4.0+cu121, FP16 fast path). The remote environment used the same 

state-spaces/mamba-370m-hf checkpoint and identically seeded sample selection. Scores on the remote machine were 0.0141 (NarrativeQA token-F1), 0.0443 (Qasper token-F1), 0.000 21 (MultifieldQA-EN exact match), and 0.1472 (GovReport ROUGE-L), matching the WSL2+RTX 3070 results in Table 3 to within expected 20-sample noise. Mean per-sample latency on the remote was higher (4890 ms average) due to NFS I/O overhead rather than GPU throughput; raw decode through-put is comparable once NFS wait time is subtracted. 

Multi-GPU data-parallel throughput. Using the same remote server, we validated a data-parallel sharding strategy for LongBench inference: the 20-sample pool is split evenly across 

K GPUs via a –sample-offset argument per shard, with each shard pinned to a dedicated 

CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICES process. The per-shard outputs are merged by a lightweight script that re-averages per-task scores and sums sample counts. Table 4 in the main paper reports the resulting wall-clock speedup. Merged per-task scores agree with the single-GPU baseline to within sampling noise, confirming that sharding introduces no change to per-sample predictions. 22
