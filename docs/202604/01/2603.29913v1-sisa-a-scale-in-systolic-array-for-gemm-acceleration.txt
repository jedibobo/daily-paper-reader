Abstract. The currently dominant AI/ML workloads, such as Large Language Models (LLMs), rely on the efficient execution of General Matrix-Matrix Multiplication (GEMM) operations. Thus, most systems are equipped with dedicated matrix hardware accelerators based on square Systolic Arrays (SAs) of Processing Elements (PEs). While this organiza-tion was effective for traditional Deep Neural Networks (DNNs), LLMs introduce input-dependent and highly skewed matrices, leading to un-derutilized SA resources. To address this challenge, we propose SISA (Scale-In Systolic Array), a novel SA architecture that partitions the traditional square array into horizontal rectangular slabs. With minimal overhead, SISA exposes par-allelism through independently scheduled slabs for efficient execution of small or skewed matrix shapes, while retaining full-array operation for large GEMMs. SISA achieves up to 8.52 × speedup and 93% energy-delay-product (EDP) reduction for representative LLMs compared to a state-of-the-art monolithic SA with the same number of PEs. 

Keywords: systolic array, GEMM, LLM, accelerator, adaptive archi-tecture 

## 1 Introduction 

In recent years, the use of AI-based applications has increased rapidly. Large Language Models (LLMs), which form the backbone of widely deployed interactive systems such as ChatGPT, are quite demanding on the computing systems. LLM inference consists of two distinct phases: prefill and decode , each with fundamentally different characteristics. During prefill, the model processes the entire input prompt using dense General Matrix-Matrix Multiplications (GEMMs), which are compute-bound. In contrast, decode generates tokens sequentially us-ing General Matrix-Vector Multiplications (GEMVs), making it predominantly memory-bandwidth bound. In addition to GEMVs, the decode also repeatedly accesses the key–value (KV) cache, which increases in size at each decode step.

Prefill benefits from Systolic Array (SA)-based accelerators that max-imize throughput for large GEMMs, whereas decode is better suited to Vector Processing Units (VPUs) that efficiently handle GEMVs. However, for LLM systems supporting multiple concurrent requests, batching may be exploited to increase arithmetic intensity and amortize memory traffic [1, 2]. This leads to having GEMM operations in the decode phase as well, making a SA-based accelerator the primary architecture for LLMs. As a result, many modern AI accelerators incorporate SAs. The most well-known example is the Google TPU, which integrates a large square SA (e.g., 128 ×128 and 256 ×256 PEs) [11] that is highly efficient for executing the large GEMMs common in LLM workloads. Nevertheless, when matrices are highly-skewed or smaller than the SA size, a significant fraction of PEs remain idle. Previous works have shown that such SA-matrix shape mismatches lead to considerable under-utilization [4], and these mismatches are common in practice. Figure 1a shows that real-world chatbot prompts are typically short (mean 41.8, median 12 tokens) [17], leading to skewed GEMMs during the LLM prefill phase. Consequently, the LLM prefill phase often involves skewed matrices (e.g., 

(12 , 8192) for a 12-token prompt in Llama3.2-3B). In addition, the number of tokens per prompt varies across requests. When multi-batching is not possible or when latency-sensitive prefill is required, matrix shapes remain highly skewed, since increasing batching may degrade Quality-of-Service by increasing the Time To First Token (TTFT). When decode requests are batched to amortize memory access costs in a GEMM-based LLM decode phase, the resulting matrix shapes depend on the batch size, with small batches yielding highly skewed matrices. However, two fac-tors prevent aggressively large batching (e.g., decode batch sizes beyond 32–64). First, larger batch sizes increase response time, so batching is kept moderate to balance QoS and efficiency. Second, the decode phase accesses the KV cache, which can become large depending on the request. Since each request main-SISA: A Scale-In Systolic Array for GEMM Acceleration 3

In order to address these and other mismatch issues between SAs and GEMM shapes of matrices, researchers have proposed different architectures that can adapt the arrangement of available PEs to better match the shapes of the ma-trices [5, 7, 9, 10, 13, 15, 20, 21]. In Figure 1b we show the tradeoff between flexi-bility/efficiency and overhead/complexity. Monolithic SA accelerators serves as the baseline, whereas flexible accelerators such as the multi-pod SA offers more flexibility at the cost of increased buffer and interconnection network overheads. To address this challenge, we propose SISA, a new SA architecture that extends the traditional n × n square design with a scale-in mechanism that partitions the array into horizontal smaller rectangular sub-arrays, referred to as slabs, capable of parallel, independently scheduled execution. This slab-level parallelism enables SISA to efficiently process highly skewed matrix shapes by increasing effective parallelism relative to a monolithic array. When full-array execution is advantageous, SISA seamlessly operates as a conventional systolic array, preserving peak GEMM performance while incurring only minimal addi-tional on-chip buffering overhead. This design point is represented in Figure 1b. The main contributions of this work are: 

– A novel SA architecture that operates either as a unified array or partitioned into slabs that better adapt to skewed matrix shapes. 

– A scheduling and tiling mechanism that exploits parallelism across slabs. 

– An instance of SISA with 128 ×128 PEs and its evaluation using state-of-the-art (SOTA) LLM models. We have evaluated SISA with GEMM operations from LLMs, and observed a speedup of up to 8.52 × and a reduction in energy-delay-product (EDP) up to 93% when compared to a TPU with the same amount of compute and memory resources. In the worst case, SISA achieves comparable performance to the TPU with only an 8.47% higher EDP. When compared to alternative SOTA flexible SA architectures, SISA performs better in most cases, achieving speedups up to 

2.61 × while also exhibiting lower area overhead. 

## 2 Related Work 

To meet the computational requirements of LLMs, SAs appear as a fitting ar-chitecture to build ML accelerators. SAs are architectures composed by a set of interconnected PEs. Thanks to their regular structure and communication pat-terns, SAs can offer high computational capabilities for GEMM while keeping energy consumption efficient compared to other architectures: in SAs, results are fed directly to the next computational unit without intermediate storage, following a systolic wavefront [12]. However, while monolithic SAs are a matching architecture for computing GEMM, multiple variations have been presented in recent works. Monolithic Systolic Arrays. Early DL accelerators adopted large, fixed-size SAs to maximize GEMM throughput, like Google’s TPU [11] and Gemmini [6]. Scaling up a single array provides high compute density and high throughput for large workloads, but it suffers from substantial under-utilization for small or irregular matrices, where many PEs remain idle due to poor alignment between workload shape and array dimensions [4]. 

Multi-Pod Systolic Architectures. To improve utilization, one approach is to build architectures based on smaller SAs, named pods, and distribute com-putation across them. Example of this approach are Scale-Out Systolic Arrays (SOSA) [21] and Vectored Systolic Arrays (SAVector) [5]. Overall, multi-pod sys-tolic architectures enhance parallel tile-level processing but inherently increase data movement and off-chip memory access due to duplicated tile loads across pods. In addition, their fixed pod granularity limits utilization on large GEMMs where a unified array would provide higher data reuse. These architectures in-crease flexibility but introduce redundant data movement and elevated memory traffic, resulting in a reduction of the overall efficiency. 

Reconfigurable Systolic Arrays. ## 3 Scale-In Systolic Array 

3.1 Architecture 

At a high level, SISA consists of a fixed pool of PEs organized as a logical two-dimensional SA and horizontally partitioned into rectangular slabs to efficiently process skewed matrices. Each slab forms an independently controllable systolic unit with local buffers for activations and weights. Through multiplexing and power-gating, slabs can be fused into larger logical arrays or operated indepen-dently to exploit parallelism within a GEMM. Unused slabs can be power-gated to reduce idle energy. SISA operates as a CPU-hosted accelerator with access to external memory. Figure 2 provides an overview of the architecture. 

Slab Structure and Dataflow. Each slab has a rectangular organization, spanning the full width of the SA while occupying a fraction of its height since LLM workloads are typically narrow in one dimension but wide in the other. As described earlier, each slab includes local buffers for activations and weights, while output values are written directly to the global output buffer. SISA employs an Output-Stationary (OS) dataflow. Under this dataflow, each PE is responsi-ble for a unique output element and maintains the corresponding accumulator 6 Altamura et al. 

locally for the duration of the GEMM processing. As a result, partial sums do not traverse inter-PE interconnects; only activations and weights are streamed through the array. This property simplifies slab composition and enables modu-lar and scalable execution across fused and independent slabs. The OS dataflow is particularly effective when the SA matches, even partially, the output tensor dimensions of the GEMM. 

Slab Fusion Mechanism. Slabs can be fused along the height dimension of the SA to form larger logical slabs. As shown in Figure 2, this is enabled through a simple buffer bypass implemented with multiplexers, forwarding the weights from one slab directly to the next while bypassing redundant slab-local weight buffers. When fused, adjacent slabs execute a single tile as a larger logical systolic array. Multiple fused slab groups may operate concurrently, each executing a different tile of the same GEMM. 

Buffering Organization. SISA employs a hierarchical buffering organization composed of a global buffer, slab-local buffers, and a dedicated global output buffer. All buffers employ double buffering to overlap data movement with com-putation. The global buffer stores activations and weights, while the output buffer collects the results produced by the slabs before they are written back to memory. 

Global buffer organization. The global buffer is implemented as a collection of partitioned SRAM banks. Each bank is dedicated to providing activations or weights to a specific set of local slab buffers. Thanks to the regular data access patterns of SA-based GEMM execution, this organization enables building larger SRAM capacity while maintaining bounded access latency. Each bank has a port width of multiple elements, allowing it to write to several local buffers per cycle and increasing effective bandwidth with limited latency overhead. 

Output buffer organization. A multi-banked output buffer collects the results produced by the slabs and supports both independent and fused slab execution. 

Local slab buffers. Each slab contains small local buffers for activations and weights placed next to the array boundary to directly feed the PEs. Each PE along the upper boundary of the slab receives data from a dedicated pair of banks. 

Power-Gating. Power management in SISA is performed at the granularity of individual slabs. Slab-local buffers can be selectively disabled, and entire slabs can be power-gated when not required for execution. This coarse-grain power-gating strategy minimizes idle energy consumption.

## 4 Evaluation 

4.1 Experimental Setup 

For area and energy evaluation, we model SISA using RTL synthesis and SRAM estimation tools. The design is synthesized with Cadence Genus 18.14 targeting a 28 nm technology node with the ASAP7 cell library [16] at 1 GHz. On-chip buffers are modeled using CACTI [3, 18]. For performance evaluation, we extend SCALE-Sim [14] to support the architectural features required by our design. We compare SISA against two representative SA baselines: Google’s TPUv4 [11], representing a monolithic SA, and ReDas [9], a reconfigurable SA that reports improvements over prior SOTA designs discussed in Section 2. All baselines are modeled according to their published configurations using the same RTL+CACTI methodology to ensure fairness. CACTI-based SRAM estimations were validated against the detailed memory area numbers reported in ReDas, obtained using TSMC’s 28 nm memory compiler. The relative error is only 0.27%. SISA is evaluated using a 128 × 128 SA, consistent with the configurations adopted in TPUv4 and ReDas. We do not model the roundabout interconnect overhead in ReDas, which benefits this baseline. Further architectural details of the evaluated designs are provided in Section 4.2. 4.2 Designing a 128 × 128 PE 8-Slab SISA Slabs. The slab height of 16 is primarily chosen to match practical off-chip band-width limits. Concurrently streaming activations and weights for eight slabs at BF16 precision requires approximately 2.3 TB/s, which aligns with the capabil-ities of modern HBM4 systems (up to ∼2.8 TB/s [8]). Finer-grain partitioning would exceed feasible bandwidth constraints and is therefore not considered. 

Memory Organization. For a fair comparison, we allocate the same total on-chip memory capacity across TPU, ReDas, and SISA, organized according to each architecture. The TPU-like baseline employs two 4 MB buffers (activation and weight) and one 2 MB output buffer. ReDas distributes four 2.5 MB buffers around the SA, following its multi-mode organization. SISA preserves the same total memory budget using 8 MB global activation and weight buffer and 2 MB of output buffer.

4.4 SISA versus Alternative Reconfigurable SA 

Figure 6 shows the speedup of SISA over ReDas across the same sequence-length sweep. We compare performance against ReDas using an extended version of 12 Altamura et al. 

SCALE-Sim, as described in Section 4.1. To avoid penalizing ReDas, the model abstracts certain control and data-movement overheads, making the comparison favorable to ReDas in terms of latency. For this reason, we report performance only and omit EDP results. The relative area and energy overheads of the two designs are analyzed later in this section. 

Small sequence lengths ( 1 ≤ m ≤ 32 ). In this regime, SISA outperforms ReDas. Operating in independent slab mode, SISA maximizes PE utilization and achieves up to 2.61 × and 1.61 × speedup in the 16 × 128 and 32 × 128 configu-rations, respectively. Although ReDas reshapes its array, its coarse granularity limits effective PE activation for skewed GEMMs, resulting in lower utilization. 

Large sequence lengths ( m ≥ 33 ). As m increases, ReDas performs best in the mid-range ( m ≈ 33 –50) across all models, where its reshaping aligns well with the workload. Beyond m ≈ 50 , SISA regains advantage for Qwen2.5-0.5B and Qwen2.5-1.5B, whose layer dimensions better match slab sizes, reducing residual-tile overhead that continues to affect ReDas. For larger models, the higher compute volume amortizes residual tiles, allowing ReDas to maintain a modest advantage, although SISA underperforms by at most 1.36 ×. At m =64 , SISA slightly recovers despite using the same PE budget: ReDas forms a 

64 × 256 array, while SISA operates as two 64 × 128 slabs, reducing fill-and-drain overhead. For 64 ≤ m ≤ 128 , both architectures operate in effectively monolithic configurations and achieve comparable performance. Beyond m > 128 , SISA again benefits from slab-level execution when processing residual tiles, achieving up to 1.17 × speedup over ReDas.

To illustrate these trends, we analyze two representative points: the best-case configuration ( m =16 ) and the worst-case configuration ( m = 33 ). Table 2 lists the linear layers of Qwen2.5-0.5B, and Figure 7 shows their latency in cycles weighted by layer occurrence for these two cases. The overall results are presented earlier in this section. For m = 16 , SISA operates in an 8 × (16 × 128) configuration, while ReDas forms a 16 × 448 array. In this setting, SISA achieves higher effective utilization and reduced tail inefficiencies. Additionally, 44% of execution occurs with one or more slabs power-gated, improving energy efficiency without affecting latency. Although smaller than the final projection layer, Layer 2 ( M = 16 , N = 4864 ,

K = 896 ) is repeated 48 times per block and therefore dominates the total latency. At m = 33 , SISA transitions to a fused 2 × 64 × 128 configuration, while ReDas reshapes into a 32 × 384 array, activating more PEs and achieving its peak advantage. For Layer 1 ( M = 33 , N = 896 , K = 896 ), SISA still attains slightly higher utilization due to better slab alignment, partially offsetting the additional PE availability of ReDas. 

Area and Energy Comparison. ReDas exhibits a slightly lower memory overhead, with SRAM accounting for about 0.4% less of the total chip area SISA: A Scale-In Systolic Array for GEMM Acceleration 13 

compared to SISA. However, when considering the PE-array, ReDas reports a 70% per-PE area overhead and a 2.49 × increase power for an INT8 design. When scaling the design from INT8 to BF16, the PE area increases due to the wider datapath. In our design, the PE array dominates chip area (87.2%), while on-chip SRAM accounts for 12.8%. Therefore, increasing PE complexity has a much larger impact on total area and energy. Although the relative overhead of ReDas may decrease in BF16, it still introduces additional logic inside each PE. In contrast, SISA preserves a simple PE design and concentrates overhead in the memory hierarchy, resulting in only a 5.44% total chip area increase and a lower overall overhead than ReDas. Overall, SISA achieves higher performance in most regimes and comparable performance otherwise, while incurring lower area and energy overhead. 

## 5 Conclusion 

In this work we proposed SISA, a new SA architecture based on slabs of PEs which is able to achieve higher speedup and better EDP than SOTA flexible and monolithic SA architectures for operations with highly skewed matrices. This design is particularly effective for small- to medium-batch decoding, the most common setting in interactive applications, and for latency-sensitive prefill like in chatbot workloads with short prompts (median ≈12 tokens). With a minimal area overhead over the baseline TPUv4, SISA delivers up to 8.52 × speedup and 93% EDP reduction compared to that baseline, while in the worst case achieving comparable performance with only 8.47% higher EDP. When compared to the best alternative SOTA, SISA performs better in most cases, achieving up to 

2.61 × speedup while also exhibiting lower area overhead.
