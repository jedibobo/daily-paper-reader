Abstract 

KV-cache quantization is framed as a quality–latency trade-off. We show it is inverted on Apple Silicon’s unified memory: a single fused Metal kernel (sign-randomized FFT + per-channel λ +per-group abs-max + int4 nibble pack), exposed as a HuggingFace Cache subclass, runs faster than fp16 across 256–4096-token prefixes on Gemma-3 1B ( −3 to −8% ms/tok) and at short context on Qwen2.5-1.5B ( −0.7 to −2.6% through 1K), with 3 × persistent memory compres-sion and quality preserved (∆PPL = 0 .000 Qwen short-prompt; +3 .6 hook ∆PPL Gemma). The kernel’s ∼ 25 ns/vec overhead is below the bandwidth savings from 3 × compression. The fused kernel also closes Qwen’s 4-bit per-token catastrophe (∆PPL = +7975 → +638 .6, 12 .5×

reduction) at 182 GFLOPS / D=128. Supporting findings: SRFT and SRHT are statistically indistinguishable for KV quality (we pick SRFT for mixed-radix and matrix-multiply align-ment); a learned-rotation ablation surfaces a regularization role for the fixed random SRFT base (learning R + λ without SRFT lowers calibration MSE 84 .9% vs 50 .3% but yields worse PPL); Householder rotations at k=d/ 2 reflectors are effectively lossless at d=256. 

# 1 Introduction 

Long-context transformer inference is KV-cache bound: K/V tensors accumulated across decode steps dominate memory footprint and bandwidth [12]. For Llama-3-70B [14] at 128K context the fp16 cache is 41 GB — larger than the model. The standard fix is rotate-then-quantize (QuaRot [7], SpinQuant [21], TurboQuant [1]): apply a random orthogonal R to Gaussianize coordinates, quantize uniformly, store the int4/int8 result, invert R on read. Production deployments report 5–20% decode-latency overhead in exchange for the memory savings.

Three design decisions support the deployment, each with an independent finding. First, we adopt sign-randomized Fourier (SRFT = D · F ) over Hadamard for mixed-radix flexibility (non-power-of-two d) and 16 ×16 matrix-multiply alignment (AMX, tensor cores); the choice is not load-bearing for quality (SRFT ≡ SRHT within seed variance across all five models tested). QuIP# [25] made the same hardware-portability argument for weight quantization; we extend it to KV cache. Second, at Qwen d=128 where per-token scaling fails (∆PPL = +7975), per-channel + per-group quantization (cf. KIVI [22]) recovers most of the damage. We realize this in a single fused dispatch reaching +638 .6 ∆PPL (12 .5× reduction) at 24 .6 ns/vec / 182 GFLOPS, +11 .7% over unscaled g32. Third, a post-training learned-rotation ablation finds that learning R + λ without the SRFT base reduces calibration MSE further (84 .9% vs 50 .3%) but yields worse PPL — a separation not isolated in prior learned-rotation work [21, 29]. Householder rotations at k=d/ 2 reflectors match full Cayley with half the parameters and are effectively lossless (∆PPL = +0 .000) at d=256 on Gemma-3 1B [16]. The rotated-then-quantized recipe, randomized FFT as rotation, and per-channel + per-group quantization are all prior work. What we add sits on top: a measured negative-cost int4 KV-cache 2deployment on Apple Silicon, the single-dispatch kernel that makes it work, and two algorithmic findings (calibration-MSE/PPL separation, Householder lossless at d=256).

# 2 Background and Related Work 

Weight quantization. GPTQ [15] and AWQ [19] compress weights to 3–4 bits; LLM.int8() [13] introduced outlier-aware decomposition for int8 inference. These methods are orthogonal to KV-cache quantization (offline weights vs online activations). 

Rotation-based weight quantization. QuIP [11] established incoherence processing via random orthogonal rotations as a precondition for aggressive low-bit weights; QuIP# [25] extended to randomized Hadamard, and — directly relevant here — introduced a randomized FFT variant for cases where Hadamard’s power-of-two factorization does not apply, reporting Llama-2 PPL within ∼ 0.1 of Hadamard. QuIP#’s stated motivation anticipates ours: “the FFT itself is also well supported on a wide variety of hardware, meaning that it may be easier to implement a fast RFFT when adapting QuIP# to new hardware.” QuaRot [7] fuses Hadamard rotations into adjacent linear layers; SpinQuant [21] learns Cayley-parameterized rotations via a small calibration set; ButterflyQuant [29] parameterizes butterflies by learnable Givens angles and reports W2A16 Llama-2-7B at 15.4 PPL vs QuaRot’s 22.1; DartQuant [24] and KurTail [3] reduce calibration cost. 

Rotation-based KV-cache quantization. TurboQuant [1] pairs Hadamard with a 1-bit Quantized-Johnson–Lindenstrauss residual to remove inner-product bias. KIVI [22] uses asymmet-ric 2-bit KV with per-channel/per-token scaling and a recent-token fp16 residual — orthogonal to rotation choice and composable with our approach. RotorQuant [23] argues against butterflies in favor of O(d) block-diagonal Givens / quaternion rotations. FourierAttention [20] compresses dimension- selective KV via deterministic Fourier projection (not rotate-then-quantize). FPTQuant [26] jointly learns per-head/per-token transforms over weights, activations, and KV. 

Randomized Fourier transforms. The Subsampled Randomized Fourier Transform has a long history in randomized numerical linear algebra [28, 17, 2]. Most LLM-quantization papers opt for Hadamard for operational convenience; QuIP#’s RFFT is the one exception and frames it as a hardware-portability fallback rather than a first-class method. 43 Method

3.2 Fused quantization kernel 

The four-step pipeline rotate → quantize → store → (on read) dequantize → inverse-rotate exhibits significant kernel dispatch overhead when implemented as separate primitives — on PyTorch MPS the eager pipeline runs at ∼ 400 ns per 64-dim vector, dispatch-dominated. We fuse all four steps for the forward half into a single Metal compute kernel, and symmetrically for the inverse half: 1. Load d fp32 values per vector with coalesced global access; apply the sign diagonal in-register via integer-byte multiply. 2. Run log 2(d/ 2) radix-2 Stockham butterfly passes (5 for d=64, 6 for d=128) on the complex length-d/ 2 FFT; Hermitian-unpack to the Rd packed representation in Equation 2. We use radix-2 rather than the higher-radix Stockham of [9] (which reaches 138 GFLOPS on the same hardware for general-purpose length-4096 FFT) because at d ∈ { 64 , 128 , 256 } the higher-radix variants do not amortize their twiddle-table cost. 53. SIMD-shuffle reduction over the vector to find the abs-max; divide by (2 b−1 − 1) to produce the per-vector scale. 4. Round each packed value to signed int {4, 8} and write packed output: one byte per value for int8, two nibbles per byte for int4, plus the fp32 scale as ( . . . , d/ 2) or ( . . . , d ) bytes + one fp32. Two correctness details that took tuning: (i) threadgroup barriers must be at uniform control flow . An earlier draft deadlocked because barriers inside divergent if (active) branches became distinct barriers across lanes. We hoist every barrier to a point reached by all 128 threads uncon-ditionally, and gate only memory reads/writes by the per-lane predicate. (ii) nibble packing uses SIMD-shuffle-xor to co-locate odd/even neighbors in the same lane before the store, avoiding a threadgroup-memory round-trip: pack two nibbles as byte = (q[2i+1] << 4) | (q[2i] & 0xF) ,assembled via simd shuffle xor(q, 1) .

3.3 Integration into PyTorch attention 

We expose the kernel to PyTorch via torch.mps.compile shader , which compiles the MSL source at process start and binds each kernel as a callable that accepts MPS tensors plus scalar uni-forms. A 40-line Python wrapper ( SRFTMetal(d=64 | 128, bits=4 | 8) ) exposes forward quant ,

inverse dequant , and a convenience round trip . A KV-cache simulation forward-hook on each attention layer’s k proj and v proj routes through this wrapper, allowing drop-in comparison against the eager PyTorch SRFT implementation on the same model. Bit-for-bit cross-validation against the reference Python implementation (signs seeded identically) yields 99 .997–100 .000% int value agreement depending on bit width and head dimension; the disagreements are off-by-one ties at quantization bin boundaries.

5.1 Method 

We add two post-training learnable components on top of the fixed SRFT pipeline ( §3): 1. Per-coordinate scale λ ∈ Rd>0: applied element-wise after the SRFT butterfly and before quantization. The inverse path divides by λ (clamped at 10 −6). The whole pipeline is orthogonal up to this diagonal rescaling, which lets different output coordinates use wider or narrower quantization bins as their empirical distributions warrant. 2. Cayley-parameterized orthogonal rotation R ∈ O(d): applied in between SRFT and 

λ, parameterized as R = eA for skew-symmetric A = U − U ⊤ with U ∈ Rd×d trainable. The matrix exponential is an exact Lie-algebra map onto O(d), more numerically stable than the classical Cayley formula and compatible with autograd on device (we compute the small matrix exponential on CPU and move the result to GPU memory). Both are trained by 200–300 Adam steps minimizing the reconstruction MSE ∥ˆx − x∥2 over a batch of K/V activations collected from a small held-out prompt stream. Calibration is per layer per channel (K and V fit separately), and takes 1–5 min on MPS for the full SmolLM2-360M. Storage overhead is d fp32 scalars per channel for the scale variant and d2 for the Cayley variant.

5.3 Ablation: the SRFT base is not redundant 

The most informative row in Table 3 is the fourth: a learned Cayley rotation without the SRFT preamble. It drives calibration MSE down by 84 .9% — far more than the SRFT+Cayley variant’s 50 .3% — because the learned R is free to absorb any orientation the SRFT had chosen and then further adapt. Yet its downstream ∆PPL is worse by +0 .017. We read this as: calibration MSE is not a sufficient proxy for attention-level quality. The fixed random SRFT base contributes something beyond MSE minimization — plausibly a regularizing prior on R (the Gaussianization from SRFT is already done, so R only needs to learn fine refine-ment), or a better optimization landscape (SRFT’s random structure places the learned rotation in a basin closer to the PPL-optimal solution than a generic from-scratch learned rotation reaches by gradient descent on MSE alone). Both readings are testable with additional compute; we report the phenomenon as observed.

# 6 A Complementary CPU Pathway via AMX 

Apple’s AMX matrix coprocessor [10, 18] reaches ∼ 350 GFLOPS FP32 single-core peak via 

cblas sgemm dispatched by the Accelerate BLAS [5]. We test whether batched length-8 com-plex DFTs — expressible as 16 × 16 real matrix multiplies — route to AMX through this path, and report a more nuanced answer. Figure 5 shows the result. The key finding is that data layout matters more than tile size for AMX dispatch via cblas sgemm . Col-batched ( M =16 , N =B, K =16) reaches ∼ 160 raw GFLOPS at B=1024 — 45% of the documented 350 GFLOPS single-core AMX peak, and 3 .4× faster than Apple’s tuned vDSP fft zop at the same batch. Row-batched ( M =B, N =K=16) plateaus at 13 ∼ 70 GFLOPS, consistent with a NEON fallback: the N =K=16 inner dimensions do not meet Accelerate’s AMX-dispatch heuristic. A plain-square SGEMM sanity sweep confirms AMX is reachable on this hardware (338 GFLOPS at N =128, 1223 GFLOPS at N =1024), so the dispatch is not a chip-generation limitation. The heuristic requires inner dimensions larger than the raw tile size; a batched DFT shaped (1 , B ) ⊗ (16 , 16) satisfies this only in one of its two layouts. 

Fused CPU-side SRFT+quantize. Building on this discovery we implemented a pure-CPU fused pipeline that applies the sign diagonal and packing with NEON and uses Accelerate

∼ 350 GFLOPS single-core AMX peak) with a round-trip error of 0 .027, matching the Python reference. The CPU path is 3–5 × slower than the Metal kernel but 8 × faster than eager PyTorch MPS, and may be preferable for small-batch latency-sensitive regimes where GPU dispatch overhead dominates.

# 7 End-to-end Deployment 

The kernel work in Sections 3–6 delivers the fused SRFT+int4 quantization step. To turn this into a usable deployment path we (i) extend the kernel to a fused per-channel λ + per-group abs-max 

variant that closes the 4-bit catastrophe at d = 128 and d = 256, and (ii) plumb the result through HuggingFace’s Cache interface so that model.generate runs unchanged. This section reports the resulting end-to-end latency, memory, and quality. 

7.1 The fused scaled-g32 kernel 

Section 3 presented two independent kernel variants: the scaled variant ( λ multiply post-FFT, single per-token abs-max) and the g32 variant (no λ, 4 per-group abs-max scales per vector at 

g = 32). Section 5 showed that neither alone closes the Qwen 4-bit catastrophe: λ alone leaves ∆PPL = +5400 from outlier-dominated dynamic range, and per-group g32 alone leaves +4520 because the per-group scales are still set by post-rotation outlier coordinates. The mathematical 14 recipe in the Python reference is to combine both: rescale by per-channel max, then quantize per-group on the rescaled values. We fuse this directly into Metal as   

> srft quant forward scaled int4 d128 g32 (item 16)
> srft quant forward scaled int4 d256 g32 (item 21)

with matching inverses. Both kernels: load input, do length-d/ 2 FFT + Hermitian unpack, mul-tiply post-FFT real coefficients by an externally-supplied λ ∈ Rd, perform d/g per-group simd max 

reductions, quantize each group with its own abs-max, and nibble-pack the int4 output. With 

λd = 1 /per channel max(SRFT-output) d the result is the GPU equivalent of the Python refer-ence’s per channel group quantizer.

7.2 SRFTInt4Cache : HuggingFace-native deployment 

We package the fused kernel as a HuggingFace Cache subclass ( srft_int4_cache.py ). The sub-class: (i) physically stores K/V at int4 + 8 fp32 scales between decode steps (3 .2× theoretical compression at d=128, g=32; 3 .0× measured); (ii) accepts a per-layer λ map at construction; (iii) 15 maintains a fp16 residual window of recent tokens, re-quantized when full; (iv) caches the dequan-tized prefix across decode steps and invalidates on residual flush, so each update() call is O(1) in prefix length rather than O(prefix len) — a deferred-engineering fix that flips Qwen at 1024-prefix from +2 .4% slower to −0.7% faster than fp16.

Why the choice of rotation reduces to hardware fit. Our main finding is that SRFT and SRHT are statistically indistinguishable at the quality level that matters for KV-cache quantization. Both achieve the same Gaussianization effect by the same underlying mechanism (concentration of measure on a random orthogonal map). The choice between them is therefore an operational one. On Apple Silicon, the FFT’s radix-8 butterfly structure matches both the Metal SIMD-shuffle pattern (at D=128 we saturate the 32-lane group per pass, vs 16 for Hadamard) and the AMX tile size (a 16 × 16 real matrix multiply is one radix-8 DFT in disguise). On commodity NVIDIA GPUs, tensor cores expose 16 × 16 FP16 / BF16 matrix multiplies that the same mapping applies to. Hadamard is fast but purely radix-2; SRFT is fast and map-preserving. 

Why per-token scaling. We use per-token (one scale per head-dim vector) throughout. Per-tensor scaling (one scale per layer) fails at 4-bit on SmolLM2-1.7B regardless of rotation because a single scale cannot cover the dynamic range of a 2048-dim layer; the comparison between rotations is swamped by seed variance. Per-token scaling is also the choice made by production KV-cache quantization systems like TurboQuant. 

Sign diagonal choice. We use a single ±1 diagonal ( D · F ) rather than the full D1 · F · D2

SRFT form. The second diagonal is needed when the transform is used as a distance-preserving projection under worst-case adversarial inputs; not relevant for learned LLM activations. 

Residual window. A 16-token fp16 residual window is empirically optimal for our cache. Dropping to 4 wins ≤ 0.01 × more compression while costing ∼ 5% latency (re-quantization fires more often); raising to 32 pushes the memory ratio below 3 ×.

# 9 Conclusion 

Int4 KV-cache compression on Apple Silicon is a strict throughput gain, not a quality–latency trade-off. Across 256 to 4096-token prefixes on Gemma-3 1B, our SRFTInt4Cache runs −3 to −8% ms/tok vs fp16; on Qwen2.5-1.5B at ≤ 1K prefix, −0.7 to −2.6%. Quality is preserved (∆PPL = 0 .000 Qwen short-prompt deployment; +3 .6 hook ∆PPL Gemma). The mechanism is bandwidth-driven and specific to unified-memory architectures: 3 × compressed cache transfers 3 × less per decode step, exceeding the kernel’s ∼ 25 ns/vec overhead. The conventional trade-off is recovered on GPUs with separate HBM and large dispatch overheads. The deployment is one fused Metal kernel (SRFT + per-channel λ + per-group abs-max + int4 nibble pack) exposed as a HuggingFace Cache subclass. Independent findings supporting it: sign-randomized Fourier ≡ sign-randomized Hadamard for quality across five models tested, with FFT extending to non-power-of-two d and matrix-multiply alignment; calibration-MSE and downstream PPL are not equivalent optimization targets, with the fixed random SRFT base acting as a useful regularizer; Householder rotations at k=d/ 2 reflectors match full Cayley with half the parameters and are effectively lossless at d=256; a complementary AMX CPU pathway reaches 91% of single-core peak for small-batch regimes. 17 Reproducibility. Code, paper sources, and raw results: github.com/aminems/ AppleSiliconFFT . The release ships a single Metal source file containing all kernel variants (d ∈ { 64 , 128 , 256 }; int4 / int8; unscaled / scaled λ / per-group g=32; plus the fused scaled g32 deployment kernel), a Python bridge wrapping torch.mps.compile shader , the Hugging-Face Cache subclass, an end-to-end model.generate bench (flags --inner group size 32 --calibrate lambdas select the fused path), and a throughput microbench.
