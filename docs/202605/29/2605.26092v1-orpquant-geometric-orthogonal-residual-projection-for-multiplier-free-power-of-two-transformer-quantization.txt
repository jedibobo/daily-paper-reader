# ABSTRACT 

The deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) and Vision Transformers (ViTs) on edge devices is significantly constrained by memory limitations and the critical timing bottlenecks introduced by dense Multiply-Accumulate (MAC) arrays. In the ultra-low bit regime, logarithmic Power-of-Two (PoT) quantization provides a hardware-efficient alternative by replacing MAC operations with bit-shifts. However, the non-uniform exponential lattice is inherently limited by a Low Angular Resolution Regime , a structural flaw that becomes particularly pronounced at sub-4-bit thresholds, leading to a notable degradation of high-dimensional feature manifolds. To address this geometric limitation, we propose Orthogonal Residual Projection (ORP), an algorithm-hardware co-design framework. By formulating quantization as a dual-basis geometric projection, ORP adaptively synthesizes a higher-resolution residual lattice using strictly shift-and-add opera-tions. Furthermore, ORP’s analytical solver offers a practical alternative to computationally intensive gradient-based optimization, reducing the full-model calibration time for LLaMA-2-7B to approxi-mately 15 minutes .Extensive evaluations demonstrate ORP’s applicability across modalities and its hardware efficiency. Under the 3-bit (W3/A16) constraint, ORP achieves a perplexity of 6.10 on LLaMA-2-7B, comparing favorably to conventional MAC-intensive baselines like AWQ without relying on asymmetric scaling, while maintaining competitive accuracy in 4-bit scenarios. At the silicon level, standard-cell RTL synthesis at a 28nm node indicates that ORP effectively mitigates the timing bottlenecks associated with dense multiplier trees. By flattening the combinational logic depth, our parallel shift-and-add datapath reduces the critical path delay to 0.35 ns . This theoretically allows the accelerator to operate at 2.85 GHz , suggesting an agile, multiplier-free execution paradigm for high-throughput edge intelligence.

# 1 Introduction 

The deployment of large language models (LLMs) and vision transformers (ViTs) on edge devices is frequently constrained by the multiplier tax —the critical combinational logic depth and routing complexity associated with hardware multiply-accumulate (MAC) units. In modern silicon processes, a 16-bit floating-point multiplier consumes an order of magnitude more energy and introduces significantly deeper logic paths than simple addition or bit-wise operations. To advance high-throughput edge computing, there is a growing motivation to transition from computation-heavy, multiplication-centric architectures to multiplier-free paradigms. Despite its hardware advantages, low-bit quantization often faces challenges regarding precision. Conventional approaches typically attribute this performance degradation to a scalar distribution mismatch between the discrete lattice and the Gaussian statistics of neural weights. However, this scalar-centric perspective often overlooks the high-dimensional nature of Transformer operations[ 1]. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the inner product is essentially the projection of a continuous weight vector onto a discrete lattice manifold. In high-dimensional spaces, the fidelity of this projection depends not just on magnitude, but critically on the preservation of the vector’s direction, which dictates the alignment of latent features. While current methods effectively manage the scaling factor ( Scaler ), directional integrity can remain unpredictable, contributing significantly to performance degradation in extreme quantization. When applying this geometric perspective to the hardware-friendly PoT domain, a key limitation emerges: its inherently non-uniform angular resolution. Unlike linear lattices, the angular distribution of PoT basis vectors is governed by the 

arctan(2 k) function. Consequently, it operates within a low angular resolution regime , as shown in Fig. 2, which is particularly pronounced around the diagonal regions of the projection space. This structural characteristic creates an 

Angular Gap , where the quantized vector’s orientation can deviate enough to substantially compromise the semantic integrity of the feature manifold. To address the angular degradation associated with a 1-D PoT mapping, we introduce the Orthogonal Residual Projection (ORP) framework. Rather than mapping continuous high-dimensional vectors onto a single discrete basis (b1), ORP constructs a 2-D orthogonal subspace to correct the angular distortion mathematically. By deriving a secondary basis ( b2) that is orthogonal to the primary projection ( ⟨b1, b2⟩ = 0 ), the framework captures the orthogonal residual—directional information often lost during the initial 1-D mapping. This dual-basis projection helps mitigate the Low Angular Resolution Regime and preserves the spatial orientation of the feature manifold, while remaining compatible with the zero-MAC hardware constraints of the PoT domain. To implement this projection efficiently while maintaining representational fidelity, ORP utilizes a decoupled, three-stage pipeline. First, a preconditioned manifold smoothing mechanism is applied to adjust the weight topology. Second, a geometric residual matching process determines the discrete orthogonal basis ( b2) in Euclidean space. By isolating this discrete search from activation data, the method enables an O(N ) basis selection that reduces susceptibility to calibration bias. Finally, a data-driven joint scale optimization incorporates calibration data to compute the continuous coefficients ( c1 and c2). This stage evaluates channel-wise importance based on empirical input features, dynamically prioritizing the projection fidelity of critical feature dimensions according to their activation magnitudes. # 2 Related Work 

The deployment of large-scale foundation models on resource-constrained edge platforms has driven extensive research into neural network quantization [ 2, 3]. While significant progress has been achieved in compressing model weights, simultaneously maintaining ultra-low bit-width accuracy and mitigating computationally heavy hardware operations remains a notable challenge. In this section, we review the prior literature across three pivotal domains. First, we examine the evolution of Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) and the hardware implications of reconstruction-heavy paradigms. Second, we analyze recent advancements in high-dimensional rotational quantization, highlighting the trade-off between geometric angular resolution and on-chip computational overhead. Finally, we discuss the trajectory of multiplier-free architectures, examining the representational limitations of scalar logarithmic quantization and demonstrating how our proposed framework aims to address these challenges. 32.1 Post-Training Quantization and the Reconstruction Bottleneck 

Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) has emerged as a fundamental technique for deploying large-scale vision transformers (ViTs) and Large Language Models (LLMs), primarily due to its circumvention of the substantial computational costs associated with retraining. Early PTQ methods predominantly focused on scalar rounding mechanisms and optimal clipping ranges [ 4, 5]. However, to extend quantization into the ultra-low bit-width regime ( ≤ 4-bit), recent state-of-the-art frameworks have increasingly shifted towards block-wise error reconstruction. In the vision domain, methods such as BRECQ, QDrop, and AIQViT aim to maintain accuracy by iteratively compensating for quantization perturbations using gradient-based optimization [ 6, 7, 8]. Similarly, in the LLM domain, methodologies mitigate quantization errors by exploiting second-order Hessian information to minimize local reconstruction loss [ 3], or by applying activation-aware scaling to protect highly salient outlier weights [4]. Despite their empirical success in significantly reducing memory bandwidth—an important advancement for LLM deployment—these reconstruction-heavy paradigms often encounter two distinct bottlenecks. First, the stochastic iterative grid search required during calibration can incur substantial time costs, often taking hours per model. Second, from a hardware architecture perspective, they predominantly operate as weight-only compression rather than full datapath quantization. Because they generally necessitate runtime dequantization to higher-precision formats (e.g., FP16) before executing the matrix multiplication, they successfully alleviate the memory wall but do not fully eliminate the timing bottlenecks of dense Multiply-Accumulate (MAC) units on silicon. To address these limitations, our ORP framework provides an analytical solver that operates natively in the quantized domain, facilitating multiplier-free hardware compatibility without the overhead of iterative searches. 

2.2 High-Dimensional Geometry and Rotational Quantization 

As traditional 1D scalar quantization approaches practical limits around the 3-bit threshold, recent literature has explored high-dimensional geometric spaces. A prominent trend involves using orthogonal rotations to precondition the weight and activation manifolds prior to discretization. Three notable methodologies represent the state-of-the-art in this rotational paradigm. First, the QuIP family establishes theoretical bounds for sub-3-bit fidelity by utilizing fixed Randomized Hadamard Transforms (RHT) to ensure error incoherence, coupling this with advanced vector projection onto the dense E8 lattice [ 9, 10 ]. Extending this concept, QuaRot applies Hadamard rotations holistically to both weights and activations, smoothing outliers to facilitate uniform low-bit quantization[ 11 ]. Advancing beyond fixed transforms, SpinQuant introduces parameterized orthogonal matrices, actively rotating the feature spaces via gradient optimization[12]. Although mathematically elegant, the paradigm of explicitly rotating data to fit an optimal lattice introduces hardware considerations. While high-performance cloud accelerators may absorb this complexity, applying these dense orthogonal matrices at runtime often requires deep addition trees and non-local memory routing (e.g., butterfly networks). This can exacerbate on-chip wire routing congestion and vector decoding latency. Such architectural complexities present challenges for the minimalist hardware philosophy required for edge computing. This architectural bottleneck motivates the exploration of purely multiplier-free and transform-free networks, which we discuss next. 

2.3 Multiplier-Free Architectures and Logarithmic Quantization 

To reduce both the MAC unit reliance of standard PTQ and the transform overheads of rotational methods, architectural focus has increasingly shifted towards multiplier-free networks. This philosophy often manifests through ternary representations or PoT quantization, replacing matrix multiplications with shift-and-add logic. Prominent examples include the BitNet architecture for models trained from scratch [ 13 ], ShiftAddLLM for retrofitting pre-trained LLMs [14], and RepQ-ViT [5], which employs a post-training per-channel PoT quantization scheme. However, achieving multiplier-free inference without compromising model utility remains an open challenge, as existing methodologies exhibit operational trade-offs. While BitNet demonstrates the viability of ternary weights, it typically requires resource-intensive from-scratch training paradigms, and matching full-precision performance at equivalent parameter scales remains an active area of exploration. Conversely, post-training methods like ShiftAddLLM rely on bit-level operational substitutions, effectively replacing standard multiplications with an expanded volume of bit-sliced additions. While arithmetically valid, this increases the accumulation workload and frequently requires layer-wise optimization. Finally, while shift-add frameworks like RepQ-ViT are effective in vision tasks, they often face challenges generalizing to LLMs. LLM fidelity relies on preserving significant activation outliers, a requirement that rigid per-channel scalar formats can struggle to accommodate, leading to notable accuracy degradation under sub-4-bit constraints. 4Crucially, independent scalar rounding often does not fully account for the non-uniform angular distribution of the PoT metric space, leading to the Low Angular Resolution Regime and substantial Angular Gaps . Here, ORP introduces an alternative paradigm. Rather than explicitly rotating the high-dimensional data at runtime or relying on expanded bit-slice additions (as in ShiftAddLLM), ORP leaves the data stationary and adaptively constructs the local lattice .By leveraging the O(N ) Strided Dual-Exchange Theorem, ORP captures critical angular fidelity in-place via a 2D orthogonal residual basis. This fine-grained geometric repair achieves competitive representational fidelity compared to state-of-the-art rotational methods, providing a robust prerequisite for viable sub-4-bit multiplier-free deployment. 

# 3 Methodology 

In this section, we detail the theoretical formulation and execution pipeline of the ORP framework. Diverging from traditional 1-D scalar quantization, ORP conceptualizes quantization error fundamentally as a geometric angular deviation. Rather than attempting to compensate for this error through conventional scaling, ORP addresses the deviation by capturing the residual within a 2-D orthogonal subspace, constrained by a multiplier-free, Power-of-Two (PoT) hardware paradigm. To promote both representational fidelity and algorithmic efficiency, the framework decouples the projection process into three distinct stages: manifold-aligned preconditioning, geometric discrete basis search, and data-driven joint scale optimization. 

3.1 Overall Framework of ORP 

Traditional Power-of-Two (PoT) quantization projects a weight vector w ∈ Rd onto a 1D discrete lattice S = {0, ±2k |

k ∈ Z} as ˆw = c1b1. However, this 1D projection encounters limitations related to the Low Angular Resolution Regime due to the non-uniform angular distribution inherent in the PoT domain. ORP addresses this by employing a Gram-Schmidt-like expansion within the discrete lattice. The objective is to find a secondary basis b2 ∈ S d and continuous scales c1, c 2 such that ˆw = c1b1 + c2b2, subject to the orthogonality constraint b1 ⊥ b2.

3.3 Primary Projection and Orthogonal Residual Extraction 

To effectively utilize the representational capacity of an ultra-low bit hardware budget (e.g., 3-bit or 4-bit), ORP transi-tions from a conventional symmetric projection to a hardware-aware, asymmetric logarithmic lattice. A requirement for energy-proportional edge inference is the existence of an absolute zero-state, which is essential to trigger hardware zero-skipping and clock gating. For instance, at the 3-bit threshold (i.e., exactly 23 = 8 discrete physical states), instead of relying on external sparsity masks, our quantization function Q(·) explicitly breaks spatial symmetry by repurposing the smallest positive quantization bin to represent the absolute zero. 3.4 Hierarchical Strided Dual-Exchange and Orthogonal Residual Projection 

A key challenge in extreme low-bit quantization is balancing the memory overhead of scaling factors with the geometric degrees of freedom required for angular fitting. to limit memory footprints. Conversely, the generation of the secondary orthogonal basis b2 is executed at a finer micro-block granularity of G = 32 (denoted as blocks a, b, c, d ). # 4 Result 

To systematically validate the ORP framework, we present an empirical evaluation structured around four core dimensions. Second, we examine the relationship between algorithmic fidelity and physical deployment through an Edge NLP algorithm-hardware co-evaluation, integrating LLaMA-2 calibration efficiency with 28nm RTL synthesis metrics. Finally, we explore the framework’s deployment flexibility and resilience to calibration bias through zero-shot reasoning tasks, comparing the data-free GEO and REF paradigms.

4.2 Edge NLP Inference and Hardware Co-Evaluation 

To explore the practical deployment potential of the ORP framework in resource-constrained edge scenarios, we present an algorithm-hardware co-evaluation. Table 2 provides a comprehensive overview, integrating predictive perplexity, software-level calibration time, and RTL-synthesized hardware metrics at a 28nm technology node. Figure 6 illustrates the architectural distinctions between our approach and traditional baselines. Conventional quantiza-tion methods (e.g., AWQ, SpinQuant) typically map their execution onto traditional Multiply-Accumulate (MAC) units. A notable characteristic in digital VLSI design is that at ultra-low bit-widths (e.g., 3-bit or 4-bit), the silicon area of a standard MAC unit does not significantly exceed that of a shift-adder, as the multiplier logic scales down. However, the primary hardware bottleneck in these extreme low-bit regimes shifts from area to combinational logic depth . Even a low-bit multiplier necessitates an internal adder tree, which introduces unavoidable routing and propagation delays. By leveraging the Power-of-Two (PoT) nature of our orthogonal residuals, the ORP datapath bypasses this multiplication tree. The computation is physically instantiated as parallel bit-shifters, effectively flattening the logic depth. This flattened architecture translates into tangible timing improvements. As detailed in Table 2, under sub-4-bit configurations, the ORP hardware unit achieves a consistent critical path delay of 0.35 ns . Coupled with these hardware characteristics, ORP demonstrates practical advantages in software deployment, particu-larly regarding calibration efficiency. Our ORP implementation relies entirely on vanilla PyTorch operators without any custom CUDA optimizations. 4.3 The Geometric Dividend of Dual-Basis Reconstruction 

To empirically validate the architectural design of the proposed method, we conduct a targeted ablation study isolating the basis dimension ( K) across distinct grid topologies (PoT versus Linear). As illustrated in Table 3, transitioning from a standard single-basis projection ( K = 1 ) to our dual orthogonal basis ( K = 2 ) yields notable geometric benefits, particularly in aggressively quantized regimes. Clear evidence of ORP’s efficacy emerges under the 3-bit Log 2 configuration. Constrained by the exponential sparsity of its representational manifold, the single-basis Log 2 projection experiences a notable angular degradation, resulting in a perplexity of 6.760 on the 7B model. However, the introduction of the orthogonal residual dimension ( b2) effectively corrects this spatial distortion, reducing the perplexity to 6.100—a substantial improvement of ∆0.66 without expanding the primary bit-width datapath. Crucially, this ablation uncovers an important hardware-algorithm synergy. Our dual-basis Log 2 formulation at 3-bit (PPL 6.100) outperforms the conventional single-basis Linear formulation (PPL 6.670). This demonstrates that ORP equips the multiplier-free shift-and-add paradigm with sufficient representational capacity to exceed standard compute-heavy linear quantization. Furthermore, the empirical data corroborates our geometric thesis regarding grid saturation. While the K = 2 expansion delivers substantial improvements at 3-bit, the relative improvement naturally narrows at 4-bit (e.g., from 5.98 to 5.78 for Log 2 7B). This scaling behavior indicates that ORP’s two-dimensional spatial correction provides the greatest impact precisely where hardware constraints and manifold sparsity are most severe. 

4.4 Zero-Shot Reasoning and The Dual-Track Deployment Paradigm 

Table 4 evaluates the ORP framework on LLaMA-2 under extreme low-bit configurations, reporting WikiText-2 (WK) perplexity and zero-shot reasoning accuracy (HellaSwag, ARC-C, WinoGrande). A notable highlight is ORP’s 11 calibration efficiency . Unlike search-based methods requiring hours, our analytical solver completes full-model calibration in approximately 11.9–15.1 minutes on a single workstation. The data-free Geometric-Only (GEO) mode serves as a strong baseline, maintaining 67.72% on WG even at 3-bit (W3/A16). Conversely, the Activation-Refined (REF) mode uses a small calibration set to suppress outliers, consistently delivering improved overall accuracy . For L2-7B at 3-bit, REF reduces WK perplexity from 6.83 to 6.10, demonstrating ORP’s practical flexibility between instant instantiation and data-driven precision. The 13B REF model (W4/A16) achieves 5.09 perplexity and 58.91% on HellaSwag, indicating that multiplier-free PoT operations can support large-scale inference while maintaining representational fidelity. Furthermore, the 4-bit configurations highlight an interesting dynamic regarding domain generalization . While the 13B REF mode achieves lower in-domain perplexity (5.09 vs. 5.14), it is marginally outperformed by the data-free GEO mode on zero-shot tasks like WG (71.27% vs. 72.14%). This observation aligns with the Calibration Bias inherent to data-driven PTQ: REF trades slight out-of-domain generalization to absorb specific calibration outliers. In contrast, GEO relies strictly on static geometric weight manifolds, making it inherently resistant to dataset overfitting and providing a highly reliable baseline for diverse reasoning tasks. 

# 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we propose the Orthogonal Residual Projection (ORP) framework, an algorithm-hardware co-design approach for ultra-low-bit model quantization. By diverging from traditional Multiply-Accumulate (MAC) dependent execution, ORP formulates quantization as a dual-basis geometric projection, enabling the mapping of both Large Language Models (LLaMA-2) and Vision Transformers (ViTs) onto a multiplier-free, Power-of-Two (PoT) datapath. Our empirical evaluation indicates that ORP achieves a favorable Pareto frontier for edge intelligence. Algorithmically, the analytical solver facilitates full-model calibration in approximately 15 minutes, reducing the computational overhead typically associated with contemporary search-based methods. Furthermore, the decoupling of the Geometric-Only (GEO) and Activation-Refined (REF) modes provides a practical perspective on calibration bias, offering practitioners a flexible choice between data-free zero-shot reasoning and data-driven precision. At the hardware level, the shift-and-add architecture reduces the combinational logic depth inherent to dense multipliers. Synthesized at a 28nm technology node, the ORP accelerator attains a critical path delay of 0.35 ns, supporting a theoretical maximum clock frequency of approximately 2.85 GHz. Additionally, under an iso-frequency comparison at 1.8 GHz, it delivers an energy efficiency of 3.33 TOPS/W under sub-4-bit constraints. Ultimately, ORP demonstrates that extreme quantization can be realized effectively without relying on asymmetric scaling, runtime dequantization, or deep multiplier trees. By aligning geometric principles with silicon-level timing efficiency, ORP offers a viable path toward the agile deployment of large foundation models on resource-constrained edge devices.
