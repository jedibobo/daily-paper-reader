Abstract 

Hyperdimensional computing (HDC) is a promising approach for energy-efficient edge machine learning (ML), where low latency, low power, and tight memory budgets are essential. However, tra-ditional HDC relies on symbolic binding and pseudo-random high-dimensional vectors, which require large dimensionality and heuris-tic updates to reach competitive accuracy, limiting deployment on edge hardware. We introduce XL-HD , a deterministic, projection-based, fully learnable HDC framework tailored for in-memory ac-celeration within edge computing systems. The method uses a fixed Sobol sequence to project binary inputs, extending learning beyond conventional HDC. During training, class prototypes are optimized in real-valued space and later binarized, enabling an en-tirely binary dot-product inference pipeline ideal for IMC hardware such as ReRAM crossbars. XL-HD achieves competitive accuracy on MNIST, UCIHAR, and ISOLET while maintaining a compact IMC-based inference engine with 0.395 mm 2 area and only 0.40 𝜇 Jper single-cycle inference.

1 Introduction 

With edge devices rapidly proliferating, machine learning (ML) frameworks require lightweight, fast, and energy-efficient process-ing to satisfy strict latency, bandwidth, and power limits. However, deploying conventional deep learning models remains challenging due to their high computational and memory demands, motivat-ing the use of in-memory computing (IMC) for massively parallel, energy-efficient processing directly within memory arrays [1]. Hyperdimensional computing (HDC) has emerged as a promis-ing alternative to computationally-intensive ML algorithms [2, 3]. HDC encodes scalar and symbolic data using high-dimensional vectors called hypervectors (HVs). However, generating these HVs – either on the fly or by fetching them from memory – incurs sub-stantial energy and latency costs, especially in resource-limited environments [4]. Improving vector generation and memory ac-cess, therefore, directly enhances baseline HDC performance. Once generated, simple operations (e.g., logic XOR , shifting, and popula-tion count ( POP+ )) complete the encoding [5]. The resulting HVs are naturally noise-robust, fault-tolerant, and effective in low-precision settings, making HDC well suited for edge AI. Despite its potential, HDC faces key limitations for practical edge deployment, as summarized in Fig. 1. 2 Background and Related Work 

In this section, we provide an overview of the principles of HDC and IMC and discuss prior works that integrate HDC into IMC. 

2.1 HDC Principles and Properties 

HDC represents each data point ( symbolic , scalar , or temporal ) by nearly orthogonal HVs that consist of hundreds or even thousands of binary (0,1) or bipolar ( ±1) elements. To generate the symbolic HVs, a random number in [0, 1] is drawn for each of the D di-mensions and compared to 0.5, producing a binary vector with roughly half 1s and half 0s. In contrast, scalar values are encoded by comparing the scalar to the same set of random numbers, re-sulting in correlated HVs for similar scalar values and dissimilar HVs for distant ones. Thus, random numbers play a central role in encoding both symbolic and scalar information, and the quality and characteristics of the randomness source are equally critical [11]. After generating HVs, HDC encoding applies the key operations: 

① binding , ② bundling , and ③ shifting , depending on the applica-tion [12]. Binding combines two HVs into a new composite HV; using element-wise multiplication or XOR operation for bipolar or binary vectors, respectively. Bundling merges multiple HVs into a single robust and holistic representation. Shifting maintains or-thogonality and positional information of symbols by bit-shuffling the HV [5]. Traditional HDC typically generates HVs using pseudo-random sources, which produce near-orthogonal vectors due to random fluctuations and offer limited control over the similarity structure, requiring a larger D to average out the randomness. In contrast, low-discrepancy (LD) sequences such as Sobol are deter-ministic mathematical constructions that distribute samples more uniformly and recurrently over the space, avoiding repeated ran-dom process while providing regular and reproducible deterministic HV patterns. This property can preserve accuracy even at signifi-cantly smaller D [13]. 

2.2 IMC Primitives and Application in HDC

IMC designs can be broadly grouped into three forms: general-purpose IMC arrays, crossbar-based VMM engines, and Content-Addressable Memories (CAMs) for parallel associative search [16, 17, 18]. 3 XL-HD Framework 

To combine deterministic encoding with learnable adaptation, Al-gorithm 1 augments the XL-HD as an HDC framework in which deterministic projection-based encoding and similarity-based classi-fication naturally map onto IMC primitives, enabling a low-latency and energy-efficient inference pipeline. The overall procedure con-sists of three phases: (a) Deterministic HV Encoding , (b) Learnable Class Prototypes , and (c) Inference .

3.1 Learning via Deterministic HV Encoding 

Most existing HDC methods rely on random projection matrices derived from pseudo-random HVs or Johnson–Lindenstrauss (JL) lemma projections [24]. However, they have two main drawbacks. First, the projection matrix is stochastic , so the encoding is not re-producible across different runs. Second, the irregular distribution of pseudo-random vectors often requires larger dimensionality to maintain separability. Instead of sampling a random matrix, we incorporate a fixed projection matrix, P := ΦSobol (𝐷, 𝐹 ) ∈ R𝐷 ×𝐹 , covering high-dimensional spaces evenly [13], and further thresholded at 0.5 to yield a deterministic bipolar encoding in {± 1}.In XL-HD , we encode an input feature vector x ∈ R𝐹 using a simple linear mapping h = Px ; which is the extended forward learning trajectory of the model. 3.2 Learnable Class Prototypes 

After encoding, XL-HD learns a set of trainable class prototypes that represent each class in D space. Each class 𝑘 ∈ { 1, . . . , 𝐾 } is assigned a prototype vector c𝑘 ∈ R𝐷 , initialized by accumulating encoded HVs from samples of the same class c𝑦 𝑖 ← c𝑦 𝑖 + h𝑖 . This creates centroids that summarize the distribution of samples for each class and forms the initial prototype matrix C = [c1; . . . ; c𝐾 ] ∈ 

R𝐾 ×𝐷 . During training, these prototypes are iteratively refined to maximize class separability. For each minibatch, encoded HVs are compared to C using cosine similarity, 

𝑧 𝑖𝑘 = h𝑖 · c𝑘 

∥h𝑖 ∥ ∥ c𝑘 ∥ . (1) The similarity scores are normalized with a softmax function to produce class probabilities and the model is trained using cross-entropy loss L = − Í𝑖 log 𝑝 𝑖𝑦 𝑖 . Then the prototypes are updated through gradient-based optimization. Over training epochs, this process pulls prototypes towards to samples of their own class and pushes away from others. Unlike conventional HDC that passively aggregates samples, XL-HD actively learns class representations, making the prototypes discriminative. 

3.3 Inference 

At inference time, a query sample x𝑞 is deterministically projected into the D space using the same Sobol projection h𝑞 = Px 𝑞 . The encoded query is then compared with the learned prototypes. Clas-sification is performed by selecting the class with the highest cosine similarity ˆ𝑦 = arg max 𝑘 cos (h𝑞 , c𝑘 ). This pipeline is highly efficient, requiring only a single VMM for encoding and a small set of sim-ilarity computations. Our proposed approach ensures consistent inference across runs, while the learned prototypes provide sharper decision boundaries than traditional HDC. 

4 XL-HD Architecture 

XL-HD adopts a ReRAM-based IMC architecture, eliminating data transfer bottlenecks and achieving low energy (sub-𝜇 𝐽 ) and single-cycle inference. By exploiting the Ohmic accumulation and bitline sensing properties of ReRAM crossbars, our architecture directly executes the two core XL-HD primitives, e.g., Sobol projection and 

classification , within the memory array. 

4.1 Hardware Organization 

Our IMC hardware design (Fig. 2(a)) comprises two key modules: a. encoding and b. classifier, each implemented using ReRAM-based crossbar arrays (Fig. 2(b)). Its modules are as follows: 

Encoding Module : The encoding module consists of two ReRAM-based crossbars, marked as 𝑃 + and 𝑃 − in Fig. 2(a). The bipolar information is critical in HDC as it pushes HVs farther apart in the D vector space, making classes more distinguishable. We preserve the full bipolar semantics by encoding −1 as (1, 0) and +1 as (0, 1).Practically, this is realized through a dual-crossbar organization: the most-significant bit (MSB) is stored in the 𝑃 − crossbar, while the least-significant bit (LSB) is stored in the 𝑃 + crossbar. All cells are initially programmed to a high-resistance state (HRS), and only the cells corresponding to active components are switched to a low-resistance state (LRS). For example, a −1 (+1) element sets the corresponding 𝑃 + (𝑃 − ) cell to LRS while the complementary cell remains in HRS. 

Classifier Module : On the classification side, the trained class HVs are stored in a dedicated 1024 ×32 crossbar, implemented from 32 ×32

Peripheral Circuitry : Our peripheral architecture incorporates hardware elements from [10], including shared row/column drivers and a lightweight I/O interface. Crossbar Non-idealities. We model our IMC engine using the Stanford ReRAM compact model [26], explicitly incorporating both device behavior (cell resistance/capacitance) and wire parasitics (Fig. 3(a)). This model captures key non-idealities such as spatial variability, cycle-to-cycle noise, nonlinear switching, IR drop, and read noise, as illustrated in Fig. 3(b), and enables XL-HD to operate robustly under these effects. To ensure robust operation, we tile the logical 1024 × 1024 array into sixteen 64 × 128 subarrays. Limit-ing the bitline height to 64 cells keeps IR-drop and line-resistance (0.76 Ω/cell) [27] effects small, while the wider 128-column layout preserves high parallelism. This tiled organization maintains signal integrity during large-scale VMM and avoids the scalability issues associated with a single large crossbar. The SET/RESET distributions in Fig. 3(b) show roughly two or-ders of magnitude separation between LRS (K Ω) and HRS (M Ω). In a 64 × 128 crossbar, this inherently limits worst-case leakage relative to the selected conduction path. Moreover, sneak-path cur-rents plays a negligible role during VMM, since all rows are fully driven with respective 𝑉 𝑟𝑒𝑎𝑑 , leaving no half-selected devices to form unintended paths [28]. Since Sobol projection matrix and class HVs are written once, a simple 1R crosspoint array is sufficient, and efficient write schemes such as SWIPE [29] can be used to fur-ther minimize programming noise. These choices make XL-HD well suited for robust, low-energy, fully parallel IMC operation. 

4.2 End-to-End Algorithm/Hardware Mapping 

Traditional HDC relies on binding, bundling, and multi-bit quanti-zation, all of which require repeated logic operations, large memory accesses, and increased storage per dimension [30, 6]. In contrast, we co-design across the algorithm and hardware, herein referred to as H-XL-HD , to enforce a fully binary representation throughout the inference pipeline to minimize hardware cost while maintaining high accuracy. Fig. 4 presents H-XL-HD , which is designed to align algorithmic steps with efficient hardware execution. 5 Evaluation 

In this section, we evaluate the performance of the proposed XL-HD 

framework on three benchmarking datasets, i.e., MNIST [34], ISO-LET [35], and UCIHAR [36], covering a wide range of applications like computer vision, voice-, and activity- recognition. We com-pare XL-HD against several state-of-the-art HDC models and analyze both software execution and the proposed ReRAM-based IMC im-plementation. Our evaluation demonstrates that XL-HD achieves competitive accuracy while significantly reducing inference latency and energy through its deterministic projection pipeline and an inference path executed entirely within the IMC architecture.

5.4 XL-HD Execution Time 

We analyze the execution time of XL-HD w.r.t. prior implementa-tions. ReX-HD [10] incurs a significant latency by reloading feature HVs during every inference, scaling with the number of input fea-tures. In contrast, H-XL-HD targets inference-only hardware, with training performed offline in software. The bipolar Sobol projec-tion matrix and trained binary class HVs are programmed once at deployment, eliminating repeated memory transfers. As a result, end-to-end inference completes in two evaluation cycles, achiev-ing a steady-state throughput of one inference per cycle through pipelining. On ASICs, tinyHD [40] offers a compact footprint, but suffers from 12 .2𝑘 cycles encoding latency. Later, GENERIC [41] improves this but still remains 256 × slower than XL-HD .

6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we present XL-HD , a deterministic, hardware-friendly HDC framework co-designed with ReRAM-based IMC architectures. By replacing traditional binding and quantization with Sobol-based projections and a fully binary pipeline, XL-HD removes major over-heads and enables direct in-situ crossbar execution. Our results show that XL-HD ach-ieves competitive accuracy with a 0.395 𝑚𝑚 2

footprint, 0.40 𝜇 𝐽 inference energy, and single-cycle throughput, combining HDC accuracy with IMC efficiency. We observe only 

3.34 % and 5.16 % degradation at 𝐵𝐸𝑅 = 15 % for XL-HD with 𝐷 = 10 𝐾 

and 𝐷 = 1𝐾 , respectively, demonstrating strong resilience under high hardware fault rates.
