# Multibit neural inference in a 𝑁 -ary crossbar architecture 

## A. Moureaux a, A. Lopes Temporao a and F. Abreu Araujo a, *

> a

Institute of Condensed Matter and Nanosciences, Université catholique de Louvain, 1 Place Croix du Sud, 1348 Louvain-la-Neuve, Belgium 

Abstract 

In-memory computing (IMC) enables energy-efficient neural network inference by computing analog matrix-vector multiplications (MVM) in memory crossbar arrays. In this work we present a simulation framework for 𝑁 -ary crossbar architectures that retrieves MVM results with minimal implementation assumptions. The XOR and MNIST classification tasks were successfully inferred using a simulated crossbar array of (4 × 4) 4-states magnetic tunnel junctions (MTJ). MNIST accuracy reached 94.48% (vs. 97.56% software baseline). The software-hardware performance gap was further reduced using PCA dimensionality reduction. We identified weight quantization as the primary error source, and studied its impact alongside systematic nonidealities and random noise. We find that cell-specific random noise is less detrimental than systematic errors due to averaging across the array. Finally, we demonstrate an optimal number of states per cell that balances quantization error against resistance state resolution to minimize total MVM error. 

Keywords: Memristors, Crossbar, In-Memory Computing, Multiply-and-Accumulate, 𝑁 -ary 

Conventional computing is reaching its limits 

Computing systems based on the von Neumann architecture face fundamental efficiency limitations. The first one is a structural constraint known as the von Neumann bottleneck, caused by the physical separation between the memory (RAM) and the processing unit (CPU). This separation requires continuous

April 28, 2026 data movement across a shared bus, resulting in significant energy consumption [1]. The other limitation is known as the memory wall and arises from the increasing gap between the speed of processors and memory access times [2]. These limitations have become even more concerning recently with the growth of artificial intelligence (AI) models and of data scales, leading to significant energy consumption and processing latency. This observation drove the demand for new computing systems targeting computational performance and energy efficiency [3,4]. However, even with specialized accelerators such as GPUs and CMOS-based application specific integrated circuits (ASICs), limitations persist due to constrained on-chip memory, leakage currents, and limited data parallelism [5,6,7,8]. These challenges have become critical barriers to further progress, driving the need for a fundamental rethinking of computing paradigms. 

In-memory computing: implementations and integration 

In-memory computing (IMC) is a promising paradigm that integrates data storage and computation within the same physical units, thereby addressing the limitations mentioned previously [9]. In particular, IMC enables the highly efficient inference of deep neural networks (DNN) by doing analog matrix-vector multiplications (MVM) inside the memory. MVMs are the core operations in neural networks, multiplying input vectors 𝒙 representing data by weight matrices 

𝑾 learned during the training phase to propagate the resulting output vector 𝒚 

through the network layers: 

𝒚 = 𝑾 𝒙 (1) 

The most suited hardware for this operation are crossbar arrays of cells with distinct programmable conductance levels. The input is injected in the array as a voltage vector, and is multiplied by the conductance of each cell in a given row. The currents produced in each cell owing to Ohm’s law are then accumulated along each column to produce the output, accordingly to Kirchhoff’s law. Several types of non-volatile memories (NVM) have been explored for designing cells in such crossbar arrays, including resistive random-access memory (RRAM), phase-change memory (PCM), and flash memory [9,10]. Among emerging non-volatile memory technologies, magnetoresistive random-access memories (MRAM) stand 2out as particularly promising candidate for IMC due to their intrinsic non-volatility, high endurance, energy efficiency, and compatibility with CMOS technology [11]. Despite the advantages of analog computation in crossbar arrays, effective integration of IMC within large-scale AI systems necessitates algorithms capable of translating the array’s analog input/output signals into the digital signals required by standard AI algorithms. Indeed, input vectors of data require digital-to-analog conversion in order to be injected in the crossbar array. Similarly, the output of analog MVM operations must often be converted back into digital signals for reliable communication with subsequent compute units in the neural network like activation functions and biasing. These conversions are facilitated by digital-to-analog converters (DAC) and analog-to-digital converters (ADC), which introduce significant challenges: they not only contribute disproportionately to the system energy and area overhead, but also impact computational accuracy due to quantization errors and analog non-idealities [12,13]. Therefore, specialized algorithm-hardware co-design is essential for optimizing these interfaces, managing precision requirements, mitigating signal degradation, and compensating for device-level non-idealities. In this regard, a wide variety of algorithm-hardware co-design approaches have emerged, leveraging recent quantized neural network (QNN) research [14,15,16,17,18] to fit with the low-bit cells currently used. For instance, memristive QNNs, spin-transfer torque binary neural networks (STT-BNNs), and IMC based on spin-orbit torque magnetoresistive random access memories (SOT-MRAM) frameworks exploit binary and ternary weight schemes for robust in-situ learning and inference with reduced digital interfacing [19,20,21,22]. Stochastic magnetic tunnel junctions (MTJ) harness intrinsic probabilistic switching for QNN training without the random number generation (RNG) overhead [23], while FeFET arrays encode multiply-and-accumulate (MAC) results in time-domain activation delays, eliminating full analog-digital multipliers [24]. MoS 2 transistor-based 2T-1C cells perform analog MAC operations with recalibrated weights to ensure linearity under multilevel storage [25], and spintronic resonators use 3frequency-coded weights to classify RF signals without requiring digitization [26]. Domain-wall spin-orbit torque (DW-SOT) devices combine logic and storage for multistate Boolean operations [27], while multi-state magnetic tunnel junctions (M2TJs ) enable quantized analog synapses with low latency and energy [28,29]. 

𝑁 -ary is where the computing power lies 

In this paper we propose a simulation-based framework for running multibit AI inference on a 𝑁 -ary crossbar architecture i.e., whose cells present more than 2 distinct states. We first present an overview of the typical 𝑁 -ary crossbar architecture, followed by our solution for the retrieval of the actual MVM result based solely on the input signal and the measured output signal. A key advantage of our approach is that it allows to use the crossbar array in its simplest form as a standalone AI coprocessor without affecting its internal architecture nor the rest of the inference pipeline. Furthermore, it directly multiplies analog input vectors by the optimal quantized version of the weights matrix, without any additional hypotheses on the physics of the crossbar array cells, making it generalizable to any kind of 𝑁 -ary crossbar array implementation without loss of generality. In our case, we showcase the use of the framework within two simple benchmarking tasks, using digital twins of the magnetic tunnel junctions presented in Refs. [29,30], which display 4 distinct resistance states, as a starting point (as better metrics probably exist now). These MTJs will be referred to as multistate magnetic tunnel junctions ( M2TJs ) in the rest of the paper, so as proposed in Ref. [31]. A 4-by-4 crossbar arrays of 4-states M²TJs was simulated to infer a neural network that was trained to learn the XOR function. The results obtained with the simulated crossbar array are equivalent to the ones obtained with the full-digital neural network, showing that the intrinsic properties of the model learned during the training phase are preserved within the crossbar array-based inference process. Then, a network trained to classify the MNIST handwritten digits dataset was inferred using the simulated crossbar array. Accuracy levels ( 94.48% )close to the software baseline ( 97.56% ) were observed. We show that the primary source of error responsible for this decrease in accuracy is the quantization of 4the initially full-precision weights into the limited number of resistance levels of the crossbar array cells, highlighting the importance of multibit implementations. In order to optimize the use of the limited size crossbar array, the network’s internal architecture is simplified through principal components analysis (PCA) dimensionality reduction. We observe a decrease of the accurcacy gap between the simulated hardware and the software inference accuracies, proving that the crossbar array is particularly well-suited for inferring simplified network architectures. Two other sources of error are finally studied: systematic nonidealities affecting all the cells of the crossbar array identically, and random noise affecting each cell independently due to thermal fluctuations and device-to-device variations. Both error sources result in an error in the MVM result that increases linearly with the amplitude of the source. However, we show that cell-specific errors have a less detrimental impact on the inference accuracy than systematic errors of similar amplitude, due to the beneficial averaging effect across the whole array. Finally, we observe the existence of an optimal number of states per cell that minimizes the error in the MVMs by balancing weights precision and noise robustness. We demonstrate through simulation that this number evolves with respect to the level of noise affecting the crossbar array, and conclude that smaller numbers of states are preferable in noisier environments, a crucial information for the future development of experimental validation platforms.

MNIST classification 

The same study was carried out for the MNIST handwritten digits classification task in order to assess the performance of the crossbar array inference on a more complex case. A neural network was trained to classify the MNIST dataset, with 11 an input layer of 784 neurons accounting for the (28 × 28) pixels of the images, a hidden layer of 128 neurons activated with the reLU function, and an output layer of 10 neurons activated with the sigmoid function, each neuron corresponding to a digit from 0 to 9. The final answer is given by the neuron of the output layer presenting the highest value. After training, the accuracy of the model reached 

97.56% on the 10000 samples of the testing set. The same procedure as in the XOR case was used to infer the network using a crossbar array of (4 × 4) 4-states M²TJs. To do so, the weights matrix 𝑨 was split into several submatrices of shape (4 × 4) that were sequentially written into the crossbar array to compute parts of the output vector  ̃ 𝒚 , a method referred to as multiplexing and presented in Ref. [21]. The input must also be modified several times to carry out the whole MVM with the crossbar. The accuracy of the crossbar array inference process reached 94.48% , marking a drop of 3.08% 

accuracy from the software baseline due to the quantization error. The total number of sub-operations required to perform the MVM is given by 

⌈𝑚 4 ⌉ × ⌈ 𝑛 4 ⌉ (19) 

where 𝑚 and 𝑛 are respectively the number of rows and columns in the matrix 𝑨 ,totalizing 6272 sub-operations in our case for only carrying out the first MVM. These numerous intermediary operations are a consequence of the limited size of the simulated crossbar array, and are expected to be reduced in the future with the development of larger crossbar arrays. In parallel, scaling aspects such as weight storage and tiling belong to the architectural design of the system and are strongly dependent on the specific implementation of the crossbar array and its integration. However, some techniques such as dimensionality reduction can already be used in the meantime to reduce the size of the weights matrices and hence reduce the number of required sub-operations without any loss of generality. We applied principal component analysis (PCA) [34] to reduce the input data dimensionality from 784 to 87 without significant loss of information ( 90% of data variance preserved) before training the model. Only the images of the training set were used to compute the principal components, which were extracted from 12 both training and testing sets before respectively training and testing the model. This allowed to reduce the number of required sub-operations down to 704 , which represents a 88.69% decrease compared to the non-reduced case. PCA also allows to reduce the amount of noise naturally present in the input data, which helps improving the performance of the model. For that reason, the software accuracy of the model after PCA dimensionality reduction reached 98.03% . On the other hand, the inference of the simplified network with the simulated crossbar array led to an accuracy of 95.24% , marking a drop of 2.79% from the software baseline. PCA as a dimensionality reduction technique hence allows the reduction of noise inside the data and the simplification of the architecture, eventually leading to a better software baseline performance, a smaller performance drop from that software baseline when using the crossbar array for inference, and a lower number of required sub-operations if the crossbar array is of limited size. The scaling of the error in larger crossbar arrays or multiplexed arrays is assessed in the last section of this paper. 

Error assessment 

Quantization error 

The quantization of full-precision weights into 2-bits values is the only source of error considered so far in the pipeline. To assess its impact on the accuracy of the crossbar array-based MVMs, we simulated MVMs with random (4 × 4) 

matrices with values drawn from 𝒩(𝜇 = 0, 𝜎 = 1) and input vectors of 4 values drawn from 𝒰(0, 1) . The root-mean-squared error (RMSE) between the software ground truth and the result obtained with the simulated crossbar array was then computed. The RMSE was averaged over 2000 MVMs, and the number of states per cell 𝑁 was swept from 1 to 64 , marking 6-bits precision weights. While such large numbers of resistance levels per cell are not yet accessible with current memristive technologies, an increase of 𝑁 can be expected in the future with the development of multistate memristive devices, with some recent works reporting up to 16 states per cell [30]. It can be seen in Fig. 3 that the quantization RMSE follows a clear 1/𝑁 evolution, due to the increasing bit precision of the weights. While the development of multistate memristive devices with a higher number of 13 states per cell is still a challenge, this result highlights the significant performance gain allowed by the use of multistate devices compared to binary ones, even if 𝑁 

is only 3 or 4.

Systematic errors 

While quantization error is inherent to the hardware inference pipeline, external sources of error also have an impact on the accuracy of the MVMs performed with the crossbar array. First, we investigate systematic cell non-idealities whose origin is common to all the cells in the crossbar array. These non-idealities induce a shared bias in the properties of the cells, and may lead to significant performance degradation. We focused this study on the misalignment between the free and the fixed ferromagnetic layers of the M²TJs reported in Ref. [29]. This defect creates a non-ideal resistance level distribution that cannot be placed on a line. In other words, the resulting resistance levels are not perfectly equidistant. In practice, we first consider that all the cells originally access the same linearly distributed set of resistance states. We then model the non-idealities by adding a random offset drawn from 𝒩(0, 𝜎 NL ) to each resistance state. The resulting non-ideal state distribution is shared by all the devices in the crossbar array to ensure systemacy. We then compute the RMSE in the result of randomized MVMs in a 14 simulated array of ( 4 × 4 ) cells presenting 4 resistance states like previously, for different values of 𝜎 NL .Fig. 4 shows that the RMSE in the MVM result increases linearly with 𝜎 NL .However, the RMSE is not equal to 0 in the absence of non-idealities( 𝜎 NL = 0 ) due to the presence of the quantization error. Indeed, at low 𝜎 NL values, the resistance levels distribution is almost perfectly linear and the RMSE is dominated by the quantization contribution ( 1/4 ), marking a lower limit of the RMSE in this case. 

Cell-specific errors 

We then investigated the presence of non-idealities specific to each cell, due for example to sources such as cell-to-cell variations and noise. To simplify the study, we consider a combination of all of these non-idealities by adding a second random offset to each resistance state drawn from a normal distribution 𝑁 (0, 𝜎 ⟂). The key difference with the previously introduced systematic non-idealities is that these random offsets are independant between each cell, and can hence benefit from averaging effects over the whole array. We repeated the same study as for systematic non-idealities, and observed that while the RMSE measured in random MVM results also evolves linearly with 𝜎 ⟂ as seen in Fig. 4, the increase in RMSE is slower with 𝜎 ⟂ than with 𝜎 NL thanks to mitigation by averaging.

Error scaling 

We now consider a crossbar array with fixed levels of systematic and cell-specific non-idealities 𝜎 NL and 𝜎 ⟂, and we investigate the scaling of the RMSE in the MVM results with respect to the size of the array. We assume that this scaling is only due to the increase of the number of cells in the array, and that the RMSE scales similarly in a multiplexed array of small size and in a geometrically larger array. When 𝜎 NL = 0 Ω and 𝜎 ⟂ = 50 Ω (i.e. , the cells are ideal but subject to noise), we observe that the total RMSE in the MVM results is constant with the number of rows 𝑚 in the matrix 𝑨 (which is either related to the size of the crossbar array or to the number of multiplexing steps required), after a short transient regime at low 𝑚 values as seen in Fig. 6 a. This is due to the fact that rows correspond to independent output channels of the crossbar array, and that the error in each output channel is drawn from the same distribution. Hence, adding a new row in 𝑨 simply adds a new sample of the same error distribution in the output vector, leaving the RMSE unchanged. However, it can be seen that the RMSE increases with the square root of the number of columns 𝑛 in the matrix 𝑨 

17 (Fig. 6 b), because the noise-induced error of the columns effectively accumulates along each row. When 𝜎 NL = 50 Ω and 𝜎 ⟂ = 0 Ω (i.e. , the cells are subject to systematic non-idealities but no noise), the RMSE in the MVM results scales similarly as in the previous case, i.e. , constant with the number of rows 𝑚 and increasing with the square root of the number of columns 𝑛 (Fig. 7). However, the increase of the RMSE with 𝑛 is more significant in this case, which can be explained here again by the fact that systematic non-idealities are not mitigated by averaging effects over the whole array as opposed to cell-specific non-idealities. n

Conclusion 

The proposed framework allows to simulate the inference phase of a neural network with a crossbar array of multistate memristive devices, and to assess the impact of different sources of error on the accuracy of the results. The result of the MVM performed with the crossbar array is retrieved by scaling the output signal with predetermined factors and offset. This allows to use the crossbar array as a standalone computational unit in the inference pipeline. The performance of the crossbar array inference process was assessed on the XOR approximation and MNIST classification tasks, showing a good agreement with the software baseline in both cases, and highlighting the performance gain allowed by the use of multistate memristive devices even with only 4 states per cell. The impact of the quantization error on the accuracy of the MVM results was also studied, showing a clear 1/𝑁 evolution with the number of states per cell 𝑁 .Two other sources of error were investigated: systematic non-idealities which lead to a shared bias in the cells properties, and cell-specific non-idealities that are independant between each cell. The RMSE in the MVM results was observed to increase linearly with the deviation from the ideal linear state distribution for both types of non-idealities, but the increase is slower for cell-specific non-19 idealities thanks to mitigation by averaging effects over the whole array. This is also observed when considering the scaling of the RMSE with the size of the array, where the RMSE is constant with the number of rows 𝑚 but increases with the square root of the number of columns 𝑛 in both cases, with a more significant increase in the presence of systematic non-idealities. Finally, the optimal number of states per cell 𝑁 opt was found to be a trade-off between the minimization of the quantization error and the maximization of the states resolution in the presence of noise. However, the MVM results were observed to be robust to non-idealities, allowing to benefit from the use of multistate devices with 𝑁 higher than 4 even in the presence of significant noise. Our general framework allows to predict the performance of a crossbar array-based inference process after including the relevant physical parameters related to the memristive cells used in the array. By assessing the actual levels of noise and non-idealities affecting the hardware, it also allows to determine the optimal number of states per cell to minimize the error in the MAC operation. We believe that this framework will be useful for the design of future crossbar arrays of multistate memristive devices, and will accelerate the development of IMC co-processors.
