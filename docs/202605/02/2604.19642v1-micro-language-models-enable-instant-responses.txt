Abstract 

Edge devices such as smartwatches and smart glasses cannot continuously run even the small-est 100M–1B parameter language models due to power and compute constraints, yet cloud in-ference introduces multi-second latencies that break the illusion of a responsive assistant. We introduce micro language models ( μLMs): ultra-compact models (8M–30M parameters) that instantly generate the first 4-8 words of a contextually grounded response on-device, while a cloud model completes it; thus, mask-ing the cloud latency. We show that useful language generation survives at this extreme scale with our models matching several 70M-256M-class existing models. We design a col-laborative generation framework that reframes the cloud model as a continuator rather than a respondent, achieving seamless mid-sentence handoffs and structured graceful recovery via three error correction methods when the local opener goes wrong. Empirical results show that μLMs can initiate responses that larger models complete seamlessly, demonstrating that orders-of-magnitude asymmetric collab-oration is achievable and unlocking responsive AI for extremely resource-constrained devices. The model checkpoint and demo are available at: https://github.com/Sensente/micro_ language_model_swen_project .

2 Related Work 

Speculative decoding and model routing. Spec-ulative decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) follows a draft-and-verify mechanism: tokens are only committed and displayed after the large model validates them, leaving TTFT bounded by the network round-trip. In contrast, our commit-and-continue framework displays the μLM output immediately upon on-device generation. The cloud model accepts them as context and computes the remainder, ensuring TTFT that is entirely indepen-dent of network conditions. Another strategy is model cascading or rout-ing (Chen et al., 2024; Sakota et al., 2024), which uses a router to direct queries to either a small on-device model or a powerful cloud model. When the cloud model is picked, it is still fundamentally limited by the Internet and queuing latencies. 

Small-scale and specialized models. Recent work has produced increasingly compact models like MobileBERT (Sun et al., 2020), TinyBERT (Jiao et al., 2020), Llama-3.2-1B (Meta, 2024), LaMini-LM (Wu et al., 2024), and SmolLM (Allal et al., 2025), yet even these remain prohibitive for always-on inference on wearable and embedded hardware. The closest model is MiniMind (Gong, 2024), a 26M-parameter project designed as an educational tool, evaluated informally on a Chinese-language benchmark. Our work differs as follows: we pro-vide a characterization of response generation qual-ity in the 8M-30M regime, and we treat micro-scale models not as endpoints but as the initiators of a collaborative generation system. Prior work has also explored specialized gen-eration tasks like next-word prediction (Yu et al., 2018), email response suggestions (Kannan et al., 2016), writing assistance (Chen et al., 2019), and story continuation (Eldan and Li, 2023). We in-stead target open-domain user query responses un-der extreme parameter and latency constraints. 

Conversation fillers versus real responses. Our work builds on filler generation strategies in spo-ken dialogue systems (Skantze and Hjalmarsson, 2013), which use disfluencies to hold the floor while preparing a full response (Sacks et al., 1974). However, scripted fillers like “Um” or “Let me think about it” are repetitive and robotic, and risk re-ducing affinity in users (Ciechanowski et al., 2019; Skjuve et al., 2019). Rather than generating vac-uous placeholders, we use μLMs to generate the first semantically grounded tokens of the actual response, sidestepping this tension entirely. 

3 Approach 

Research on latency perception consistently shows that visible forward progress, even partial output, dramatically reduces perceived wait time (Kim et al., 2025a; Gnewuch et al., 2022; Arapakis et al., 2014; Harrison et al., 2010). Our core design prin-ciple follows directly: commit tokens immediately. We propose a collaborative generation frame-work that decouples two goals: the first usable out-put , which must arrive instantly, and a cumulative high-quality response , which requires cloud-scale reasoning. A lightweight on-device μLM generates a short, contextually grounded opener; the cloud model continues within the same context to ensure completeness and quality. Smooth continuation requires the cloud model to access the μLM output as its starting context. This can be achieved either by replicating the μLM in the cloud or by transmitting its generated tokens alongside the original query. Given the negligible size of μLMs and its quick runtime, both are prac-tical and the choice is a deployment decision based on infrastructure and operational constraints. 

3.1 Micro Language Models 

Our strict inference-time latency, memory and en-ergy constraints push us to make the local model as small as possible without collapsing its ability to produce a contextually grounded snippet. We construct a family of decoder-only Trans-formers in which the modeling stack and train-ing pipeline are held constant. Each variant fol-lows a GPT-style causal language model built on a Transformer backbone, using pre-norm resid-ual blocks (Xiong et al., 2020), rotary positional embeddings (Su et al., 2024), RMSNorm (Zhang and Sennrich, 2019), and a gated feed-forward network (Shazeer, 2020). To better respect tight on-device latency and memory budgets, we adopt grouped-query attention with fewer key-value heads (Ainslie et al., 2023), and tie the input embed-dings with the output head (Press and Wolf, 2017). We keep the gated MLP width rule fixed across variants. Autoregressive decoding uses key-value caching. We instantiate five variants by sweeping hidden size 256 , 384 , 512 and layers 8, 16 , span-ning 8.8–29.5M parameters (Fig. 4a). 

3.1.1 Micro LM Training 

To align our models with their chatbot-based in-teraction application, we pretrain from scratch on chat-style instruction dialogues, using the standard next-token prediction objective. The detailed train-ing setup and hyperparameters are in §A. 

Datasets and pre-processing. Our pretraining cor-pus is built from 3 instruction-following dialogue datasets: UltraChat (Ding et al., 2023), MOSS (Sun et al., 2024), and Instruction_merge_set (LinkSoul, 2023). These were curated for supervised fine-tuning (SFT), which contain dense, high-quality multi-turn conversations. We retain English-only conversations and serialize each example into a unified multi-turn chat format with explicit turn-boundary tokens. To reduce formatting ar-tifacts and better match μLM’s intended role as a lightweight dialogue opener, we apply a cleaning pipeline, including HTML unescaping, Unicode canonicalization, and control-character removal, followed by dialogue-specific filtering to remove web-page-like dumps, boilerplate code or math templates, markdown table remnants, decorative separator lines, and emoji- or symbol-heavy noise. We discard turns that become empty after cleaning, collapse adjacent duplicate turns, and de-duplicate the resulting training sequences. The final pretrain-ing corpus contains 1.485B non-padding tokens. 

Tokenizer and fine-tuning. We train a shared byte-pair encoding (BPE) tokenizer on the pretraining corpus with a vocabulary size of 12,288, which helps control embedding overhead in our micro on-device setting. For supervised fine-tuning, we adopt a widely used open instruction-following cor-pus (Xu et al., 2024b) and retain only English ex-amples. We remove malformed or over-length in-stances and export the retained conversations in the same chat format for training. The resulting fine-tuning set contains 323.4M non-padding tokens. 

Keeping FLOPS consistent across variants. We approximately match the pretraining compute bud-get across μLM variants. Following prior scaling-law practice (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022), our total training compute satisfies 

FLOPs ∝ N D , where N is the number of train-able parameters and D is the number of training tokens. With the data pipeline, tokenizer, max-imum sequence length, and effective batch size fixed, the number of tokens processed per optimizer step is constant, so D is proportional to the num-ber of optimizer updates. We assign each variant an optimizer-step budget inversely proportional to its parameter size using our 28.85M model trained for three epochs as the reference, and terminate training once the assigned step budget is reached. 

3.2 Collaborative Generative Framework 

The central challenge of collaborative generation is what happens at the boundary. The cloud model receives a prefix from a model orders of magnitude smaller, potentially mid-sentence and imperfect. Left to its own defaults, a cloud LLM would treat this prefix as a complete utterance and respond to it rather than continuing it; restating/evaluating the opener or contradicting it in a way the user sees. 

3.2.1 Handoff via instruction following 

To address this challenge, we design a handoff scheme via prompting: the cloud model is in-structed to act as a continuator : its output will be concatenated directly after the μLM’s tokens, and the combined text must read as a single flu-ent response. 3.2.2 Error correction and graceful recovery 

Even a well-trained μLM will occasionally gen-erate an opener that is factually dubious or head-ing in an unhelpful direction. Prior work shows that self-correction in LLMs requires external feed-back (Madaan et al., 2023; Gou et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023) and successful refinement requires additional scaffolding to guide the model toward the correct trajectory despite being provided the wrong prefix. So, we treat error correction as an ex-plicit design dimension of collaborative generation, which must be deliberately structured rather than left to the cloud model’s defaults. We design three modes shown in Fig. 3. 

Explicit correction. This is the default and the most transparent mode. When the opener is er-roneous, the cloud model issues a direct, single-sentence correction, prefixed with "Correction:", before proceeding with the full response. This mode prioritizes factual accountability over seam-lessness, and is most appropriate in high-stakes or information-critical contexts. 

Natural recovery. Rather than flagging the error, the cloud model recovers the way a fluent human speaker does: acknowledging the error naturally, without abruptly pivoting the μLM response. A short bridging sentence of at most twelve words piv-ots naturally away from the problematic opener, fol-lowed immediately by a concise, correct response. 

Humor-aware recovery. This mode is motivated by prior conversational agent research: when chat-bots integrate humor, users perceive the interaction as more interesting and anthropomorphic, which in turn enhances satisfaction (Niculescu and Banchs, 2019; Shin et al., 2022). Rather than treating an errant opener as a failure to be corrected, this mode reframes it as a deliberate creative detour. The cloud model pivots back to the correct answer wit-tily and without breaking character. The full in-struction sets for these modes are provided in M.2. 

4 Evaluation and Results 

We first benchmark μLMs, then evaluate the collab-orative generative framework, present the results of our user study, and finally report on latency. 

4.1 Benchmarking Micro Language Models 4.1.1 Metrics Quantitative metrics. We evaluate μLM zero-shot on five benchmarks via lm-eval-harness (Gao et al., 2024): HellaSwag, PIQA, BoolQ, ARC-Challenge, and OpenBookQA. All multiple-choice tasks use length-normalized accuracy ( acc_norm )to reduce answer-length bias. We report an overall quantitative score as the mean across all tasks. 

Qualitative metrics. We evaluate our micro language models on dialogue-style QA datasets, scored via LLM-as-a-judge ( GPT-4o being the eval-uator) across five rubric dimensions. Since μLM initiates responses rather than completing them end-to-end, the judge is explicitly instructed not to pe-nalize brevity or truncation (Zheng et al., 2023; Dubois et al., 2024). We use five rubric dimensions used in prior work on open-ended generation and dialogue (full prompt shown in §I): • Local Helpfulness : does the opening make meaningful progress toward answering the query (Dubois et al., 2024). • Relevance : does the content stay on-topic with respect to the user question, a key criterion for generation (Fabbri et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2023). • Correctness : is the response factually plausible and free of obvious hallucination (Ji et al., 2023; Fabbri et al., 2021). • Clarity & Coherence : is the text readable and well-formed (Xu et al., 2023), a key metric in hu-man evaluation of generated text (Xu et al., 2023). • Informativeness : does the response provide concrete content over vague filler, aligning with prior dialogue and generation evaluations that dis-tinguish substantive answers from merely fluent ones (Fabbri et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2023). 

Validating LLM scores with human judgment. 

To validate our LLM-as-a-judge setup, 10 hu-man annotators each score 10 randomly sampled question-response pairs on the same five rubric di-mensions and 1–5 scale, and then compute the over-all score. We find strong human–LLM agreement: Pearson correlation, r = 0 .803 , and mean absolute error, MAE = 0 .546 , confirming the LLM judge reliably tracks human scoring trends. 

4.1.2 Datasets 

To compute the qualitative metrics, we benchmark 

μLMs on three QA datasets: WikiHow (Koupaee and Wang, 2018), Vicuna_Bench (Chiang et al., 2023), and AdvisorQA (Kim et al., 2025b). To reduce evaluation contamination, we verify near-duplicate overlap between the evaluation questions and all training data used in this work. Follow-ing (Xu et al., 2024a), after light normalization, we apply MinHash-based matching (Broder, 1997) to detect high-overlap cases and exclude flagged questions from evaluation.

4.2.3 Reference comparisons and setup 

We compare against two standalone reference mod-els: Qwen3-0.6B (Yang et al., 2025), which re-sponds directly to user queries, and μLM-28M in standalone mode, which generates a complete re-sponse without cloud handoff. We use deterministic decoding ( temperature =0) for both μLM and cloud models. Each local-cloud pair is evaluated on a fixed prompt set un-der identical continuation and correction instruc-tions across three prefix lengths, with all stitched responses scored by the LLM-as-a-judge protocol.

5 Conclusion 

We introduced micro language models, which in-stantly generate the first words of a response on-device while a cloud model completes it. We showed that useful language generation survives at this extreme scale, that semantic continuity across the device-cloud boundary is achievable, and that graceful error recovery can be structured as an ex-plicit design dimension rather than an afterthought. Together, these results establish that orders-of-magnitude asymmetric collaborative generation be-tween a micro model and a cloud LLM is feasible. 

6 Limitations 

While our ablation studies show that within the query-lengths common in our target application, the performance is fairly stable, μLMs may still be less robust on much longer prompts, where critical cues are buried deep in the input. Similarly, our training focuses on everyday conversational data rather than specialized domains such as math or coding. However, in our target deployment, where users interact via wearables such as smartwatches, glasses, or even smartphones, long or technical prompts are unlikely in practice. A mitigation, if needed, is to fall back to a generic response when input length exceeds a threshold. In addition, our current formulation focuses on single-turn response initiation rather than long-context multi-turn dialogue management. A po-tential solution is for this context to be managed by the cloud LLM instead of μLMs. Our embed-ded evaluation also uses Orange Pi as a practical first-order proxy for target wearable platforms; val-idation on production wearable SoCs remains an important direction for future work. While μLM generates 4–8 words near-instantaneously, the user interface can pace the words at a rate matching comfortable reading speed until cloud streaming takes over. This preserves the perceptual benefit of instant first-word appearance while maintaining a smooth, progressive reading experience that users are accustomed to. Finally, our paper focuses on text, but the col-laborative framework also extends to audio-based interfaces on devices such as earbuds and smart glasses. Pairing μLMs with tiny ASR models, e.g., Whisper-tiny (Radford et al., 2022), could enable responsive on-device speech interfaces, a promis-ing direction we leave for future work. 

Ethical Considerations. This work introduces a collaborative generation framework pairing a micro language model with a cloud LLM. A key privacy consideration is that user queries are ultimately transmitted to a cloud model. While the μLM initi-ates the response locally, the cloud handoff means sensitive queries are not fully on-device. Deploy-ments in sensitive contexts (health, personal ad-vice) should apply appropriate data minimization and consent practices. Our user study involved human participants who were informed of the study’s purpose, and all col-lected interaction data was privacy-sanitized before use. The study does not involve vulnerable popula-tions and presents no foreseeable participant risk.

Architecture details. μLM is implemented as a decoder-only causal language model with a pre-norm transformer architecture. Each block applies RMSNorm before self-attention and before the feed-forward sublayer. For positional encoding, we use rotary positional embeddings (RoPE) with 

θ = 10 6. The attention module follows a grouped-query design, where the number of key-value heads is smaller than the number of query heads, reducing KV-cache cost while retaining multi-head query projections. The feed-forward network adopts a SwiGLU-style formulation with separate gate, up, and down projections and SiLU activation. Un-less otherwise specified, the intermediate dimen-sion is set following the common 83 d rule and then rounded to a multiple of 64. Consistent with mod-ern lightweight LM practice (Liu et al., 2024; Allal et al., 2025), the linear projections are bias-free, and the input embedding matrix is tied with the output language-model head. 

Hyperparameters details. We train all model variants with AdamW ( β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.95 ,

ϵ = 10 −8), weight decay 0.1, and gradient clipping with a maximum norm of 1.0. Unless otherwise specified, we use a peak learning rate of 5 × 10 −4,batch size 64 , maximum sequence length 1024 ,and bfloat16 mixed-precision training. We apply a short linear warmup followed by cosine learning-rate decay to 10% of the peak learning rate. If not manually specified, the warmup length is set to 2% 

of the total optimizer steps, with a minimum of 10 

steps. Under gradient accumulation, all schedul-ing quantities are defined in terms of optimizer steps rather than micro-steps, including learning-rate scheduling and the stopping criterion used for FLOPs-matched training. For FLOPs-matched comparisons, we approximate training FLOPs as proportional to the number of trainable parameters times the number of optimizer steps, using a base configuration with hidden size 512 , 8 layers, and 4

epochs to determine the budget for other variants. 

B Additional Details About Dataset Processing and De-duplication 

Additional processing details. We first convert all examples into a unified multi-turn chat serializa-tion with explicit turn-boundary tokens, yielding a consistent dialogue representation across hetero-geneous raw data sources. We then apply turn-level cleaning to reduce formatting artifacts while preserving natural conversational content. This includes HTML unescaping, Unicode canonicaliza-tion, control-character removal, whitespace normal-ization, and the removal of residual HTML frag-ments, fenced or inline code, markdown-table ar-tifacts, decorative separator lines, and emoji- or symbol-heavy noise. Turns that become empty af-ter cleaning are discarded, and adjacent duplicate turns may be collapsed. To satisfy a fixed context budget, we split long dialogues at turn boundaries and pack them into sub-dialogues under a maxi-mum token limit rather than truncating them arbi-trarily. If an individual turn is itself too long, we first split it into token-based segments and then re-pack the resulting segments as pseudo-turns. We also merge very short tail segments back into the preceding window when possible, which helps pre-serve multi-turn locality and reduce wasted context capacity. 

Cross-split near-duplicate verification. We per-form cross-split near-duplicate verification between the evaluation prompts and both the pretraining and supervised fine-tuning corpora. For the pre-training corpus, we extract prompt-like local views from each serialized training example and use them as the matching units, which is more suit-able for detecting whether an evaluation prompt is locally contained within a much longer training se-quence. For the supervised fine-tuning corpus, we index only user turns rather than full conversations or assistant responses, since the main concern is whether evaluation questions overlap with training-side user queries. All texts are lightly normalized before matching. To make verification scalable, we first use MinHash and locality-sensitive hash-ing to retrieve candidate matches, and then apply a containment-based criterion for final verification: 

Cont( q, x ) = |S(q) ∩ S(x)||S(q)| , (1) where S(·) denotes the shingle set of a normalized text, q is an evaluation prompt, and x is a training-side candidate view. A pair is flagged when the containment score exceeds a high threshold. This design targets normalized near-duplicates and high-overlap lexical matches rather than only ex-act string matches, following classic containment-based similarity estimation for large-scale doc-ument matching (Broder, 1997). It also aligns with prior findings that deduplication can reduce train–test overlap and memorization in language model training (Lee et al., 2022), as well as re-cent leakage-detection practice that uses MinHash-based retrieval to identify potential benchmark con-tamination in large-scale LLM settings (Xu et al., 2024a).

H Safety Behavior on Should-Not-Answer Prompts 

We further examine model behavior on a set of 80 prompts (Cheng et al., 2025) that should not be answered directly. Our experiment here uses the 28M μLM model and Qwen3-235B-A22B for the cloud model. Using a conservative criterion, we count a case as a self-refusal only when the local model ex-plicitly rejects the request, e.g., with direct refusal language such as “I’m sorry” or “I can’t assist”. Under this definition, the local model alone refuses in 31 out of 80 cases. Among the remaining 49 cases where the lo-cal prefix does not explicitly refuse, the cloud LLM successfully steers 33 cases into harmless responses, such as refusal, safety-oriented redirec-tion, or non-actionable reformulation. For example, on “how to hack into email ac-count” , the local model immediately responds with a direct refusal, showing that safety-aligned behav-ior can emerge even at the local prefix stage. At the same time, in some cases, the local model still begins with unsafe or instructional-style continua-tions, such as on “how to crack specific software” .In our system, such failures are further corrected by the downstream cloud LLM, which serves as an additional safety layer to redirect problematic local outputs into harmless refusals or safe alternatives. These observations suggest that, beyond improv-ing the local model itself, the staged design also provides a practical safety strategy by allowing a stronger continuation model to intervene when the local prefix fails.

M Prompts for Collaborative Framework 

M.1 Prompt for collaborative generation 

> Continue the assistant 's answer after an already-spoken opener. Your output will be concatenated immediately after the opener. Write ONLY the continuation (no new opener, no meta). Do NOT mention, quote, evaluate, or negate the opener. IMPORTANT: The opener may be an incomplete fragment (e.g., cut off mid-sentence). Start by continuing from that fragment so the combined text reads as one fluent sentence. Do NOT restate the opener; only add the missing continuation.

M.2 Prompt of Error Correction and Graceful Recovery 

We designed three modes of error correction, the prompt of each mode is attached to the general generation. 

Explicit correction. 

> If correction is needed, start a new line with \"Correction: \" and state the corrected content directly in ONE sentence. (do not repeat or quote the opener), then continue with the rest of the response. Otherwise, continue seamlessly and then provide substantive answer content.

Natural correction. 

> Goal: produce a single natural-sounding message after concatenation, including smooth self-repair when needed. If the opener is an incomplete fragment, add the minimum tokens needed to finish that sentence naturally. If the opener is already a complete sentence, do NOT restate it; just continue. If the opener 's direction is dubious or unhelpful, use a HUMAN SELF-REPAIR pivot: - Start with ONE short bridging sentence (<= 12 words) that sounds natural. - The bridge MAY include a light, playful aside, but must stay polite and brief. - Do NOT use blunt negation or diagnosis (avoid: wrong/incorrect/misunderstanding/does not support/not possible). - Do NOT explain the mistake. Just pivot. After the bridge, give the correct answer with concrete steps.

Humor-aware recovery. 

> GOAL: If the opener is hallucinated or factually absurd, treat it as a deliberate creative detour and pivot. wittily back to reality without breaking character. PIVOT EXECUTION (Only when the opener is hallucinated/absurd): - If the opener is incomplete, finish it quickly. - Start a NEW LINE. - Use a playful, self-deprecating tone as if the detour was intentional. - Immediately continue with the factual, helpful answer. ANSWER STYLE: - Make the bridge-to-answer transition snappy and high-energy.

O Additional Details About Orange Pi Experiments 

For Orange Pi 5, we evaluate each model under a fixed inference setup with the same prompt, decod-ing configuration, and thread setting. For each run, we first perform a few warm-up iterations, and then continuously repeat single-turn inference within a fixed 90-second measurement window. During this window, we record the total wall-clock time, the number of valid runs, and the total number of generated and prompt tokens. Power is measured externally with a USB power meter placed in series with the Orange Pi power supply. We record the meter’s cumulative en-ergy reading before and after the 90-second mea-surement window, and compute the total energy consumption from their difference. To estimate inference-only cost, we subtract the idle-board baseline measured under the same setup, and report dynamic energy per query and dynamic energy per generated token accordingly.
