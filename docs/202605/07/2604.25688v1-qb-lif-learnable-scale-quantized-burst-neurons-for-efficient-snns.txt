# QB-LIF: Learnable-Scale Quantized Burst Neurons for Efficient SNNs

# Abstract 

Binary spike coding enables sparse and event-driven com-putation in spiking neural networks (SNNs), yet its 1-bit-per-timestep representation fundamentally limits in-formation throughput. This bottleneck becomes increas-ingly restrictive in deep architectures under short sim-ulation horizons. We propose the Quantized Burst-LIF (QB-LIF) neuron, which reformulates burst spiking as a saturated uniform quantization of membrane potentials with a learnable scale. Instead of relying on predefined multi-threshold structures, QB-LIF treats the quantiza-tion scale as a trainable parameter, allowing each layer to autonomously adapt its spiking resolution to the underly-ing membrane-potential statistics. To preserve hardware efficiency, we introduce an absorbable scale strategy that folds the learned quantized scale into synaptic weights dur-ing inference, maintaining a strict accumulate-only (AC) execution paradigm. To enable stable optimization in the discrete multi-level space, we further design ReLSG-ET, a rectified-linear surrogate gradient with exponential tails that sustains gradient flow across burst intervals. Exten-sive experiments on static (CIFAR-10/100, ImageNet) and event-driven (CIFAR10-DVS, DVS128-Gesture) bench-marks demonstrate that QB-LIF consistently outperforms binary and fixed-burst SNNs, achieving higher accuracy under ultra-low latency while preserving neuromorphic compatibility. 

Keywords: Spiking Neural Network; burst spiking; learn-able quantization; low-latency inference; surrogate func-tion 

# 1 Introduction 

Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) are widely regarded as a promising paradigm for energy-efficient neuromorphic computing, owing to their event-driven processing mech-anism. Unlike Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) that rely on continuous-valued activations and energy-intensive multiply-accumulate (MAC) operations, SNNs communi-cate via sparse spike events and can be efficiently executed on neuromorphic hardware such as Loihi [ 1 ] and Tian-jic [ 2 ]. On such platforms, synaptic computation reduces to low-power accumulate (AC) operations, offering the potential for orders-of-magnitude improvements in energy efficiency [3]. Despite this advantage, the strict binary nature of con-ventional spiking neurons introduces a fundamental infor-mation bottleneck . A binary spike conveys at most one bit of information per timestep, which is drastically lower than the representational capacity of real-valued activa-tions in ANNs [ 4]. As a result, directly trained SNNs often suffer from severe quantization loss and struggle to achieve competitive performance on complex visual recog-nition tasks, such as ImageNet, unless long simulation horizons are employed. However, increasing the number of timesteps inevitably undermines the latency and energy advantages that motivate SNNs in the first place. To alleviate this bottleneck, recent studies have ex-plored burst spiking or multi-level spiking neurons, which allow a neuron to emit multiple spikes within a single timestep. These approaches effectively extend the bi-nary spike representation to discrete multi-level outputs and have demonstrated encouraging performance improve-ments. However, most existing methods remain rooted in a threshold-centric design philosophy . Representative works, such as multi-level spiking neurons [ 5] and integer-valued LIF neurons [ 6], typically rely on fixed, equidistant threshold sets or predefined integer spiking levels shared across layers. From a representational perspective, such designs im-plicitly impose a uniform quantization rule on membrane potentials, yet without explicitly modeling or optimiz-ing the associated quantization scale. Crucially, treating burst levels as fixed hyperparameters overlooks the in-herent layer-wise heterogeneity of feature distributions in deep SNNs, where early layers often require fine-grained sensitivity to input variations, while deeper layers operate over substantially different dynamic ranges. Consequently, a globally fixed spiking granularity can lead to either pre-mature saturation or excessive information loss, limiting the effectiveness of burst-based spiking models. In this work, we revisit burst spiking from a quanti-zation perspective and propose Quantized Burst Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (QB-LIF) neurons. Instead of treating burst spiking as a byproduct of multiple hand-crafted thresholds, we explicitly model burst spiking as a satu-rated uniform quantization of membrane potentials with a learnable scale. Specifically, a single scale parameter γ

controls both the quantization granularity of burst levels and the effective contribution of each emitted spike. By integrating γ into the computational graph, the spiking resolution of each layer can be optimized end-to-end via backpropagation, allowing the network to automatically 1

adapt its representational precision to task demands. Crucially, we further introduce an absorbable scale strat-egy , through which the learned quantization scale is folded into synaptic weights during inference. This design decou-ples training-time flexibility from inference-time efficiency: while QB-LIF benefits from multi-level burst represen-tations during learning, the deployed network preserves a strictly accumulate-only execution paradigm without introducing additional MAC operations or control flow. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: 

• We reinterpret burst spiking as an explicit uniform quantization of membrane potentials and propose the Quantized Burst-LIF (QB-LIF) neuron, establishing a learnable quantization framework for adaptive multi-level spike representations. 

• We introduce a layer-wise learnable quantization scale together with an absorbable scale strategy, enabling adaptive spiking resolution during training while pre-serving strict accumulate-only (AC) inference for neu-romorphic deployment. 

• We design ReLSG-ET, a surrogate gradient with expo-nential tails, to ensure stable optimization in discrete multi-level burst spaces. 

• Extensive evaluations on static and event-driven benchmarks validate that adaptive quantized burst representations substantially enhance representa-tional capacity under ultra-low latency while main-taining event-driven sparsity and hardware compati-bility. 

# 2 Related Work 

## 2.1 SNN Training Paradigms 

Training methods for Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) primarily fall into two categories: ANN-to-SNN conversion and direct training. ANN-to-SNN Conversion leverages parameters from pre-trained ANNs to circumvent training difficulties. Techniques such as weight scaling, threshold balancing [ 7 ], and soft-reset mechanisms [ 7] have success-fully mapped analog ReLU activations to spike rates in deep architectures. Advanced optimization strategies, in-cluding quantization clip-floor-shift (QCFS) [ 8] and layer-wise calibration [ 9], align spiking rates with ANN activa-tions to achieve high accuracy. However, this paradigm largely treats spikes as rate-based approximations of static values, and often does not explicitly exploit fine-grained temporal dynamics, requiring long simulation windows to reduce quantization error. Direct Training utilizes sur-rogate gradients to address the non-differentiability of spike generation [ 10 ]. By introducing differentiable ap-proximations for membrane potentials [ 11 ] and enforcing joint optimization of spatial and temporal dynamics (e.g., STBP [ 12 ]), these methods significantly reduce latency. Our work adopts the direct training paradigm to fully leverage the temporal efficiency of SNNs. In addition to training paradigms, several works enhance SNN perfor-mance through architectural adaptations. Wang et al. [ 13 ]proposed SpikeGoogle, which integrates a GoogLeNet-style inception module into a spiking framework to capture multi-scale spatial features within spiking convolutional layers. Such approaches focus on structural enrichment of spatial representations rather than modifying the spike coding mechanism itself. 

## 2.2 Burst Spiking Dynamics 

Binary spike coding fundamentally limits the information throughput of spiking neurons, which has motivated a series of studies aiming to mitigate quantization-induced information loss. Early efforts mainly focus on rectify-ing the membrane potential distribution prior to bina-rization, including InfLoR-SNN [ 14 ], IM-Loss [ 15 ], and RecDis-SNN [ 16 ]. These methods alleviate information degradation at the distribution level, yet the spike out-put remains binary and thus intrinsically bounded by a 1-bit-per-timestep representational capacity. Beyond distribution rectification, several works extend the discrete spiking space of spiking neurons. Ternary Spikes [ 4 ] introduce signed spike states {− 1, 0, 1}, provid-ing a modest increase in expressiveness while still relying on a fixed and low-cardinality output space. However, such limited extensions remain insufficient for modeling the wide dynamic ranges encountered in deep spiking networks. More expressive spiking behaviors have been explored through multi-level or burst-based spiking mechanisms. Multi-Level spiking (MLF) [ 5] enables neurons to emit multiple spikes within a single timestep by employing fixed, equidistant thresholds. While MLF increases representa-tional capacity, its rigid threshold design lacks adaptability to layer-wise heterogeneity in deep architectures. Related integer-valued training paradigms, such as I-LIF [ 6], op-timize networks using integer activations and decompose them into spike trains at inference. Similarly, Spike-driven Transformer [ 17 ] adopts spiking approximations to facil-itate large-scale SNN training, but primarily focuses on architectural scaling rather than learning adaptive spike quantization levels. Fan et al. [ 18 ] propose a multi-synaptic spiking neuron that enhances representational capacity by introducing parallel synaptic pathways with independent thresholds and dynamics. By distributing spiking responses across multiple synaptic channels, this model improves spatiotem-poral information encoding without relying on high spiking rates. At a phenomenological level, such synaptic paral-lelism also increases the information carried by individual spike events, yielding effects partially analogous to burst spiking, but realized through structural expansion at the synaptic level rather than neuron-level multi-spike emis-sion. 2In parallel, burst spiking has been explored in ANN-to-SNN conversion frameworks [ 19 , 20 , 21 ], where burst spikes are leveraged to reduce conversion error and resid-ual information loss. By permitting a neuron to emit multiple spikes within a single timestep, these methods effectively compress the simulation time required for ac-curate inference. However, these approaches inherit the limitations of conversion-based pipelines and do not fully exploit burst dynamics during direct SNN training. Existing burst spiking methods implicitly implement a uniform quantization through fixed threshold spacing. However, the quantization step size is treated as a pre-defined structural constant rather than an optimizable parameter. As a result, spiking granularity remains man-ually specified and cannot adapt to layer-wise membrane-potential statistics. 

## 2.3 Surrogate Gradients for Discrete Spik-ing 

Surrogate gradient methods [10 ] are essential for train-ing SNNs. Classical functions include rectangular (box-car) [ 12 , 22 ], sigmoid, and arctan variants [ 23 ]. While effective for binary neurons, they face limitations when the membrane potential spans multiple threshold inter-vals, as in burst neurons. Smooth surrogates (e.g., Arctan) decay rapidly away from the threshold, causing gradient starvation for upper-level burst intervals where potentials are large. Rectangular surrogates provide stable flow but introduce abrupt cutoffs, destabilizing learning when mem-brane potentials shift between quantization bins during the optimization of learnable thresholds. Taken together, prior studies have explored efficient training paradigms, enhanced spike representations, and surrogate gradient designs largely in isolation. Conversion-based methods emphasize accuracy but incur long tem-poral windows, while direct training approaches improve latency yet remain constrained by limited spike expres-siveness. Meanwhile, existing burst and multi-level spik-ing schemes increase capacity through fixed structures or architecture-specific approximations, and surrogate gradients are predominantly tailored to binary spiking regimes. In contrast, our work reconsiders quantization as an intrinsic spiking mechanism within neuron dynam-ics, conceptually distinguishing it from prior approaches that treat integer-valued or multi-level spiking as train-ing or inference approximations, and thereby providing a quantization-oriented perspective for adaptive and expres-sive spike-based representations.

# 5 Experiments 

We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate the effec-tiveness of the proposed QB-LIF and compare it against recent state-of-the-art SNN training methods. Evaluations are performed on both static and event-based benchmarks to assess generalization across different input modalities. For static image recognition, we use CIFAR-10, CI-FAR 100 [ 32 ], and ImageNet [ 33 ]. For neuromorphic vision tasks, we adopt CIFAR10 DVS [ 34 ] and DVS128-Gesture [ 35 ], which are widely used benchmarks in event-driven perception. We evaluate QB-LIF on multiple representative archi-tectures, including ResNet-18 [ 23 ], ResNet-19 [ 36 ], and ResNet-20 [ 37 ], following standard SNN design practices. For fair comparison, we adopt the same data preprocessing and augmentation strategies as in prior work, including dataset-specific normalization, random horizontal flipping and cropping, AutoAugment [38], and Cutout [39]. Optimization is performed using SGD or AdamW with a cosine annealing learning rate schedule, depending on the dataset and architecture. All experiments are con-ducted on a workstation equipped with an AMD EPYC 7282 16-core CPU and 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. ## 5.4 Layer-wise Behavior of Quantized Burst Representations 

To analyze how quantized burst representations evolve across network depth, Fig. 5 visualizes empirical burst-level activation distributions in representative layers. Each subplot corresponds to a trained model with its learned quantization scale γ(ℓ) for that layer. Since γ(ℓ) determines the discretization step from mem-brane potential to burst level, it modulates the effective dynamic resolution of spike responses. Depending on its alignment with the membrane potential statistics of each layer, different burst-level utilization patterns emerge. In some layers, probability mass spreads across a wide range of intermediate burst levels, indicating active multi-level usage. In others, responses concentrate toward lower lev-els with rapidly decaying tails, reflecting more restricted dynamic resolution. These observations confirm that burst quantization behavior is depth-dependent rather than uni-formly distributed across the network. We further quantify these patterns using the statistics shown in Fig. 6. The learned quantized scale γ(ℓ) exhibits a non-monotonic dependence on network depth. Corre-spondingly, burst-level entropy varies across layers and typically reaches higher values in intermediate stages of the network. The elevated entropy indicates more active utilization of available burst levels in those layers. Impor-tantly, the measured entropy remains substantially below the theoretical maximum log 2(n + 1) ∣∣n=20 ≈ 4.39 , sug-gesting that burst capacity is selectively allocated rather than uniformly saturated. Despite increased multi-level utilization, neuronal spar-sity is preserved. As shown in Fig. 6(d), layers with higher burst entropy do not exhibit systematically higher acti-vation ratios. This demonstrates that quantized burst coding increases information density per spiking event without inducing a proportional rise in firing frequency. Moreover, most intermediate and deep layers retain a sub-stantial number of effective burst levels (with probability 

p > 10 −2), ensuring representational flexibility without enforcing uniform level usage. Overall, these results indicate that quantized burst learn-ing induces a structured, depth-dependent allocation of dynamic resolution, enhancing representational efficiency while preserving the event-driven sparsity characteristic of spiking neural networks. 

## 5.5 Energy Efficiency 

Table 6 reports the estimated inference energy per image on CIFAR10. All models are trained for 200 epochs under identical settings. Energy is computed following the neu-romorphic estimation protocol of Tang et al. [ 49 ], based on floating-point operations (FLOPs) and synaptic opera-tions (SOPs), assuming 12.5 pJ per 32-bit FLOP, 77 fJ per 32-bit SOP, and 3.7 pJ per sign operation. Memory access and data movement costs are excluded, so the compari-son reflects arithmetic energy under identical hardware assumptions. As Nmax increases, both SOP count and energy con-sumption rise due to the higher number of burst-induced synaptic events. For example, compared with binary spik-ing (50.14 μJ), QB-LIF with Nmax = 20 consumes 67.64 μJ. However, this increase accompanies a substantial accuracy improvement from 90.50% to 95.58%. Importantly, the energy growth is gradual and sublinear relative to the ac-curacy gain. While SOPs increase by approximately 4.3 ×

from binary to Nmax = 20 , energy rises by only 35%, owing to the dominant cost contribution of FLOPs, which re-mains constant across configurations. This indicates that burst coding enhances information throughput primar-ily through event-level modulation rather than structural computation expansion. Overall, QB-LIF achieves improved accuracy under a modest increase in estimated energy, reflecting a controlled trade-off between representational capacity and synaptic activity. 

# 6 Conclusion 

In this study, we revisited burst spiking from an explicit quantization perspective and proposed the Quantized Burst-LIF (QB-LIF) neuron for efficient multi-level spik-ing representation. By formulating burst emission as a saturated uniform quantization process with a learnable scale, QB-LIF enables layer-wise adaptive control over spiking resolution. The absorbable scale strategy decou-ples training-time flexibility from inference-time efficiency, preserving strict accumulate-only execution on neuromor-phic hardware. In addition, the proposed ReLSG-ET surrogate gradient stabilizes optimization in discrete multi-level spiking spaces by maintaining consistent gradient flow within the burst interval while suppressing instability outside it. Extensive experiments across static and event-driven benchmarks demonstrate that QB-LIF consistently im-proves accuracy under ultra-low latency settings while retaining compatibility with integer-based neuromorphic computation.
