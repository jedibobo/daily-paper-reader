# Stability properties of Minimal Gated Unit Neural Networks ⋆

Department of Management, Information and Production Engineering, University of Bergamo, Via G. Marconi 5, 24044 Dalmine, Bergamo, Italy 

Abstract 

In this work, we address the need for efficient and formally stable Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) in environments with limited computational resources by analyzing the stability of the Minimal Gated Unit (MGU) network, a lightweight alternative to common gated RNNs used in system identification. We derive sufficient parametric conditions for the MGU network’s input-to-state stability and incremental input-to-state stability properties. These conditions enable a-posteriori validation of model stability and form the basis for novel stability-promoting training methodologies, including a warm-start of the network’s parameters and a projected gradient-based optimization scheme, both of which are presented in this work. Comparative evaluation, including robustness analysis and validation on synthetic and real-world data (i.e., the Silverbox benchmark), demonstrates that the minimal gated unit network successfully combines formal stability guarantees with superior parameter efficiency and faster inference times compared to other state-of-the-art recurrent neural networks, while maintaining comparable and satisfactory accuracy. Notably, the results attained on the Silverbox benchmark illustrate that the stable MGU network effectively captures the system dynamics, whereas other stable RNNs fail to converge to a reliable model. 

Key words: Stability of discrete-time nonlinear systems; Recurrent neural networks; Minimal gated unit networks. 

1 Introduction 

Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) , in particular gated 

RNNs [23] such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) and Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) networks, are widely adopted in the control and system identification communi-ties [18, 26]. These networks offer powerful internal mem-ory mechanisms for modeling nonlinear dynamical behav-iors in Multiple-Input Multiple-Output (MIMO) systems. Furthermore, their inherent state-space formulation makes them ideal for integration into model-based control strate-gies, leading to many recent applications within the Model Predictive Control (MPC) framework [15, 34, 4, 29, 30]. However, despite their proven modeling ability, these gated RNNs are built upon architecturally complex structures [23]. Specifically, LSTM and GRU networks, with their multiple-gate mechanisms, inherently carry a high parameter count 

and significant computational load during training and in-ference. This complexity poses a major barrier for applica-tions with limited hardware resources, such as those used in embedded control systems [19], where fast inference times

Beyond implementation constraints, a significant theoretical challenge lies in the limited formal guarantees available for the identified RNNs [26]. For instance, traditional training of RNNs does not inherently ensure stability properties , lead-ing to potential physically-inconsistent behaviors and un-bounded predictions [21]. To address this issue, the control community has recently worked on establishing formal sta-bility properties for these networks [34, 3, 4, 11], primarily focusing on Input-to-State Stability (ISS) and Incremental Input-to-State Stability ( δISS) [31]. While ISS ensures that bounded inputs lead to bounded state trajectories [13], the stricter δISS property guarantees that the distance between any two state trajectories is bounded by the difference be-tween their respective initial states and input sequences [31]. Establishing these properties is vital for the identification task itself, to have a consistent model when the identified system is itself stable, but it becomes even more critical when the identified models are intended for use in control loops, where one is interested in the best stable model that approximates the given system [5, Chapter 12]. For instance, formal stability is a prerequisite for ensuring the safety of MPC controllers [5, 4] and for designing converging state observers [29, 30]. Addressing this, sufficient parametric conditions have been derived sufficient parametric conditions for the δISS of LSTM [34] and GRU [3] networks , paralleling efforts in other model classes such as kernel-based methods [27, 28]

This context highlights the need for alternative architectures capable of reconciling the pow-erful modeling capabilities of gated RNNs with the param-eter efficiency required by embedded applications, while simultaneously allowing for the derivation of parametric conditions to promote their stability during training. 

1.1 Contributions 

In this work, we introduce the analysis and promotion of stability in Minimal Gated Unit (MGU) networks [37], a 

parameter-efficient and computationally lighter alternative to GRU and LSTM networks, along with its first validation in system identification tasks. The primary contributions of this paper are: (1) We extend the stability analysis framework, previously established for GRU networks in [3], to the MGU archi-tecture. Reflecting the MGU network’s specific struc-ture, which induces a tighter coupling between mem-ory retention and input updates compared to the GRU architecture, we derive distinct parametric conditions that structurally differ from their GRU network coun-terparts. We establish these sufficient conditions for ISS and δISS in both single-layer and multi-layer con-figurations, and we prove that the δISS condition para-metrically implies the ISS one. (2) We evaluate three methodologies designed to promote the derived δISS condition during network training. While loss augmentation constitutes the state-of-the-art approach currently employed in RNNs to promote stability [34, 3, 11], we introduce and evaluate two novel strategies: (i) warm-start of the parameters via an ad-hoc pre-optimization, and a (ii) projected gradient-based optimization scheme utilizing a convex feasible set. All methodologies are complemented by a modified early stopping strategy , inspired by the ones in [11, 3], that ensures that the best network parameters found during training also imply stability and not just superior performance on a validation dataset. (3) We perform a numerical evaluation of the MGU net-work using three benchmark datasets : the pH Reactor dataset [33] to compare MGU and GRU networks and to test the proposed training methodologies, the Four-Tank system [1] to assess robustness against measure-ment noise, and the Silverbox dataset [35] to validate the performance on real-world data. (4) We provide open-source MATLAB code, available at [10], allowing testing the proposed training method-ologies for MGU networks.

5 Stability-promoted training of MGU networks 

Estimation of the parameters θ of the MGU network in (7b) (more commonly referred to as MGU network training )involves solving an optimization problem using gradient-based optimization procedures specifically tailored for neu-ral networks [26]. Traditional training methodologies (re-viewed in Section 5.1) entail the unconstrained minimiza-tion of a loss function that measures the discrepancy between the output predictions of the RNN and the available output data, possibly with the addition of a regularization term [26]. However, this rationale offers no stability guarantees for the identified model. To address this, we introduce and com-pare three complementary methodologies for training stable MGU networks. These include: (i) the current state-of-the-art approach known as loss augmentation [34, 3, 11] (de-tailed in Section 5.2), which we find insufficient alone for MGU network training; (ii) a parameters warm-start proce-dure (Section 5.3); and (iii) a Projected Gradient-based op-timization Method (PGM) (Section 5.4). All methodologies are enriched with a stability-driven early stopping strategy, inspired by those in [11, 3], which ensures that MGU net-work training returns the best-performing δISS network, if available 3 (see Section 5.2). We remark that the proposed methodologies are not mutually exclusive alternatives but can be used in synergy , each pro-moting stability in a different way. 6 Numerical results 

In this Section, we evaluate the MGU network performance and the proposed stability-promoting training methodolo-gies. We first introduce the benchmark datasets and the training configuration in Section 6.1. Then, in Section 6.2, we benchmark the traditional MGU network against the GRU network to validate its efficiency. Finally, we assess the robustness of the resulting models against noise in Section 6.4 and their performance on real-world data in Section 6.5. 

6.1 Benchmark systems and datasets 

We employ three distinct benchmarks to validate different properties of the MGU network. 

Dataset 1: pH Reactor. To compare the performance and stability properties of MGU and GRU networks, we use the pH neutralization simulator from [33], a standard nonlinear benchmark for RNN stability [30, 34]. The system controls the pH level ( y) of a mixture via an alkaline flow ( u), subject to acid and buffer disturbances. The dataset [33] consists of V = 20 sequences ( N (v) = 2000 , ∀v ∈ { 1, . . . , V })generated by a Multilevel Pseudo-Random Signal (MPRS) with sampling time Ts = 10 s . D is partitioned into Vtr = 15 

sequences for training, Vval = 4 for validation, and Vtst = 1 

for testing. 

Dataset 2: Four-Tank. To evaluate the robustness to noise of stable MGU and GRU networks, we employ the quadru-ple tank benchmark system [1], which also constitutes a standard benchmark for RNN stability evaluation [3, 29]. This system consists of four interconnected tanks receiving water from two pumps via two split valves. It exhibits non-linear, non-minimum phase dynamics governed by the spe-cific coupling between the upper and lower tanks. The task is to model the water level in the first lower tank ( y) as a function of the two input pump flow rates ( u), constituting a multiple-input single-output identification problem. Unlike the other benchmarks, where data is obtained from stan-dard repositories, we generated this dataset by simulating the physics equations using the parameters reported in [3]. The system was excited with an MPRS to generate a train-ing/validation trajectory of 2500 steps windowed into 250 

steps subsequences ( Vtr = 8 and Vval = 2 ), and an indepen-dent test trajectory ( Vtst = 1 ) of 5000 steps, with Ts = 15 s .To assess robustness, additive white Gaussian noise is added to the output data at three Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) lev-els: 100 , 10 , and 5 6 . For each SNR level, 50 independent

Dataset 3: Silverbox. To validate the performance on real-world data, we utilize the Silverbox benchmark [35]. This dataset represents a nonlinear electrical circuit simulating a mechanical vibrating system with a Duffing oscillator. We use the standard data split from the literature [8], dividing training/validation sequences with an 80-20% ratio and win-dowing them in 250 steps subsequences. 

Training configuration. As customary, we normalize inputs and outputs to the range [−1, 1] to satisfy Assumption 2. We train all networks for emax = 2000 epochs using the Adam optimizer [12, Chapter 8] with a base learning rate of

The stability penalty weight ρ, the stability margin

This specific configuration was selected as it empir-ically demonstrated a robust balance between training con-vergence speed and stability enforcement across the differ-ent datasets. The only dataset-dependent hyperparameter is the number of mini-batches Nmb , which is adjusted accord-ing to the specific dataset dimensions. All networks are implemented, trained, and validated in MATLAB 2024b on a workstation with a 64-core 3.20 GHz CPU, 2.23 GHz GPU with more than 16000 cores and 24 GB of memory, and 255 GB of RAM. We remark that the numerical results presented here can be regarded as the first

7 Conclusion 

This work investigated the use of MGU networks for the identification of stable nonlinear models, with a particular focus on deriving sufficient ISS and δISS parametric con-ditions for the MGU model. To promote compliance with the δISS condition, we also proposed and compared three distinct training methodologies: loss augmentation, param-eters warm-start, and a projected gradient-based optimiza-tion method. These methodologies are complemented by a stability-driven early stopping strategy that ensures that the training process returns the best-performing δISS MGU net-work, if available. Numerical results showed that the MGU network exhibits superior parameter efficiency and faster in-ference times compared to the GRU network, while achiev-ing comparable predictive performance. This favors the use of the MGU network in embedded predictive control appli-cations, where model stability and resource constraints are of paramount importance. Our analysis of stability promo-tion revealed that, while the combination of loss augmen-tation and parameters warm-start yielded the best trade-off between prediction accuracy and δISS compliance for the MGU network, loss augmentation alone was insufficient to favor stability consistently for this smaller architecture, even though it aligned with stable GRU network levels of accu-racy. This contrasts with the GRU network, where loss aug-mentation alone sufficed, likely due to its higher parameter count and over-parameterized nature [12, Chapter 8]. This finding motivates the design of ad-hoc stability-promoting methodologies for smaller deep learning models, for which the δISS property may not be easily guaranteed. Finally, on real-world data from the Silverbox benchmark [35], the stable MGU networks significantly outperformed the stable GRU model baseline, demonstrating superior convergence capabilities under stability constraints. 11 A Proofs Proof of Proposition 1.
