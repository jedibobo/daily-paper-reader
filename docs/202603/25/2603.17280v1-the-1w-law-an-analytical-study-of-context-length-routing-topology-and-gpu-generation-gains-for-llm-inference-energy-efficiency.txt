Abstract 

How many tokens can a GPU inference cluster deliver per watt? Across deployments of identical hardware, the answer varies by 40 ×—not because of software inefficiency, but because of the serving context window. We derive the 1/W law : tokens per watt halves every time the context window doubles. The reason is simple: a larger context window shrinks the KV-cache concurrency limit while leaving GPU power draw roughly unchanged. At 64K context, an H100 can hold just 16 sequences in flight (tok/W = 1 .5); at 4K context, the same H100 holds 256 sequences (tok/W = 17 .6). What operators often miss is that routing topology —which determines the effective con-text window each GPU actually services—is a more powerful energy lever than buying newer GPUs. Working from published H100 power measurements [Chung et al., 2026], a calibrated logistic power model [Liang et al., 2026], and a roofline throughput model [Xu et al., 2026], we derive these results analytically using the inference-fleet-sim planning framework [Chen et al., 2026b]; no new hardware experiments were conducted. The analysis shows that two-pool context-length routing (FleetOpt [Chen et al., 2026a]) delivers roughly 2.5× better tok/W over a homogeneous fleet on either H100 or B200, while a hardware upgrade from H100 to B200 delivers roughly 1.7× at any topology. The two gains are independent— combining FleetOpt with B200 yields 4.25 × over the H100 homogeneous baseline, the prod-uct of the individual gains. Neither intervention alone gets you halfway there. B200 and H200 numbers are analytical projections ( ±20% uncertainty); H100 results are calibrated to published measurements and carry higher confidence. For MoE models, the model architecture itself adds a third lever. Using an active-parameter weight-streaming model, Qwen3-235B-A22B (22B active) on H100 reaches roughly 37.8 tok/W at 8K context analytically—5.1 × better than Llama-3.1-70B—because the per-iteration decode time scales with the number of activated weights rather than the total. MoE dispatch overhead is excluded from this estimate, so the number is an upper bound; empirical measurements on real hardware are needed to narrow the range.

The number of requests processed in parallel is therefore not just a throughput concern— it directly determines how much useful work you get per joule. The main thing controlling how many requests a GPU can hold in parallel is the serving context window. A KV cache of fixed VRAM can hold fewer concurrent sequences as the per-token memory footprint grows. A Llama-3.1-70B deployment on H100 (80 GB, TP=8 with tensor-parallel KV sharding) [Meta AI, 2024] can hold 256 concurrent 4K-token sequences but only 16 concurrent 64K-token sequences. Tokens per watt drops by the same factor across those two operating points—roughly 12 ×—and extends to a nearly 40 × spread across the full 2K to 128K context range. GPU vendors have been responding to growing context demands with more powerful accel-erators. B200 replaces H100 with 2.4 × the memory bandwidth and 2.4 × the VRAM—but also a TDP that is 43% higher (1,000 W vs. 700 W). Whether you actually get better tokens per watt from a B200 depends entirely on the context window distribution your workload presents at inference time. This paper works out the quantitative relationships between context window, routing topol-ogy, hardware generation, and fleet energy efficiency. We connect three existing tools—the logistic GPU power model of Liang et al. FleetOpt routing gives roughly 2.5× over homogeneous on both H100 and B200; B200 gives roughly 1.7× over H100 at any topology. Because the gains are orthogonal they multiply, reaching 4.25 × combined. Neither lever alone gets you half of that. Section 3.2 examines MoE models, where sparse activation collapses the per-iteration weight-streaming time and adds a third, architecture-level efficiency dimension.

# 4 Fleet Topology and Token Economy 

4.1 Fleet-Level Analysis Setup 

When serving a production workload at a given SLO, an operator provisions enough GPUs to sustain the request arrival rate. Fleet tok/W depends on both per-GPU efficiency and the number of GPUs required—a topology that sends all traffic to a 64K context pool forces every GPU to run at the low-efficiency end of the 1/W curve. But on a per-physical-GPU basis, the 8B semantic pool wins: a single 8B GPU handles as many sequences as eight 70B TP-sharded GPUs combined, at a fraction of the cost. The bottom line is that the energy case for semantic routing depends not on tok/W but on whether the 8B model can satisfy output quality requirements for the short-context fraction of traffic. 

5.2 Quantization Effects 

Quantization to fp8 or int4 cuts weight bytes by 2–4× [Micikevicius et al., 2022, Frantar et al., 2023], proportionally reducing W . For H100+70B, fp8 gives W ≈ 3.36 ms (vs. 6.72 ms in fp16), which roughly doubles tok/W at any fixed concurrency. The benefit is largest for dense models currently bottlenecked by weight streaming at moderate concurrency, and smallest for MoE models where W is already small relative to KV overhead.

# 8 Related Work 

The GPU power model at the core of this analysis comes from Liang et al. [Liang et al., 2026], who showed that GPU power under LLM workloads follows a logistic curve in batch size and that max_num_seqs is the primary control knob for grid voltage regulation. ML.ENERGY Benchmark v3.0 [Chung et al., 2026] provides the measurement data used to calibrate our H100 profile, and the AIConfigurator [Xu et al., 2026] supplies the roofline decomposition of W and 

H. We extend these single-GPU results to the fleet level and show that topology and generation gains are approximately independent and multiplicative. On the inference systems side, Splitwise [Patel et al., 2024] introduced prefill-decode disag-gregation, and vLLM [Kwon et al., 2023] introduced PagedAttention for KV-cache management. Both optimize for latency and cost per token. FleetOpt [Chen et al., 2026a] and inference-fleet-sim [Chen et al., 2026b] approach the same problem from a fleet capacity perspective. The connection we make here is between routing topology and energy: topology controls which part of the P (b) curve each GPU operates on, and therefore directly determines tok/W. On the energy measurement side, Chung et al. [Chung et al., 2026] and TokenPower-Bench [Niu et al., 2025] both confirm empirically that longer context degrades energy efficiency— TokenPowerBench reports roughly 3× higher energy per token when prompt length grows from 2K to 10K on H100. That direction is consistent with the 1/W law, but the mechanism differs: TokenPowerBench captures the cost of processing more input tokens during prefill, while our law governs the decode throughput ceiling set by KV-cache concurrency at a fixed serving context window. Maliakel et al. [Maliakel et al., 2025] show that the decode phase dominates inference time (77–91%) and is largely memory-bandwidth-bound, directly supporting the mechanistic basis of the 1/W law. GreenServ [Ziller et al., 2026] is the closest contemporaneous work on routing for energy efficiency. It routes individual queries across a heterogeneous pool of 16 open-source LLMs 8using a multi-armed bandit policy, achieving a 31% energy reduction over random routing. The shared intuition is that not every request needs the most expensive GPU or model. Where GreenServ and this paper diverge is in the mechanism: GreenServ routes across model families to avoid oversized models for easy tasks, while we route by context window within a single model to exploit the 1/W law. The structural guarantee from KV-cache capacity yields larger gains (roughly 2.5× at the fleet level) because context-window partitioning directly controls which segment of the logistic power curve each GPU occupies, rather than relying on learned query-difficulty estimates. Routing topology as an explicit energy lever—distinct from model selection, quantization, or hardware choice—remains underexplored. Treating energy as a linear proxy for cost obscures the 1/W relationship, which is multiplicative with hardware generation and independent of it. 

# 9 Conclusion 

Energy efficiency in LLM inference is shaped by three things that operators can actually control: GPU generation (which sets W , Pidle , and KV capacity), model architecture (which determines weight-streaming time and KV footprint), and routing topology (which determines the context window each GPU actually services). Of these, topology is the easiest to change today—you can restructure routing without buying anything new. The 1/W law explains why topology matters so much. Because tok/W halves every time the serving context window doubles, a routing strategy that confines short requests to a small-context pool effectively cuts the context window for the bulk of traffic. FleetOpt delivers roughly 2.5× over a homogeneous fleet, and B200 delivers roughly 1.7× over H100. These two improvements multiply, giving roughly 4.25 × total when combined— 2.5 + 1 .7̸ = 4 .25 because the gains stack on orthogonal dimensions. MoE models have a natural tok/W edge from fast weight streaming—only the active experts get loaded per step—but putting a reliable number on that edge requires empirical MoE dispatch measurements that we do not have. The values in Table 2 are upper bounds. All results are computed analytically via inference-fleet-sim [Chen et al., 2026b], with H100 power validated against ML.ENERGY v3.0. B200 numbers are projections with stated uncertainty. As measurement data for B200 and H200 becomes available, we expect the absolute numbers to shift somewhat while the qualitative picture— topology first, generation second, architecture third—holds. 

# 10 Limitations, Assumptions, and Future Work 

10.1 Modeling Assumptions 

A few simplifying assumptions limit the scope of the results. 

TP-sharded GQA KV storage. Fleet results assume tensor-parallel sharding of KV heads, so each GPU stores nkv /TP KV heads. For Llama-3.1-70B with nkv = 8 and TP=8, this gives one head per GPU ( κ ≈ 40 –55 KB/token), maximizing nmax . Models with fewer KV heads than TP ranks, or frameworks that replicate the full KV cache per GPU, will have lower nmax 

and correspondingly lower tok/W. 

Continuous-batching decode. We model throughput as n/τ (n, ¯L) with τ = W + H · n from the roofline. This is accurate for continuous-batching decoders (vLLM, TRT-LLM) in steady state. It does not capture chunked prefill interleaved with decode, head-of-line blocking, or KV-cache eviction under memory pressure. All three effects reduce achievable throughput, so the analytical tok/W is an upper bound. 9Steady-state traffic. Fleet sizing uses a steady-state queuing model at a fixed arrival rate, with context lengths drawn from a measured CDF. Real traffic is bursty and diurnal. Bursts require spare capacity beyond the steady-state minimum, which reduces average utilization and therefore tok/W. The reported values are attainable only under smooth, sustained load. 

B200 power model quality. B200 power parameters are derived from TDP-fraction heuris-tics calibrated on H100 data, then applied to B200’s 1,000 W TDP. No direct power-vs-concurrency measurements are available. We estimate ±20% uncertainty on absolute B200 tok/W; the generation-gain ratios ( ≈ 1.7×) are more robust because systematic errors partially cancel in the ratio. 

Fixed TP=8, single model per pool. All experiments use TP=8 instances of the same model in each pool. Smaller TP configurations are more power-efficient per GPU at low con-currency but reduce per-GPU throughput. Mixed-TP or mixed-model pools are not explored. 

Output-only energy accounting. Tok/W counts output tokens only. Prefill computation— which can account for 2–5× the power per request for long-prompt workloads—is excluded. For workloads with prompt-to-output ratios much greater than one (e.g., document summarization, RAG), the reported tok/W overestimates true energy efficiency. 

10.2 Limitations 

No empirical validation on B200/H200. The B200 and H200 results are projections. We have not measured vLLM or any production serving stack on B200 or H200, and cannot confirm that the power-vs-concurrency curve follows a logistic shape with the projected parameters. Researchers with access to these platforms should measure and publish the P (b) curve to fill this gap. 

Two-pool topology only. FleetOpt defines a single split boundary with one overflow param-eter. Real fleets may benefit from three or more pools partitioned by context length, or from content-adaptive pools that split on semantic class. The multiplicative gain structure suggests that finer-grained topologies could compound further efficiency improvements, but this is not analyzed here. 

Static fleet sizing. The analysis assumes a fixed, provisioned fleet. It does not model au-toscaling, spot instances, or GPU sharing. In practice, dynamic scaling changes effective uti-lization and would alter the quantitative tok/W values. 

Single workload CDF. Each experiment uses one of two traces. Real deployments serve mixed traffic with time-varying CDFs. The optimal split boundary Bshort and overflow γ∗ would need to be re-estimated as the workload shifts. 

10.3 Future Work 

Empirical B200/H200 power calibration. The most immediate follow-on is measuring the logistic P (b) curve for B200-SXM and H200-SXM using the ML.ENERGY methodology [Chung et al., 2026] and updating the fleet projections with HIGH-quality data. 

Prefill-decode disaggregation. Splitwise-style separation [Patel et al., 2024] assigns prefill and decode to different GPU pools. Combined with context-length routing, this could remove prefill energy from the output tok/W accounting and unlock further efficiency. Extending the power model to mixed prefill-decode batches is a natural next step. 10 Multi-pool topology optimization. Extending FleetOpt from two pools to K ≥ 3, poten-tially with different context windows and GPU types, could be formulated as a mixed-integer program. The multiplicative independence result suggests each additional split could compound the topology gain further. 

Carbon-aware joint optimization. Tok/W does not capture grid carbon intensity or elec-tricity price. Minimizing $/token or gCO 2/token requires accounting for PUE, time-of-day pricing, and grid mix. The per-GPU power model in this paper provides a natural starting point for a joint energy-cost objective. 

Speculative decoding interaction. Speculative decoding changes the batch-size distribu-tion in the decode loop, potentially increasing average nact . Whether this improves or degrades tok/W depends on the draft model’s power footprint and the verification hit rate—an open problem within the P (b) framework. 

Adaptive topology control. The split boundary is currently fixed offline from a historical CDF. An online controller that monitors the live request-length distribution and adjusts pool boundaries dynamically could maintain near-optimal tok/W under distribution shift.
