# Mask-aware inference with State-Space Models 

Ignasi Mas 1, Ramon Morros, Javier Ruiz-Hidalgo, and Ivan Huerta 

ignasi.mas.mendez@upc.edu 

Abstract. Many real-world computer vision tasks, such as depth com-pletion, must handle inputs with arbitrarily shaped regions of missing or invalid data. For Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), Partial Convolutions solved this by a mask-aware re-normalization conditioned only on valid pixels. Recently, State Space Models (SSMs) like Mamba have emerged, offering high performance with linear complexity. How-ever, these architectures lack an inherent mechanism for handling such arbitrarily shaped invalid data at inference time. To bridge this gap, we introduce Partial Vision Mamba (PVM), a novel architectural component that ports the principles of partial operations to the Mamba backbone. We also define a series of rules to design architectures using PVM. We show the efficacy and generalizability of our approach in the tasks of depth completion, image inpainting, and classification with invalid data. 

# 1 Introduction 

Many real-world vision pipelines do not receive complete, valid images due to sensor errors, application constraints, or other problems ( e.g ., sparse LiDAR or anonymized regions). Concurrently, State Space Models (SSMs), particularly Mamba [13] and its vision variants (like Vision Mamba [57] or VMamba [29]), are rapidly becoming a dominant architecture in computer vision. They have proven to be a strong alternative to Transformers [42] by achieving state-of-the-art or competitive performance while maintaining linear complexity, making them ideal for high-resolution tasks and efficiency-critical applications. Standard architectures (such as CNNs or Mambas) are designed for fully valid inputs. They treat all data as valid, including "invalid" values (often filled with placeholders, e.g ., zeros). This corrupts feature extraction and alters hidden states, leading to failures. Partial Convolutions (PConvs) [28] solved this for CNNs: the authors modified standard Convolutional layers to be mask-aware, meaning they consider which pixels are invalid. PConvs consist of a mechanism for making outputs agnostic to the placeholder values of the invalid data. While the principle of partial operations is well-established for CNNs, its adaptation to sequential, patch-based architectures like Mamba is non-trivial. To bridge this gap, we introduce Partial Vision Mamba (PVM), the first mask-aware, Mamba-based architecture designed for inference on arbitrarily shaped invalid inputs. PVM is a novel architectural component that, inspired by PConvs [28], adapts the solution for Mamba state-space models to ensure that only valid data influence the model’s output. Along with PVM, we establish a mask-aware

Although Masked Image Modeling (MIM) meth-ods, such as MAE [16] and MAP [51], do exist, they are pre-training strategies rather than mask-aware architectures like PConvs [28] and their final model expects a complete image during inference and is not inherently mask-aware. Notably, adapting mask-aware logic to SSM mechanisms is non-trivial, and re-quires a specific token-processing design that we propose to prevent invalid data propagation, otherwise a single token corrupted by invalid data would contami-nate the entire image. We prove the utility of our novel PVM block and our mask-aware frame-work by designing adapted architectures for 3 different tasks: depth completion, image inpainting and image classification. This proves the generalizability of the approach in 3 problems of different nature (generative and discriminative), that also belong to different domains (the first works entirely on depth data while the rest are RGB tasks). In the Depth Completion case, as there are no unguided methods based on VM, we define 2 architectures leveraging VM and PVM equivalently. For the remaining tasks we design solutions by modifying standard architectures and compare the improvement. We expect PVM-based solutions to improve the original VM-based ones in the presence of invalid data. Our contributions are summarized as: 

– We design a novel component, PVM, that allows processing inputs containing invalid data within the Vision Mamba architecture. 

– We formalize a mask-aware framework that allows the design of solutions using PVM for arbitrarily shaped invalid input data tasks. 

– We prove the generalizability of our approach by demonstrating its improve-ments in 3 distinct tasks: depth completion, image inpainting and image classification with invalid data. 

# 2 Related Work 

2.1 Sequence modeling and Computer Vision 

2D CNNs [21, 22, 38] are a popular solution to multiple tasks, including long-range visual modeling, but their strong locality requires deep stacks for a global receptive field. Sequential RNNs [9,18] saw limited adoption for vision problems due to poor parallelization. A paradigm shift occurred with the Transformer [42] and its adaptation to vision, ViT [10]. ViT modeled the global context through self-attention on patch sequences, but its O(N 2) quadratic complexity is prohibitive for high-resolution images. This efficiency bottleneck was addressed by hierarchical models such as the Swin Transformer [30], which used windowed attention for linear complexity. More recently, State Space Models (SSMs) like Mamba [13] offered an efficient O(N ) solution via a selective SSM. This was ported to vision in Vim [57] and VMamba [29], with hybrid models [3] also emerging. However, these standard models fail on partially invalid inputs, as they treat invalid pixels as valid data, which corrupts feature maps. Mask-aware inference with State-Space Models 3

2.2 Architectures for mask-aware inference 

Inference on partially invalid data requires mask-aware architectures. For CNNs, early approaches (such as [26]) pioneered mask-aware operations to efficiently process sparse data like LiDAR. Building upon this, Partial Convolutions [28] solved this with a mask-based re-normalization step, conditioning the output only on valid pixels. Soft alternatives include Gated Convolutions [49] or OR-Former [4]. For Transformers [42], self-attention offers an implicit, but not guar-anteed, mechanism for ignoring pixel masks. Explicit mask-aware Transform-ers (MAT [23]) and Diffusion models [36] also exist, but are outside the scope of our work, which focuses on Mamba-based models that are computationally much less complex. We explicitly differentiate from Masked Image Modeling (MIM) [1, 16, 41, 51] as its final models are mask-unaware at inference (MIM is a pre-training strategy). Critically, an equivalent, explicit architectural solution for the Mamba back-bone does not exist. Current Mamba models lack a built-in mechanism to dy-namically ignore arbitrarily shaped invalid inputs at inference, preventing the propagation of invalid tokens. Our work is the first to bridge this gap by propos-ing a mask-aware formulation for State Space Models. 

2.3 Tasks Depth Completion . Depth Completion regresses a dense depth map from a sparse, irregularly-masked input, often from LiDAR. Datasets like KITTI-3D [12] (LiDAR) or NYUv2 [37] (incomplete depth) are well-established benchmarks for this task. State-of-the-art methods, mainly specialized CNNs, employ diverse strategies such as spatial propagation (NLSPN [33]), dynamic affinities (DySPN [25]), recurrent updating (LRRU [44]), or guided feature upsampling (DFU [45]). Transformer models, such as CompletionFormer [56], later applied self-attention to leverage global context. Mamba-based models [19] and hybrids [46] have been explored, but lack a specific mechanism for invalid input data. These approaches are typically guided by an HQ RGB image (including the recent DMD 3C [24], which uses RGB-based models for knowledge distillation). Our PVM is designed to handle missing data with an efficient, mask-aware SSM backbone. Moreover, by using a PNCC projection, our architecture performs optimally even without RGB data, as explained in [32]. 

Inpainting . Image Inpainting, the task of filling arbitrarily shaped holes (masks), was addressed by both early [2] and deep learning [47] methods. Par-tial Convolutions [27, 28] established a foundational mask-aware architecture by re-normalizing operations conditioned only on valid data. Subsequent works im-proved semantic understanding and visual quality using GANs (CRFill [52]) and Transformers [6]. LaMa [40] achieved a major leap in global context modeling by using Fast Fourier Convolutions for a global receptive field. More recently, Diffusion Models [34] became state of the art for photorealistic generation, us-ing unconditional pretraining [31], direct inpainting training [35], or even text-guidance [53]. However, these iterative models are computationally expensive, and produce hallucinations, being less suited for structured content recovery. 4 I. Mas et al. 

Image Classification with invalid data . Different works [11,39,43] aim to solve Image Classification being robust to occlusions ( e.g ., a labeled car occluded by a pedestrian), including several data augmentation techniques (CutOut [8], CutMix [50]). This could include the aforementioned Masked Image Modeling strategies when adapted to this task [16]. However, our scope is completely dif-ferent as we interpret a mask-aware problem where we aim to condition the classification on only the valid data in the input. Mask-aware inference solutions like Partial Convolutions [28] could be adapted, but an architectural solution based on SSM is still missing to solve this problem at inference time. 

# 3 Methodology 

In this section we introduce the mask-aware framework that allows the de-sign of mask-aware architectural components (Sec. 3.1), present our novel PVM (Sec. 3.2, Sec. 3.3), and propose solutions for the three distinct tasks (Sec. 3.4) detailed in Sec. 1. 

3.1 Mask-aware processing 

Our framework for handling invalid data defines the input as a tuple (x, m),where x is the data tensor and m is its corresponding boolean validity mask. Let x ∈ RC×H×W be the input feature map, which contains both valid pixel data and placeholder values ( e.g ., zeros) for invalid regions. The mask m ∈ { 0, 1}H×W

The main principle of our methodology is to use m to ensure that all operations on x are conditioned only on valid data. A crucial aspect of our mask-aware framework is that the boolean validity mask m is not static; it is dynamically updated as data flows through the net-work, ensuring that the validity of each new feature is correctly tracked. The update rules depend on the specific operation. 

– Element-wise Operations : For operations like element-wise addition or multiplication between two tensors, (x1, m1) and (x2, m2), the resulting fea-ture’s validity is the logical intersection (AND) of the input masks. An output location (i, j ) is valid only if the inputs at that location were both valid. This mechanism allows valid features to be retained even when paired with invalid values, ensuring that only placeholder-agnostic information propagates. 

– Feature concatenation : When different feature maps are concatenated along the channel axis, their corresponding validity mask is also the logical intersection of the input masks as in Element-wise operations. Mask-aware inference with State-Space Models 5

– Feature reshaping : When feature maps are reshaped ( e.g . flattened), the validity mask is reshaped accordingly. 

– Receptive Field Operations : For standard, mask-unaware layers that aggregate information (like convolutions or fully-connected layers), an output feature is marked as invalid if it was computed using at least one invalid input. In contrast, when the partial, mask-aware, analogous version is used, an output feature is marked as valid if it was computed using at least one valid input. Some examples: 

• Convolutions : A standard 2D Convolution output is invalid if any input in its receptive field is invalid (morphological erosion [15]). A PConv [28] output is valid if at least one input is valid (morphological dilation [15]). 

• Fully-Connected Layers : A standard FC layer’s entire output is invalid if any input feature is invalid ( i.e ., sum (min ) < count (min )). A mask-aware equivalent produces a valid output if at least one input feature is valid ( sum (min ) > 0). 

• Pooling Layers : Pooling operations update the mask m identically to their convolutional counterparts. A standard pool ( e.g . average pooling) requires all features in the receptive field to be valid, while a partial pool (e.g . partial average pooling, where the average would be computed over input valid features) requires only one valid pixel. 

• Sequence Modeling : In operations like SSMs, the sequence history acts as the receptive field. Assuming that different types of scanning mechanisms are applied, a standard SSM’s output is corrupted if any input token is invalid. A partial mechanism [23] requires at least one valid token to produce a valid output. These properties are the design principles of our mask-aware framework. They can be used to create a mask-aware solution to any task, allowing the application of SSM to problems with partially invalid input data. 

3.2 Partial Vision Mamba 

The Partial Vision Mamba (PVM) is our novel architectural block designed to replace standard Mamba layers for processing inputs with any form of invalidity. The VM backbone [57] is composed of a Patch embedding step (typically a linear layer), with the goal of describing each patch in the image by a one-dimensional feature vector (the SSM input tokens), followed by the SSM. Fig. 1 illustrates the phenomenon of using VM when the input contains irregularly shaped invalid data, with the patch split resulting into three type of patches: (i) when all the data contained is valid ( e.g ., only foreground in the image, marked as green in Fig. 1), (ii) when only invalid data is contained ( e.g ., hole, marked as red), (iii) when we find valid data but also some invalid pixels ( e.g ., patches placed at the border between foreground and hole, marked as yellow). Two types of invalidity appear: inner-patch, where partially valid patches result in corrupted, invalid tokens (grey in Fig. 1), and inter-patch (where invalid to-kens are processed normally by the SSM along with valid tokens, resulting in 6 I. The inner-patch invalidity is solved by modifying the Patch embedding module to make it mask-aware. This implies the use of a Partial Patch projection. We replace the Linear layer that performs the projection by a Partial Linear layer, which consists of a regular Linear layer preceded by a mean padding on the invalid positions. This trick allows using the same mechanism as in the PConvs approach [28]. Hence, the resulting tokens from partially valid input patches are now considered valid, and only valid and invalid tokens are present at this point. This operation results in two sequences: a sequence of feature tokens t ∈ RL×D , and a corresponding token-level validity mask mtoken ∈ { 0, 1}L, where L is the number of tokens and 

D is their dimension. To avoid the inter-patch invalidity that would arise during the Mamba SSM processing, we explicitly replace the invalid token by some rep-resentation that the SSM should learn to identify (called masked tokens, purple in Fig. 1). We use a learned masked token (inspired by BERT [7]). We explicitly remark that this PVM design provides a structural guarantee of insensitivity to invalid placeholder values, which become mathematically irrelevant to the valid output stream. This was empirically verified during development. 

3.3 PVM Block Functionality 

The PVM blocks alone cannot guarantee the proper processing of invalid data. Some additional requirements are necessary: the input data must contain at least Mask-aware inference with State-Space Models 7

one unit of valid data, and the validity of the data received by the PVM block must be identified by the validity mask. The layers preceding the PVM block must handle these requirements. For instance, a Convolutional layer preceding a PVM block should be replaced by its equivalent PConv, which is able to handle the invalid data at the input and update the validity mask accordingly. Note that, although the PVM block cleans out all the input invalid data, the presence of skip connections around a given block can re-corrupt the resulting feature maps after element-wise operations or concatenation (Sec. 3.1). The PVM block’s role is determined by its position in the network: when used with a residual connection (x′ = x + P V M (x), where x are the input and x′ the output features), the PVM block can update the full feature map, but only features in the input valid positions will be used after the merge (the rest must be discarded), thus producing a still partially valid feature mask and requiring the input validity mask min to be retained. When used without a residual connection, the PVM block ingests a sparse sequence and outputs a dense, fully-valid sequence. Often, a VM backbone module is formed by N residual VM blocks. When this happens with PVM blocks, the first N − 1 act as partially valid feature extractors and the last one as a valid feature completion module.

Inpainting. As inpainting is the original scope of PConvs [27], we select VM-UNet [54] as a Mamba-based equivalent. We modify its decoder with an inpaint-ing head as our mask-unaware baseline. Our aim is not to propose a state-of-the-art inpainting architecture, but to quantify the benefit of our framework. We propose two mask-aware models. The first, PVM-UNet-1, acts as the direct, minimal mask-aware equivalent to VM-UNet. It replaces the VM-UNet’s Patch Embedding with our Partial Patch Embedding (see VM and PVM in Sec. 3.2) and swaps only the first encoder Visual State-Space (VSS) block (Sec. 3.2) with a Partial VSS (PVSS) block. These blocks are the SSM part of VM and PVM respectively (as the patch embedding is already applied). All other VSS blocks remain. The second model, PVM-UNet-N (Fig. 3), demonstrates the potential of our framework by enabling more complex, mask-aware designs. It modifies PVM-UNet-1 by adding skip connections between encoder scales, using a Partial Average Pool layer (described in Sec. 3.1) to align feature dimensions. According to our design principles (Sec. 3.1), these skip connections require all encoder VSS blocks to be replaced by PVSS blocks. As the final PVSS block feeds the decoder non-residually, the decoder remains unchanged with standard VSS blocks. 

Image Classification with invalid data. For this task, we aim to solve a regular Image Classification problem with the presence of invalid data (properly identified) at the input. To this end, we simply select PlainMamba [48] as a state-of-the-art Mamba-based image classification model. For our mask-aware solution we modify it to create our novel PVM-Cls (Fig. 4), by replacing the regular VM block by a PVM block, as well as the Patch embedding ( i.e . a convolutional layer) by a Partial Patch embedding ( i.e . a PConv). Finally, a Partial Average Pooling (see Sec. 3.1) layer is added to replace the originally proposed Global Pooling layer. As it works at a global level (receptive field), the features become completely valid after this step. The final Classification head is retained.

4.1 Depth Completion 

We use the KITTI-3D Depth Completion benchmark [12]. This task is an ideal real-world testbed as the input LiDAR is inherently sparse, providing a natural, irregular validity mask ( mi,j = 0 for missing pixels) without the need for syn-thetic occlusions. We use the official dataset split and evaluate in the developer’s test set, where the Ground Truth is available. We evaluate PVM-DC using only the sparse depth input (RGB-unguided). We compare it to its mask-unaware equivalent, VM-DC. We configure both mod-els equally, with 6 RSSB/RPSSB blocks in the DFE, with 2 VMM/PVMM blocks each. We use a pixel-wise Charbonnier Loss on the valid regions of the output, like [32]. We report the root mean square error (RMSE) and mean absolute error (MAE) on the valid pixels of the ground truth.

# 5 Discussion 

Our results demonstrate that the PVM block, along with its careful integration into an architecture by following the design principles outlined in Sec. 3.1, is an effective and generalizable architectural mask-aware solution for handling invalid data, with no constraints on the mask shape. Across all tasks, we observe that our mask-aware "Partial" models significantly outperform their mask-unaware counterparts. This confirms that although a standard Mamba model possesses some ability to handle partially invalid inputs in a mask-unaware manner, it re-mains suboptimal. By architecturally enforcing mask-awareness, our PVM-based models become able to achieve higher performance and more robust solutions. While we have proposed solutions for three distinct tasks, the design princi-ples are broadly applicable. We believe the results of PVM are promising and can be intuitively applied to many other scenarios where modern, efficient SSMs must interface with sparse, occluded, or otherwise incomplete real-world data. 

# 6 Conclusions 

In this work, we introduced Partial Vision Mamba (PVM), the first Mamba-based architectural component designed to perform robust inference on inputs with arbitrarily shaped invalid data. We also formalized a set of validity proper-ties that serve as a framework for designing mask-aware models using PVM. We validated our contributions across three diverse tasks, demonstrating that: (i) Our PVM-DC model benefits from the mask-aware design and surpasses (23%) its non-partial counterpart. (ii) For Image Inpainting, we demonstrate improve-ment by using a Mamba-based architecture and using our PVM novel compo-nent. (iii) On Image Classification with invalid data, the use of PVM yields a remarkable improvement. For future work, given PVM’s demonstrated generalizability, we aim to ex-plore more tasks within our mask-aware framework. We will specifically target other depth-related tasks, such as depth enhancement, by developing optimal PVM-based models, following the mask-aware principles studied in this work.
