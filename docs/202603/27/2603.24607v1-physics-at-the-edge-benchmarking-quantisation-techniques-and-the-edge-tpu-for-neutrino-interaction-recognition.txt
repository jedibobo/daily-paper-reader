# Physics at the Edge: Benchmarking Quantisation Techniques and the Edge TPU for Neutrino Interaction Recognition 

## Stefano Vergani 1,2* , Hilary Utaegbulam 3, Michael Wang 4, Leigh H. Whitehead 2,Arden Tsang 1, Lorenzo Uboldi 51Department of Physics, King’s College London, London, UK. 

2Cavendish Laboratory, University of Cambridge, Cambridge, UK.

4Fermi National Accelerator Laboratory, Batavia, IL, USA. 

5Department of Physics, Politecnico di Milano, Milan, Italy. *Corresponding author(s). E-mail(s): stefano.vergani@kcl.ac.uk; 

Abstract 

This work presents a comprehensive benchmark of different quantisation techniques for convolutional neural networks applied to neutrino interaction recognition. Utilising simulation for a generic liquid argon time-projection chamber, models are quantised and then deployed on the Google Coral Edge TPU. Four Keras models are tested, and accuracy is measured across two different pipelines: using post-training integer quantisation and quantisation-aware training. Inference speed is benchmarked against an AMD EPYC ™ 7763 CPU and NVIDIA A100 GPU. A study of the energy consumption is also presented, with attention to potential costs and environmental issues. Results show that, among the four models tested, accuracy degradation is limited and, in particular, Inception V3 presents almost no accuracy degradation across the two quantisation and deployment pipelines. The speed of the edge TPU is comparable to that of the CPU, and one order of magnitude slower than the GPU. Moreover, the energy consumption of all models deployed on the edge TPU is several orders of magnitude lower than that of the CPU and GPU. In the energy consumption-latency parameter space, CPU, GPU, and edge TPU performances can be clearly separated. This paper explores possible future integrations of edge AI technologies with neutrino physics. 

Keywords: Edge TPU, Deep Learning, Neutrinos, Quantisation, AI, Edge AI, Google Coral 

# 1 Introduction 

Over the last decade, artificial intelligence (AI) has played a revolutionary role in science, including particle physics [1, 2]. In particular, advanced convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have reshaped the way pattern recognition is per-formed in experiments, allowing for accuracy and latency 1 never obtained before [3]. This includes AI for fast triggering, where rare signatures need to be detected in very short timescales [4, 5]. Traditionally, advanced AI algorithms have been trained and deployed on graphics processing units (GPUs)[6]. They provide great performance in

This trans-lates into greater electrical power required by the device and the cooling system, and unsustainable environmental heating. The impact of AI on the environment is considered one of the biggest issues of this decade [10–16], and solving this remains on the priority list of many strategic white papers [17, 18]. Another issue is that they cannot be mounted close to the detector or source of data, but are often stored in datacentres away from it; this is the case for experiments at the Fermi National Accel-erator Laboratory (FNAL) [19] and the Conseil Europ´ een pour la Recherche Nucl´ eaire (CERN) [20]. Some attempts have been made to attach GPUs to the experiment, such as in the case of the Large Hadron Collider beauty (LHCb) exper-iment with radiation-tolerant GPUs [21]. Edge AI could be a solution to these problems, since Edge AI devices have very low power consumption [22, 23] and are designed to be deployed directly where data is generated. Examples of their usage can be found in satellites [24], unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) [25], and even medicine [26]. Very recently, physicists have also started to explore the potential of edge AI for future detectors [27]. This work presents the benchmark of various CNNs running on the Edge tensor processing unit (TPU), an edge AI device produced by Google Coral, performing event recognition on a neutrino dataset simulating a Liquid Argon Time Projec-tion Chamber (LArTPC). The concept of the LArTPC was first presented in 1977 [28], and since then it has been used in several successful neutrino experiments such as Imaging Cosmic And Rare Underground Signals (ICARUS) [29], Argon Neu-trino Teststand (ArgoNeuT) [30], Liquid Argon In A Testbeam (LArIAT) [31], and Micro Booster Neutrino Experiment (MicroBooNE) [32]. The setup of a LArTPC comprises a cryostat filled with liquid argon (LAr) kept at 87 °K, at least one cathode and anode separated by an electric field, allowing ionisation electrons to drift towards the anode. The anode is typically instrumented with wire planes or pixel detectors. We have cho-sen to simulate a LArTPC detector because they represent the state-of-the-art technology, and will continue to play a central role in next-generation neutrino detectors, such as Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment (DUNE) [33]. 

# 2 Edge TPU 

Edge computing has become increasingly useful for experiments where inference must occur close to the data source, and under power and latency budgets [34]. The Google Coral Edge TPU is an application-specific integrated circuit (ASIC) designed specifically to accelerate machine learn-ing inference at the “edge”, close to the sensors that produce the data, as opposed to conducting inference at centralised data centers [35]. Unlike GPUs, which require significant power (tens to hundreds of Watts), the Edge TPU is optimised for high efficiency. It is capable of performing 4 TOPS (trillion operations per second) while consuming approximately 2 Watts [34, 36]. The core architecture of the Edge TPU relies on a systolic array design. Data flows through a grid of arithmetic logic units that are able to perform parallel matrix multiplications, the fun-damental operation of CNNs, without frequent access to memory [35]. However, the Edge TPU architecture imposes specific constraints on the neural networks it can support. To achieve high throughput, the device operates exclusively on 8-bit unsigned integers (uint8 ). Machine learning (ML) models are typi-cally trained using 32-bit floating-point precision (float32 ), which means models must undergo a conversion process known as quantisation to convert their operations from float32 to uint8 .This requirement necessitates the use of either post-training quantisation (PTQ) or quantisation aware training (QAT) (as explained in Section 3) to map the model parameters to the uint8 format required by the hardware [37]. 

# 3 Neural Network Quantisation 

Quantisation is the process of mapping amodel’s floating-point weights and activations (e.g., float32 or float16 ) to lower-precision numeric formats, e.g., uint8 , to reduce memory footprint and computational cost. It is usually applied either via PTQ or QAT. Both techniques can be particularly valuable for deploying mod-els on resource-constrained devices, such as edge devices, by enabling faster inference and lower 2power, typically at the cost of a small accuracy drop if properly configured [38]. 

## 3.1 Post-Training Quantisation 

PTQ converts a pre-trained floating-point model to a lower precision representation without fur-ther training. The process involves a calibration step where a small representative dataset is passed through the model to characterize the distribution and range of the model’s weights and activa-tions. These statistics are used to determine the appropriate tensor and channel scaling factors and zero point values for quantization. The model’s 

float32 or float16 weights are then converted to 

uint8 or another low precision format using these parameters. 

## 3.2 Quantisation-Aware Training 

QAT incorporates quantisation effects directly into the training pipeline by simulating low-precision operations during the forward pass. Fake-quantisation operators are inserted into the network to mimic the behaviour of low-precision operations in the forward pass. These operators are placed after layers that produce weights and activations. The process starts with a pre-trained floating-point model where QAT acts as a fine-tuning step with quantisation simulation. During QAT, the backward pass uses full-precision gra-dients to update weights, so that the model can adjust its float32 weights to minimise the loss despite the simulated quantisation during the forward pass. 

# 4 Neutrino Dataset 

GENIE v3 00 06 [39] was used to produce neu-trino interactions with a uniform flux distribution in the range 1–4 GeV/ c2, roughly approximat-ing the main part of the DUNE neutrino flux distribution [33]. Events were produced in three broad categories: charged-current (CC) νμ, CC νe,and neutral current (NC). The final-state parti-cles produced in the neutrino interactions were passed through a simple LArTPC detector sim-ulation [40] based on Geant4 v4 10 6 [41]. The simulated detector is a monolithic cuboid of liq-uid argon of dimensions (x ,y,z) = (5 m , 5 m , 5 m), where x is the electron drift direction, y is the height, and z is the neutrino beam direction. The 3D energy deposits produced by the ionising par-ticles are projected onto three two-dimensional views ( u, v, and w) in the yz -plane, similar to the readout planes planned for DUNE [42]. The planes are oriented at ±35.9 ◦ and 0 ◦ to the verti-cal. Images of size 224 ×224 pixels were produced for each of the readout views after cropping the images to cover an area of size of 224 cm ×224 cm around the true interaction vertex, such that each pixel represented a 1 cm ×1 cm area of the readout plane. The sample of simulated neutrino interac-tions is a subset of the sample used in Ref. [43]. A total of 22,338 neutrino interactions are used in this work, split into a training set of 17,338 events (CC νμ: 6,328, CC νe: 4,742, NC: 6,268) and a test set of 5,000 events (CC νμ: 1,149, CC νe: 2,696, NC: 1,155). Figure 1 shows three neutrino interactions from the dataset: CC νμ (left), CC νe (middle), and NC (right). The events are shown in the w

readout view only for clarity, but the images in the dataset have the three readout views stacked to mimic colour images with ( u, v, w ) instead of (r, g, b ). The CC νμ interactions are typically char-acterised by a long muon track, CC νe events by an electron-induced electromagnetic shower ema-nating from the interaction vertex, and NC events lack either of these features. 

# 5 Model Architectures 

CNNs have historically pursued deeper, wider, and more resolute networks to boost image recognition performance [44], but this quickly becomes com-putationally heavy and harder to optimise [45]. To manage complexity while preserving performance, different families take distinct approaches. 

## 5.1 Residual Neural Networks 

Residual Neural Networks (ResNets) [46] use residual blocks with identity skip connections, which make it easier to train deeper networks. These skip connections help reduce vanishing-gradient problems and improve optimization sta-bility. ResNets can achieve greater depth without the training degradation often seen in plain net-works. ResNetV2 improves the original ResNet design by using pre-activation residual blocks, 3Fig. 1 : Examples of CC νμ, CC νe and NC interactions shown in the left, middle and right panels, respectively. The events are shown in the w readout plane in the ( w, x ) parameter space and the colour scale goes from dark blue (low charge) to cyan (high charge). which support better gradient flow during train-ing [47]. Because of their strong performance and reliability, ResNet-based architectures remain widely used as baseline models. 

## 5.2 Densely Connected Convolutional Networks 

Densely Connected Convolutional Networks (DenseNets) use dense blocks, where each layer connects to every other layer in a feed-forward fashion. Within a dense block, for each layer, the feature maps of all preceding layers are used as inputs, and its own feature maps become inputs to all subsequent layers. This architecture alle-viates vanishing gradients, strengthens feature propagation, encourages feature reuse, and sub-stantially reduces parameter count. Dense block concepts allow for deep scaling and can also be employed outside DenseNets [48]. 

## 5.3 Inception Networks 

InceptionNets [49] circumvent depth scaling by employing multi-branch Inception blocks. These blocks process the same input through parallel convolutional paths and concatenate their out-puts. Larger spatial filters are factorised into sequences of smaller ones to reduce computational cost. InceptionV2 introduces factorised convolu-tions and BatchNorm to reduce computation and improve training, while InceptionV3 further adds label smoothing, refined factorisation, and opti-misation enhancements to increase depth and accuracy. Unlike ResNets and DenseNets, Incep-tion families primarily widen rather than deepen networks [50]. 

## 5.4 Efficient Networks 

EfficientNets [51] do not merely scale depth or width; instead, they use neural architecture search (NAS) to discover an optimal baseline network (B0) which is then compound-scaled—jointly adjusting depth, width, and resolution to balance accuracy and FLOPs. This produces state-of-the-art accuracy at drastically lower computational cost. EfficientNets use MBConv blocks (inverted bottlenecks with depthwise filtering and Squeeze-and-Excitation attention) with stage-wise down-sampling. EfficientNetV2 replaces early MBConv blocks with Fused-MBConv (standard convolu-tions that merge expansion and filtering for faster early-stage training) and incorporates progres-sive resizing and regularisation. This unified scal-ing strategy yields compact, deployment-friendly models with strong accuracy–efficiency trade-offs [52]. Across modern deep learning, the CNNs exam-ined in this study provide foundational building blocks underpinning many more complex archi-tectures. Hybrids such as Inception-ResNet [53] combine different block types to scale networks deeper and wider. In the particle-physics set-ting, this paper demonstrates the effectiveness of quantising these baseline models, suggesting that downstream, more elaborate CNNs built from the same blocks can also be quantised and deployed 4without prohibitive accuracy loss. They were cho-sen as they are available as predefined networks in 

keras , are representative of architectures used in the field, and perform well on the neutrino dataset described in Section 4. 

## 5.5 Training details 

The four CNNs have been trained and sub-sequently fine-tuned using QAT. The result-ing quantised models have been compiled and deployed on the Edge TPU. 

5.5.1 Data Handling 

The four convolutional models have been trained using TensorFlow 2.15 (tf.keras) on the neutrino dataset described in Section 4. Input files are streamed directly from disk to prevent out-of-memory (OOM) issues, and no data augmentation is applied. The images are 3-channel PNGs (three views stacked), with inputs resized to 224 × 224, except for InceptionV3, which uses its canoni-cal input size of 299 × 299. Input pixel values are scaled to [0 , 1]. All models were trained from scratch using the Adam optimiser [54]. The learn-ing rate starts at 1 × 10 −6, together with a

ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler: if validation loss does not improve for 5 epochs, the learning rate is reduced by a factor of 0.1; if validation loss does not improve for 10 epochs, training stops early. A training set of 17,338 events and a test set of 5,000 events are used, as described in Section 4. A batch size of 64 is employed, epochs are capped generously (e.g. 5000 or 50000), and early stopping is driven by validation performance. Efficient-NetV2B0 is the exception, using batch size 12. Note that although ExponentialDecay schedul-ing is available as an alternative, it has not been explored. 

5.5.2 Checkpointing 

Per-epoch checkpoints are saved automatically, and the best checkpoint (based on validation loss) is continuously updated. A copy of checkpoints is also saved in HDF5 (.h5) format for convenience and compatibility. 

5.5.3 Logging and Evaluation 

The training script supports optional real-time logging with Weights & Biases (wandb), including automatic run naming. After training, the script generates a test-set confusion matrix (using scikit-learn, with a Blues colormap and dpi =600), a classification report, and the macro-averaged F1 score. 

5.5.4 Quantization Aware Training 

For QAT, compatibility requires setting 

"TF USE LEGACY KERAS"="1" before import-ing Keras/TensorFlow to ensure TensorFlow Model Optimization (TFMOT) functionality. Selective quantization is applied to Conv2D ,

DepthwiseConv2D , and Dense layers, while Batch-Norm, ReLU, pooling, and padding layers are skipped. When available, a float32 checkpoint is loaded for initialization, after which the quan-tized graph is fine-tuned so scale and zero-point parameters calibrate without large accuracy degradation. Scheduling differs by architecture: Efficient-NetV2B0 uses a per-step warmup over the first 10% of total steps followed by cosine decay, while ResNet50V2, DenseNet169, and InceptionV3 use validation-driven ReduceLROnPlateau with fac-tor 0.2, min lr = 1 × 10 −7, and patience 5. Batch sizes are reduced where necessary for mem-ory: ResNet50V2 retains batch 64 where feasible; DenseNet169 uses approximately ⌊64 /1.4⌋ ≈ 45; EfficientNetV2B0 uses batch 24; and InceptionV3 uses approximately ⌊64 /1.4⌋ ≈ 45 at 299 × 299. The data pipeline uses drop remainder=True 

during training to maintain fixed shapes, enables 

ignore errors and caching during evaluation, and uses explicit evaluation step counts to han-dle occasional decode issues. For checkpointing and serialization, “best” snapshots are saved as weights-only ( .weights.h5 ) for reliability with quantized graphs, while final quantised mod-els are saved as .keras within a TFMOT 

quantize scope so quantization metadata is pre-served. 

5.5.5 Reproducibility, Environment, and Acknowledgment 

A fixed random seed is used for Python, NumPy, and TensorFlow to improve run-to-run repro-ducibility. GPU memory growth is enabled to reduce pre-allocation and avoid OOM. The envi-ronment includes TensorFlow 2.15 (tf.keras), 5Python 3.9.18 , CPU AMD EPYC ™ 7763, and GPU Nvidia A100. 

# 6 Measurements 

After the models have been quantised using the QAT fine-tuning step (as described in Section 3.2) and PTQ (as described in Section 3.1), they have been compiled for the edge TPU using the com-piler version 16.0.384591198 . Subsequently, they have been deployed on the edge TPU using a Linux system and connecting the edge TPU via a USB-A to USB-C cable. Results of the study are described below. 

## 6.1 Accuracy Degradation 

Table 1 and Figure 2 shows the results on balanced accuracy for the models following PTQ and QAT deployment pipelines. The QAT pipeline has an extra intermediate step, which is the quantisation-aware training fine-tuning phase before quanti-sation. Different models appear to have different responses to one or the other quantisation tech-nique, according to their internal structure. 

## 6.2 Processing Time and Power Usage 

Table 2 shows the latency, defined as ms per infer-ence, obtained on all four models before quantisa-tion, after QAT fine tuning, on central processing unit (CPU), GPU, and edge TPU. It can be seen how the GPU provides the fastest execution speed, whilst the edge TPU performs slightly better than the CPU, with DenseNet-169 in particular being three times faster. There are small differences between the speed on the edge TPU obtained via PTQ and QAT, with the former being consistently faster by 1 ms. As described in [43], the function Einf is defined as thermal design power (TDP)*speed, giving a proxy for the energy consumed per infer-ence in mJ. As shown in Figure 3 and Table 3, it can be seen that every CNN presents similar patterns, with the GPU being the process that consumes the most amount of energy and the edge TPU the least, by a significant two orders of magnitude. 

## 6.3 Discussion 

As shown in Table 1 and Figure 2, InceptionV3 appears to be the best performing model, with almost no accuracy loss on both PTQ and QAT pipelines. DenseNet-169 and ResNet-50 V2 show a similar pattern, experiencing an accuracy degra-dation at the quantisation stage and after being compiled for the edge TPU. The accuracy loss when deployed on the edge TPU is more evident in the QAT pipeline. EfficientNet V2B0 expe-riences a significant accuracy drop in the PTQ pipeline. For the QAT case, it shows an accuracy improvement after the QAT fine-tuning step, but a significant accuracy drop after being compiled for the edge TPU. The reason is that this model cannot be mapped directly on the edge device, and some layers need to be frozen, which can cause noticeable accuracy loss. Speed measurements, shown in Table 2 are consistent among models. The edge TPU performs slightly better than the CPU, and slower than the GPU. In terms of energy consumption, the proxy study demonstrates that the edge TPU requires significantly less energy per inference than any other processing unit. It is important to assume that this is a proxy and it compares the worst-case scenarios, when the device is saturated, and the power draw equals TDP during inference. For this reason, this analysis might indicate a lower per-formance for the GPU than it could be. Still, it gives a valuable tool to perform a high-level com-parison between devices. That being said, even considering half of the TDP values, the differ-ence between CPU, GPU, and edge TPU remain significant. From Figure 3, it is evident that the CPU is the worst-performing in the parameter-space energy/latency trade-off. The edge TPU is the least power-consuming option, and the GPU is the fastest option.
