## Abstract 

Learning effective netlist representations is fundamentally con-strained by the scarcity of labeled datasets, as real designs are protected by Intellectual Property (IP) and costly to annotate. Exist-ing work therefore focuses on small-scale circuits with clean labels, limiting scalability to realistic designs. Meanwhile, Large Language Models (LLMs) can generate Register-Transfer-Level (RTL) at scale, but their functional incorrectness has hindered their use in circuit analysis. In this work, we make a key observation : even when LLM-Generated RTL is functionally imperfect, the synthesized netlists still preserve structural patterns that are strongly indicative of the intended functionality. Building on this insight, we propose a cost-effective data augmentation and training framework that sys-tematically exploits imperfect LLM-Generated RTL as training data for netlist representation learning, forming an end-to-end pipeline from automated code generation to downstream tasks. We conduct evaluations on circuit functional understanding tasks, including sub-circuit boundary identification and component classification, across benchmarks of increasing scales, extending the task scope from operator-level to IP-level. The evaluations demonstrate that models trained on our noisy synthetic corpus generalize well to real-world netlists, matching or even surpassing methods trained on scarce high-quality data and effectively breaking the data bot-tleneck in circuit representation learning.

2.1.1 Supervised Learning Approaches. Early works adopt an end-to-end supervised paradigm, directly optimizing the encoder for a specific downstream task label. For graph-level tasks, GNN4IP [ 17 ] and GNN4TJ [ 1] apply graph readout operations to obtain graph embeddings for IP piracy and hardware trojans insertion. While supervised methods have shown promise, they heavily rely on high-quality datasets with annotations. Due to the high cost of labeling, these methods are predominantly confined to small-scale benchmarks like ISCAS-85 [ 18 ] and EPFL [ 19 ], limiting their generalization to complex real-world IPs. 

2.1.2 Self-Supervised Learning Approaches. To circumvent the scarcity of labels, self-supervised learning (SSL) adopts a two-stage para-digm : pre-training on unlabeled data followed by fine-tuning. 

Stage 1: Pre-training. The encoder is trained on a pretext task using self-generated supervisory signals derived from the circuit‚Äôs intrinsic properties: Typically, two mainstream pretext strategies are employed. DeepGate2 [20 ] and DeepGate3 [21 ] utilize logic probabilities obtained from logic simulation, training the model to predict logic-1 probabilities and truth table similarities. Contrastive learning methods like FGNN2 [ 6 ] construct positive pairs via logic rewriting, and align their representations by minimizing contrastive loss. 

Stage 2: Finetuning. The pre-trained encoder is then trans-ferred to downstream tasks and fine-tuned using limited labels. Although this SSL paradigm reduces label dependency, the efficacy of the pre-training stage heavily depends on the diversity of the unlabeled data. This reliance on data diversity proves to be a key bottleneck for robust pre-training. 

## 2.2 Netlist Data Augmentation 

Data augmentation can enhance the scale of the dataset, thereby im-proving the robustness of representation learning. Current method-ologies primarily rely on logic synthesis transformations. For in-stance, FGNN2 [6 ] performs logic rewriting of circuits based on boolean logical equivalence, generating structurally diverse AIGs from the same truth table. Other works generate netlist variants by altering synthesis recipes (e.g., optimizing for area vs. delay) or technology mapping libraries [9, 21, 22]. However, these rule-based methods face a fundamental limita-tion: architectural homogeneity. Because synthesis operates on a fixed RTL implementation, it can only introduce variations at the gate level through different mapping strategies or optimization objectives. Critically, synthesis cannot alter high-level architectural decisions determined during RTL design, such as choosing between ripple-carry and carry-lookahead adders, or selecting different mul-tiplier architectures. This architectural rigidity restricts models from generalizing to the diverse implementations encountered in real-world designs. In contrast, LLMs can regenerate RTL from specifications, explor-ing architecturally distinct implementations of the same functional-ity. By leveraging their extensive knowledge base, LLMs introduce a qualitatively different form of diversity, which encompasses high-level design variations inaccessible to rule-based augmentation. ## 3 Methodology 3.1 Framework Overview 

As shown in Fig. 2, our proposed framework introduces an end-to-end pipeline from automated code generation to downstream tasks, composed of three core stages: (a) Circuit Data Augmentation ,which utilizes an LLM-based pipeline to automatically generate a large-scale dataset of gate-level netlists from input design specifica-tions or RTL; (b) Netlist Representation Learning , where these netlists are transformed into graph representations and a GNN is trained to learn robust structural and functional embeddings; and 

(c) Classification Tasks , where the learned embeddings are finally fed into downstream classifiers to perform node-level tasks like sub-circuit boundary identification and graph-level tasks such as function classification. 

## 3.2 Circuit Data Augmentation 

The Circuit Data Augmentation stage (Fig. 2(a)) forms the foun-dation of our framework, designed to systematically overcome the bottleneck of labeled data scarcity. It generates a large-scale, diverse, and structurally-relevant dataset of netlists from limited ini-tial inputs. This stage is composed of three key components: (1) an LLM-based RTL Generation Framework, (2) a Netlist-level Filtering Mechanism for data quality and relevance control, and (3) an RTL-level Architecture Voting Mechanism to promote generalization capability. 

3.2.1 LLM-based RTL Generation Pipeline. As shown in Fig. 2, our generation pipeline ‚Ď° accepts inputs in two forms: either functional specifications provided directly by users, or existing RTL designs that are first converted into specifications ‚Ď†. Regardless of the input form, the pipeline operates through a unified specification-driven workflow. When starting from RTL, the LLM extracts a com-plete functional specification that captures the design intent. This unified specification-mediated approach, whether starting from user-provided specifications or LLM-extracted ones, serves two critical purposes. First, it enables the LLM to generate architec-turally diverse implementations of the same functionality, which is fundamentally different from rule-based augmentation that can only vary local topology while preserving the same architectural decisions. Second, by having the LLM regenerate RTL from speci-fications rather than directly mutating the code, we naturally in-troduce structural variations that maintain functional relevance without requiring hand-crafted mutation rules. To address the established limitations of LLMs in generating complex, hierarchical designs [ 23 ],we adopt a bottom-up genera-tion approach. For complex designs involving module instantiation, we ensure that each sub-module is generated from its own func-tionally complete specification. To preserve design integrity, we further employ a rule-based methodology to verify the correctness of interface definitions and sub-circuit instantiations. To produce synthesizable code at scale, our framework integrates a synthesizer feedback loop. An additional debug agent is intro-duced to analyze the error log from the synthesis tool and offer suggestions for repairing this design. This process ensures that all generated RTL is synthesizable. For hierarchical designs, this loop verifies that the top-level module synthesizes without any "unresolved" references. 3.2.2 Netlist-level Filtering Mechanism. Recognizing that augmented data can be unreliable, we implement a two-stage filtering mecha-nism at the netlist level to ensure the quality and relevance of our synthetic training data. First, we apply a filter based on synthesis metrics. Using the post-synthesis reports, we identify and discard designs with a cell count that is significantly lower than the average for their functional class. This step effectively removes trivial or over-optimized designs that may have resulted from faulty generation. Second, we filter for structural similarity. Our key observation is that LLM-generated RTL, while potentially containing functional errors, often produces netlists that preserve structurally characteris-tic patterns of the intended function. This property holds regardless of whether the LLM generates from user-provided specifications or LLM-extracted ones, as both generation paths rely on the same underlying architectural understanding. This allows us to retain architecturally diverse variants that maintain structural fidelity to the reference design. ## 3.3 Netlist Representation Learning 

Following the data augmentation phase, this stage (Fig. 2(b)) trans-forms the raw netlist data into high-dimensional embeddings. This is achieved by converting netlists into graphs and training a GNN to learn their structural and functional properties. To isolate and validate that the primary contribution of our framework stems from the Circuit Data Augmentation stage, we deliberately reuse the core representation learning framework introduced by GNN-RE [ 8 ]. This allows for a direct and fair compar-ison in our experimental evaluation. The process consists of three components: graph dataset construction, feature extraction, and GNN training.

## 4 Experiment 4.1 Setup, Evaluation Methods and Metrics 

4.1.1 Implementation Details. We employ DeepSeek-V3.2-Exp and GLM-4.6 as coding models to generate RTL based on the data aug-mentation pipeline. We synthesize all designs using Synopsys De-sign Compiler for the 90nm TSMC technology. For each input speci-fication, we generate a set of synthesizable designs: half are curated using the netlist-level structural similarity filter (Sec. 3.2.2), and the other half are selected via the RTL-level architecture voting mecha-nism (Sec. 3.2.3). To encourage LLM creativity, the temperature is set to 0.8. We utilize the Pytorch implementation of GraphSAINT for scal-able GNN training [ 26 ]. To create an ablation study that isolates the impact of our data augmentation, we adopt the network architec-ture and hyperparameters from our baseline, GNN-RE. All training processes are conducted on an NVIDIA A100 80GB GPU. 

4.1.2 Evaluation Tasks and Metrics. We evaluate our framework on sub-circuit boundary identification and classification task. The performance of our framework is measured by comparing the predictions to the ground-truth labels. We report two key met-rics: F1-Micro and F1-Macro . F1-Micro measures the global ratio of correct predictions, which is mathematically equivalent to accu-racy in our single-label classification setting. F1-Macro computes the unweighted mean of the F1-scores for each class independently, providing a robust measure of performance on imbalanced datasets by treating all functional classes equally regardless of their size.

## 4.3 Evaluation of Generalization

We compared three datasets: the Baseline (original data of GNNRE), LLM-Raw (LLM-augmented without any filtering), and Voting (incorporat-ing the Architecture Voting mechanism). ## 4.4 IP-Level Case Study 

To evaluate the scalability of our approach to real-world system-level scenarios, we selected two open-source System-on-Chips (SoCs) from GitHub: PicoRV32 [ 27 ] and NEORV32 [ 28 ], com-posed of a RISC-V CPU Core and a set of peripherals. To rigorously demonstrate the generalization capability of our data augmentation framework, we enforced a strict domain separation between train-ing and testing. We utilized the augmented data that are generated when both the functional specifications and RTL of the PicoRV32

NEORV32 SoC. The objective of this experiment is to perform IP-level sub-circuit node classification, specifically requiring the model to correctly identify the functional boundary of the CPU Core module within the unseen target SoC.

## 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we propose an end-to-end framework utilizing LLMs as scalable labeled data generators to address the data scarcity in circuit representation learning. Leveraging the insight that netlist topology retains critical semantic patterns despite functional errors, our approach exploits imperfect LLM-generated designs to produce structurally representative and architecturally diverse training data. Comprehensive evaluations demonstrate that models trained on our synthetic corpus competitive with or superior to the ef-fect of those trained on scarce high-quality labels. Critically, this work extends the sub-circuit boundary partitioning task from prior work‚Äôs focus on operator-level to IP-level, validating our method‚Äôs scalability and offering a practical solution to the persistent data bottleneck in circuit representation learning.
