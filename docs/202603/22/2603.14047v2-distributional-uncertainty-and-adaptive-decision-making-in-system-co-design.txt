# Distributional Uncertainty and Adaptive Decision-Making in System Co-design 

Yujun Huang, Gioele Zardini 

Abstract — Complex engineered systems require coordinated design choices across heterogeneous components under multiple conflicting objectives and uncertain specifications. Monotone co-design provides a compositional framework for such problems by modeling each subsystem as a design problem: a feasible re-lation between provided functionalities and required resources in partially ordered sets. Existing uncertain co-design models rely on interval bounds, which support worst-case reasoning but cannot represent probabilistic risk or multi-stage adaptive decisions. We develop a distributional extension of co-design that models uncertain design outcomes as distributions over de-sign problems and supports adaptive decision processes through Markov-kernel re-parameterizations. Using quasi-measurable and quasi-universal spaces, we show that the standard co-design interconnection operations remain compositional under this richer notion of uncertainty. We further introduce queries and observations that extract probabilistic design trade-offs, including feasibility probabilities, confidence bounds, and distri-butions of minimal required resources. A task-driven unmanned aerial vehicle case study illustrates how the framework captures risk-sensitive and information-dependent design choices that interval-based models cannot express. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Embodied intelligence and cyber-physical systems (CPS) tightly couple heterogeneous hardware (sensors, actuators, processors) with software (perception, planning, control), and must be evaluated against multiple, often incomparable, metrics. As a result, designers face nontrivial trade-offs 

across components, subsystems, and system-level objectives, while coordinating stakeholders with diverse expertise. For safety- and mission-critical applications, uncertainty cannot be treated purely adversarially: designers must quantify risk 

(e.g., probability of meeting specifications) and reason about how robustness trades against performance. Moreover, design is rarely one-shot: decisions are made in stages as informa-tion becomes available, so later choices should be adaptive 

to earlier commitments and intermediate observations. Monotone co-design provides a compositional framework for such multiobjective system design [1]–[3]. Each compo-nent is modeled as a design problem (DP): a feasible relation between provided functionalities and required resources, ordered in partially ordered sets. Existing co-design tools, however, treat uncertainty pri-marily via interval bounds on feasible sets [2], [9]. Inter-vals yield worst-case robustness but are too coarse to en-code probabilistic risk (chance constraints, confidence levels, quantiles) and do not natively express multi-stage adap-tive decision policies. This is the first framework that simultaneously handles general distributional uncer-tainty over compositional design models, supports multi-stage adaptivity, and remains closed under the standard co-design compositions. 

A. Related work 

This work lies at the intersection of system-level design for CPS, compositional specification formalisms, and robust and stochastic optimization. A recurring gap in the literature is that methods with rich probabilistic and adaptive uncertainty models typically lack a modular, multiobjective, composi-tional language for heterogeneous subsystems, while compo-sitional design frameworks often rely on coarse uncertainty descriptions that do not support quantitative risk or staged policies. 

1) System-level design in robotics and CPS: System-level co-optimization of hardware and software has long been recognized as central to robotics and CPS [2], including clas-sical multiobjective formulations for mechanism and system design [10] and cross-layer CPS methodologies spanning sensing, computation, and control [11]. Recent work also studies explicit cost–performance trade-offs in probabilistic

planning models (e.g., action-set expansion versus expected performance) [12]. These efforts motivate holistic reasoning, but typically target specific problem classes rather than pro-viding a general compositional language that simultaneously supports heterogeneous components, multiobjective trade-offs, and distributional uncertainty. 

2) Contract-based and compositional design: Assume– guarantee contracts enable modular reasoning by associating components with assumptions on their environment and behavioral guarantees [13], with broad applications from circuits to autonomy [14]. They naturally support open-system composition [15], but require substantial adaptation when subsystems interact through closed interfaces or ex-plicit resource exchanges [13], [16]. Moreover, contracts emphasize satisfaction/refinement of specifications, and only indirectly capture multiobjective trade-offs or quantitative risk; probabilistic extensions typically focus on satisfaction probabilities rather than compositional optimization over heterogeneous resources and functionalities. 

3) Robust, stochastic, and adaptive optimization: Robust and stochastic optimization provide mature tools for uncer-tainty, including chance constraints and distributionally ro-bust formulations [17]–[19]. Multi-stage stochastic program-ming models here-and-now versus wait-and-see decisions via policies over evolving uncertainty, often implemented with scenario trees [20], [21]. Such representations can be restric-tive under continuous and high-dimensional uncertainty, and many approaches focus on a single scalar objective (e.g., expected cost) rather than multiobjective trade-offs across interconnected subsystems. They also commonly abstract away how observations depend on prior design choices. Our goal is to import distributional and adaptive ideas into a compositional, multiobjective co-design setting. 

4) Monotone co-design and uncertainty: Monotone co-design models components as monotone relations between functionalities and resources and composes them via se-ries/parallel/feedback while preserving order structure and enabling optimization [2]. It has been instantiated across autonomous systems and control design [4]–[6] and be-yond [7], [8]. Uncertainty has primarily been treated via interval models that guarantee conservative feasibility [9], but do not encode probabilistic risk or adaptive policies. This paper addresses these limitations by enriching monotone co-design with compositional distributional uncertainty and multi-stage adaptivity. 

B. Statement of Contribution 

The main contributions of this paper are fourfold. First, we introduce probability measures and parametric uncer-tainty over DPs using quasi-measurable spaces, yielding a distributional modeling layer that is closed under the algebra of co-design compositions. Second, we formalize multi-stage design processes in which implementations and specifications can depend on intermediate observations and earlier choices, generalizing interval uncertainty toward an adaptive stochastic-design setting while retaining composi-tionality. Third, we define query operators that map dis-tributional co-design models to probabilistic performance characterizations (e.g., feasibility probabilities, distributions of minimal required resources), enabling risk-aware system design. Finally, we illustrate the framework on unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) co-design with uncertain task profiles and component specifications, showing how distributional and adaptive modeling reveals trade-offs and robustness properties that interval methods cannot capture.

D ISTRIBUTIONAL UNCERTAINTY AND ADAPTIVE DECISIONS IN CO -DESIGN 

Real design processes are more complex than one-shot im-plementation choices under best– and worst–case uncertainty. Different implementations come with parameters implying various system specifications, and these parameters are un-certain in ways that cannot be captured by intervals alone. Moreover, some implementation decisions can be postponed until after part of this uncertainty has been resolved. Consider again the perception sub-system of a UAV with sensor and algorithm components. The designer chooses a sensor from a catalogue (e.g., different camera variants), each posing specifications such as field of view, noise char-acteristics, and failure modes of the system. Manufacturers typically guarantee only distributions over parameters; when a sensor is purchased, one obtains a random sample from these distributions. Similarly, perception algorithms with tunable hyperparameters are characterized by distributions over performance metrics (e.g., detection accuracy), and their performance may depend on the chosen sensor. Crucially, the algorithm and its parameters can often be selected after the actual sensor instance and part of the environment have been observed. Thus, the resulting design process is inherently 

adaptive and involves full probability distributions, not only interval bounds. One could try to reduce distributions to interval bounds by taking confidence intervals, but this loses the quantitative trade-offs between functionality, resources, and risk that practitioners care about. To support such trade-offs in a com-positional way, we move from intervals to distributions over DPs , and from static MDPIs to MDPIs with distributional uncertainty and adaptive decisions .In this section we: (a) Formalize distributions over system performance as distributions over DPs; (b) introduce MDPIs with distributional uncertainty and an adaptive framework based on Markov kernels and re-parameterization; (c) de-scribe queries and observations for these models, including probabilistic versions of classical co-design queries. 

A. Distributions over design problems as uncertain design results 

In deterministic co-design, the performance of components and systems, i.e., the design results for fixed implemen-tations, is described by DPs, encoding trade-offs between functionalities and resources (Definition 15). To model dis-tributional uncertain design, we wish to talk about random 

DPs, i.e., distributions over DPs , in a way that is:  

> •

Compositional : the series, parallel, and feedback oper-ations in Definition 16 remain measurable;  

> •

Semantically expressive : for a random design problem, we can ask for the probability that a given function-ality/resource pair (or countable family of pairs) is feasible;  

> •

Compatible with co-design : deterministic DPs embed naturally as degenerate distributions. A naive approach is to assign a sigma-algebra σ(DP {F , R }) to DP {F , R } and consider ordinary probability measures on it, requiring at least that (i) the composition operations in Definition 16 are measurable, (ii) for each xF ∈ F and xR ∈ R, the feasibility evaluation map ev xF ,x R : DP {F , R } → Bool , dp 7 →  ⟨xF , x R⟩ ∈ dp 

1) Quasi Measurable Spaces and Quasi Universal Spaces: In probability theory, we first fix a sample space Ω

Quasi Measurable Space provide another perspective of probability theory to resolve this issue, where random variables are viewed as first-class citizens and sigma algebras are derived from them. Let’s fix a sample space with random events ⟨Ω, ΣΩ⟩, and denote ΩΩ the set of measurable maps from Ω to Ω.

Definition 21 (Quasi-measurable spaces, quasi-measurable maps, and induced sigma algebras) . A Quasi Measurable Space (QMS) is a pair ⟨A, A Ω⟩ where A is a set and AΩ is a distinguished set of maps Ω → A, called random variables 

on A such that: 1) For every mA ∈ A, the constant map const mA : ω 7 →

B(A) def

Ω being Polish, the induced sigma algebra will be the same as the completion of Borel sigma algebra. One can be tempted to make a QMS A very general by taking all maps from 

Ω to A as random variables, but this makes very few maps from A to be quasi-measurable, just as taking the power set as sigma algebra makes very few maps into A measurable.

6) Confidence bounds on distributions over design prob-lems: Distributions over DPs can be summarized by confi-dence bounds , which generalize interval uncertainty. Because DPs are rarely totally ordered, we distinguish between con-fidence levels for sampling inside a bound and confidence levels for sampling outside a bound. 

Definition 28 (Inner confidence bounds) . Let ⟨μ, α ⟩ ∈P(DP A{F , R }) be a distribution on DPs and p ∈ [0 , 1] .An interval [dp L, dp U] of DPs is an inner confidence bound 

of level p if there exists universally measurable set X ⊆ Ω

satisfying: X ⊆ α−1([dp L, dp U]) and μ(X) ≥ p. If both equalities hold, we call the bound strict .

Definition 29 (Outer confidence bounds) . Let ⟨μ, α ⟩ ∈P(DP A{F , R }) and p, q ∈ [0 , 1] . An interval [dp L, dp U]

of DPs is an outer confidence bound of level p, q if there exist two universally measurable subsets of Ω, X and Y ,satisfying: X ⊇ α−1([ ∅, dp L]) and μ(X) ≤ p; Y ⊇

α−1([dp U, F op × R]) and μ(Y ) ≤ p. If the four equalities hold, the bound is called strict .Informally, an inner bound guarantees the design result to stay between the bounds with at least probability p. An outer bound instead guarantees the probabilities of having design results worse or better than the boundaries will be at most p and q, respectively. The bounds are strict when the probabilities are exact. 

Remark 11 . For a given distribution ⟨μ, α ⟩, inner and outer confidence bounds need not exist, and if they exist they are in general not unique, even when restricted to strict bounds. Under additional structure, for instance, when Ω carries an order and α is monotone, confidence bounds can often be constructed from suitable subsets of Ω (e.g., intervals). 

Remark 12 . Although intervals of DPs can always be com-posed by composing their bounds (Lemma 3), the resulting interval need not be a valid confidence bound due to the non-constructive nature of lifted compositional operations in Theorem 4. However, whenever composition does yield a valid bound, its confidence levels can only deteriorate, by monotonicity of the co-design operations. For instance, when the decomposition of sample spaces in Remark 10 holds, inner confidence bounds always compose to new bounds. Outer confidence bounds are left for future research.

B. Implementations, specifications, and adaptive decisions 

In deterministic co-design, MDPIs represent design pro-cesses: choosing implementations yields deterministic DPs (Definition 17). With distributional uncertainty, we extend this to MDPIs with distributional uncertainty , where design results are distributions over the QUS of DPs, and adaptivity is modeled by Markov kernels and re-parameterization. 

Definition 30 (MDPIs with distributional uncertainty) . Let F

and R satisfy Assumption 1. An MDPI with distributional uncertainty is a tuple ⟨S, I, a ⟩ where S and I are QUSs representing system specifications and implementations, re-spectively, and a is a Markov kernel a : S×I ⇒ DP A{F , R },i.e., a quasi-measurable map a : S × I → P (DP A{F , R }).Even though all uncertainty, including uncertainty in specifications induced from component parameters, could in principle be modeled as randomness in Ω, we keep specifications explicit in Definition 30. This allows us to 

re-parameterize design processes and to express multi-stage adaptive decisions. 

Definition 31 (Re-parameterization of distributional un-certain MDPIs) . Given a distributional uncertain MDPI 

⟨S, I, a ⟩, re-parameterizing it with another Markov kernel 

f : S′ × I′ ⇒ S × I gives distributional uncertain MDPI 

⟨S′, I ′, a ◦P f ⟩.Re-parameterization captures multi-stage adaptive deci-sion processes . At each stage k, specifications Sk and implementations Ik are determined from the previous stage via a Markov kernel ⟨obs k−1, π k−1⟩ : Sk−1 × Ik−1 ⇒ Sk ×

Ik (Fig. 3). It has two components: (a) an observation model obs k−1 : Sk−1 × Ik−1 ⇒ Sk, describing how the environment and specifications evolve; (b) a decision pol-icy πk−1 : Sk−1 × Ik−1 ⇒ Ik, choosing the next implemen-tation (possibly randomly) based on past specifications and decisions. 

Remark 13 . Beyond parameterized policies, one can also consider higher-order objects: the space of Markov ker-nels QUS {A, P(B)} is itself a QUS, and the evaluation map is quasi-measurable. Thus, policies can themselves be included among implementations. In practice, however, parameterized policies (e.g., controllers or design rules with a finite-dimensional parameter vector) are more convenient. 

Remark 14 . Since P forms a symmetric strong monad on QUS [16], its Kleisli category is monoidal. Re-parameterizations can thus be represented as arbitrary string diagrams in this monoidal Kleisli category, like those in Fig. 7, and categorical coherence guarantees that any such diagram corresponds to a well-defined composite Markov kernel. 

Remark 15 . The lifted co-design operations of Theorem 4 and the re-parameterizations of Definition 31 interact to provide two layers of adaptivity: (1) at the policy level ,via the kernels πk; and (2) at the co-design level , via the fact that co-design composition is performed after all relevant uncertainties for the composite have been observed. In interval uncertain co-design (Section III-B), the optimistic and pessimistic DPs are solved independently, so the design choices are allowed to be different in the two cases and are all made on the co-design level , namely after observing all the relevant uncertainties. Formally, these structures can be orga-nized into a 2-category whose 1-cells are uncertain co-design problems and whose 2-cells are re-parameterizations [26]. Overall, compositionality and re-parameterization give de-signers a quantitative, compositional, and adaptive language for uncertain system design: uncertain MDPIs for different components can be defined locally, and then composed and re-parameterized using Markov kernels that represent the adaptive design process. Diagrams like Fig. 8 represent such compositional adaptive design processes compactly.

N UMERICAL EXAMPLE : UNCERTAIN TASK -DRIVEN CO -DESIGN OF UNMANNED AERIAL VEHICLES 

This section instantiates the proposed distributional co-design framework on a task-driven UAV delivery problem adapted from [9], [27]. The goal is to highlight, within a single benchmark, the conceptual gap between (i) de-terministic co-design, (ii) interval uncertainty (best- and worst-case bounds), and (iii) distributional uncertainty with adaptive decision-making. First, Section V-A introduces the co-design model and its decomposition into subsystems. Second, Section V-B solves the deterministic and interval-uncertain baselines and clarifies the interpretation of the op-timistic/pessimistic envelope. Third, Section V-C introduces distributional uncertainty in the task profile and component specifications and computes distributions of optimal cost via Monte Carlo sampling. Finally, Section V-D compares de-sign procedures that differ in which implementation choices may depend on which observations, matching the adaptive-decision language of Section IV-B.

The resulting envelope in Fig. 5 should be interpreted with care. Because the optimistic and pessimistic problems are solved independently , the envelope corresponds to a wait-and-see interpretation: for each realized parameter instance, one is allowed to pick the implementation that is optimal for that instance (so the optimal actuator/battery choices may differ between the two curves). Ω induce DP intervals [α(ωL), α (ωU )] as inner bounds. Because parameters are independent, if each scalar parameter interval is chosen at level ρ, then the rectangular set has probability ρK where K is the number of uncertain scalar specifications included in the rectangle. Equivalently, to target an overall confidence level p, choose ρ = p1/K 

per parameter. The bounds shown in Fig. 6 are obtained by composing such inner bounds along the co-design dia-gram. In this example, the ±5 % per-parameter construction yields ρ = 0.9 and (for K = 30 uncertain scalars) a conservative guarantee p = ρ30 ≈ 0.042 . As expected, this rectangular/product construction is conservative; empirically, the realized minimal lifetime costs fall inside the composed bound with probability much higher than 0.042 (see Fig. 6). 

D. Adaptive decision processes and design results 

The maximally adaptive benchmark above corresponds to postponing all discrete implementation choices until after random parameters are observed. Practical design processes typically restrict which decisions can be postponed and which parameters can be observed before committing. We now compare three adaptivity levels for task-driven UAV design. All three allow recourse decisions inside subsystems (e.g., actuator operating point and battery weight) after uncertainty is realized; they differ only in when the discrete choices (actuator model and battery technology) may depend on observations.

VI. C ONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK 

We developed a compositional framework for co-design under distributional uncertainty with explicit adaptive multi-stage decision-making. Building on monotone co-design, we used Quasi Measurable Space/Quasi Universal Space to define distributions over design problems that avoid classical measurability pathologies while preserving a tractable notion of feasibility. In this setting, the standard co-design inter-connection operations (series, parallel, feedback, union, and intersection) remain compositional and lift naturally to dis-tributions over design problems. We also introduced adaptive design processes via Markov-kernel re-parameterizations, supporting uncountable specification spaces, dependence be-tween specifications and design choices, and risk-aware objectives and constraints. Finally, we formalized queries 

and observations as the interface between the mathematical model and engineering questions, including probability-of-feasibility, confidence bounds, and minimal-resource obser-vations, and illustrated the framework on an uncertain task-driven unmanned aerial vehicle co-design study. Several directions remain. On the algorithmic side, we currently rely on Monte Carlo estimation; an important next step is to develop compositional estimators and probability bounds that exploit the co-design graph structure, including variance-reduced sampling, concentration-based certificates, and deterministic inner/outer bounds when monotonicity is available. For modeling, we will incorporate standard risk measures and constraints, such as chance constraints, CVaR-type objectives, and distributionally robust variants, directly into the query layer, and to study policy synthesis under partial observations. More broadly, the framework suggests an end-to-end learning loop: infer and update specification distributions from data, propagate uncertainty composition-ally, and optimize adaptive policies at the system level.
