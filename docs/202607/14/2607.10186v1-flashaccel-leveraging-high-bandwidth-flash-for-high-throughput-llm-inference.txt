## Abstract 

Large language model (LLM) inference is increasingly lim-ited by the capacity of High-Bandwidth Memory (HBM) in GPUs, as model weights and KV cache grow rapidly. High-Bandwidth Flash (HBF) provides higher capacity than HBM while retaining comparable bandwidth, making it a promis-ing substrate for capacity-constrained LLM inference. How-ever, its inherently high access latency, low bandwidth uti-lization, and lack of support for heterogeneous resource man-agement make it difficult to integrate HBF into GPUs for LLM inference. We present FlashAccel, a co-designed system that enables efficient LLM inference using HBF. FlashAccel inte-grates HBF into HBM-based GPUs, providing architectural support to mitigate access latency. It improves bandwidth utilization through specialized data layouts for both model weights and KV cache, and introduces an HBF-aware storage management layer together with a programming model to or-ganize persistent data in HBF and coordinate heterogeneous memory resources at the system level. Experimental results demonstrate that integrating six HBF stacks into the GPU enables FlashAccel to deliver an average improvement of 2.54 × and 1.93 × in throughput per GPU and energy efficiency over the HBM-only GPU under 100ms latency constraint, respectively.

## 1 Introduction 

The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) is driving inference workloads toward multi-turn interac-tions [ 17 , 66 , 70 ], agents [ 43 , 56 , 64 ], and long-context pro-cessing [ 7 , 8, 36 ]. Two key trends emerge in this evolution. First, model sizes continue to grow following the scaling law [ 29 ], at the cost of drastically increased parameter scale. Second, new workloads increasingly rely on long context lengths, leading to substantial growth in KV cache footprints during inference [ 9 ]. These trends fundamentally increase the memory capacity requirements of inference systems. However, the capacity of High-Bandwidth Memory (HBM) in GPUs has not kept pace with the rapid growth of model size. Figure 1 shows that, although GPU memory capacity has increased steadily over time, the memory footprint of popular models has grown much faster. Today, models can exceed terabytes in size, far surpassing the capacity of a single GPU, which is at most a few hundred gigabytes. Mean-while, as the context length has grown by 4 × on average over the past two years [ 5], the storage overhead of KV cache has increased proportionally by 4 ×.The limited HBM capacity introduces several critical chal-lenges in LLM inference. First, during the memory-bound de-code phase, insufficient memory capacity restricts the batch size, leading to low compute utilization and throughput. Third, due to the limited capacity of a single GPU, storing large models requires multi-GPU systems, which incur significant hardware cost and introduce additional complexity in inter-GPU communication, task scheduling, and fault tolerance. Prior works [ 35 , 52 , 68 ] have leveraged Flash memories to address the capacity bottleneck. They typically store the model weights in Flash and use in-storage computation to exploit internal parallelism. However, these works are pri-marily designed for edge scenarios. Their limited bandwidth and computational capacity are insufficient to meet the re-quirements of high-throughput scenarios that require pow-erful compute devices (i.e., GPUs) and memory devices with both large capacity and high bandwidth. To provide both high bandwidth and large capacity, High-Bandwidth Flash (HBF) has been proposed. HBF is a specialized Flash memory that delivers bandwidth comparable to HBM while retain-ing the high density and non-volatility of Flash [23, 25]. By integrating HBF into the GPU alongside HBM, we can sig-nificantly expand memory capacity to achieve potentially higher decode throughput. However, this capacity advantage does not automatically translate into throughput gains. Several critical challenges prevent leveraging HBF for high-throughput inference. First, Flash suffers from inher-ently high access latency [10 ], which can hurt the end-to-end latency. Second, although HBF provides high peak bandwidth, it requires a high degree of parallelism to reach the peak bandwidth. Inefficient data layout can limit parallel access opportunities and result in low bandwidth utiliza-tion . Third, integrating HBF introduces heterogeneous mem-ory resources, raising the challenge of managing these resources and exposing efficient abstractions to soft-ware .Addressing these challenges requires hardware–software co-design. FlashAccel addresses these challenges through co-design across architecture, data layout, and system soft-ware. Architecturally, FlashAccel mitigates Flash access la-tency with SRAM buffers distributed across HBF dies for data prefetching. It also integrates HBF, HBM, and GPU cores into a unified accelerator to relieve the capacity bottleneck. At the data layout level, FlashAccel addresses the low bandwidth utilization challenge of HBF by designing specialized layouts for weights and KV cache, enabling high parallelism during inference. At the system level, FlashAccel provides an HBF-aware storage management layer to organize persistent data in HBF and a programming model that allows applications to efficiently utilize heterogeneous memory resources. In summary, we make the following contributions. 

• We identify the key challenges of employing HBF for LLM inference, including high access latency that af-fects the end-to-end latency, the gap between peak read bandwidth and effective bandwidth under LLM workloads, and the lack of management for heteroge-neous memory resources. 

• We introduce FlashAccel, an HBF-based accelerator for high-throughput LLM serving. FlashAccel com-bines hardware–software co-design techniques that hide high access latency, enable near-peak bandwidth for weights and KV cache, and efficiently manage het-erogeneous resources. 

• Experimental results show that FlashAccel signifi-cantly improves throughput and energy efficiency by 2.54 × and 1.93 ×, respectively, compared with HBM-only GPU implementations. 

## 2 Background & Motivation 

2.1 LLM Workload 

LLMs consist of multiple stacked transformer layers [ 55 ], each requiring storage for static model weights (shared across all requests) and dynamic KV cache (isolated per request) [ 55 ]. LLM inference consists of prefill and decode phases with dis-tinct computational characteristics [ 33 , 67 ]. Prefill processes all input tokens together, making it predominantly compute-bound. In decode, each request provides one token per step, leading to frequent access to the model weights and KV cache, which makes it memory-bound. As KV cache grows over inference steps, most frame-works [ 33 , 71 ] adopt PagedAttention-style mechanisms [ 33 ]that partition KV cache into fixed-size blocks for fine-grained memory allocation, reducing fragmentation and improving memory utilization. Frameworks also manage KV cache evic-tion. Upon request completion, the computed KV cache is retained in memory for potential reuse by future requests. When memory is exhausted, the framework evicts history KV cache to serve new requests.

A Flash device consists of multiple channels, each of which serves as an in-dependent interface and is shared by one or more dies. Each die contains multiple planes with independent read/write circuits in the circuit die, enabling parallel data exchange be-tween the NAND array and the page buffer. Recent works such as AiF [ 35 ], Lincoln [ 52 ], and Cambricon-LLM [ 68 ] use Flash for LLM inference. They store model weights in Flash and place compute units within Flash to exploit plane-level parallelism. These designs enable edge inference, but are not suitable for high-throughput inference. These designs provide only hundreds of GB/s bandwidth and few TFLOPS of computational capacity, far below modern GPUs. These limitations in both bandwidth and compute capacity directly restrict inference throughput. Moreover, they store only model weights in Flash but ignore the large KV cache footprint. A more promising approach to accelerating LLM inference with Flash is to integrate a Flash device of high bandwidth with the GPU, alleviating its capacity bottleneck. The key to achieving high bandwidth is exploiting plane-level par-allelism. An HBF can be realized by increasing the number of planes per die (e.g., partitioning a die into 16 planes [ 46 ]), adopting HBM-like 3D stacking [ 28 ] to enable parallelism across multiple Flash dies, and leveraging TSVs to provide high-bandwidth interconnects between Flash and GPUs. The main concern with storing KV cache in Flash is en-durance. For example, single-level cell (SLC) Flash typically provides about 100K P/E cycles [ 30 , 39 , 59 ]. Prior works [ 35 ,52 , 68 ] avoid storing frequently updated KV caches in Flash to circumvent endurance limits. However, recent advances in attention mechanisms make storing KV cache in Flash feasible. According to the DeepSeek report [ 15 ], the prefill cluster (approximately 764 GPUs) writes 266 billion new tokens per day 1. Building upon these inputs, the decode cluster (approximately 1,051 GPUs) generates an additional 168 billion tokens. Based on these statistics, each GPU in the prefill cluster writes 22.7TB of KV cache per day, while each GPU in the decode cluster writes 26.9TB of KV cache per day. SLC Flash can provide about 55 P/E cycles per day over a 5-year lifetime. Thus, a 1TB HBF device can sustain about 55TB of data writes per day, which is sufficient for the infer-ence workloads. ## 3 Challenges 

3.1 High Access Latency 

Compared with HBM, Flash exhibits significantly higher access latency. Even with high read bandwidth comparable to HBM, the high read latency of HBF severely degrades end-to-end performance. Using HBF, the GPU receives the first batch of data roughly 4 𝜇𝑠 (tR) after issuing an access request [ 32 ], whereas HBM requires only about 100 𝑛𝑠 [27 ], which is a 40 × gap. For example, a 4k × 4k FP8 GEMV operation can be completed on H200 in approximately 4 𝜇𝑠 . When using HBF instead, it incurs an additional 4 𝜇𝑠 access latency, leading to a 50% end-to-end latency increase. 

3.2 Low Bandwidth Utilization 

Due to the inherent high read latency, Flash exhibits low per-plane bandwidth (e.g., a plane reads a 4KB page in 4 

𝜇𝑠 , 1 GB/s). Achieving high aggregate bandwidth requires exploiting parallelism across thousands of planes. For exam-ple, matching the 4.8 TB/s bandwidth of H200 requires 4,916 planes to read concurrently. We define a hyper page as a logical access unit that aggre-gates one page from every plane. Accessing multiple hyper pages sequentially can maximize inter-plane parallelism and approach the peak bandwidth. When accessing data in HBF, we define plane load as the number of pages that need to be read from a plane. Since a plane serves page reads seri-ally, the overall read latency is determined by the maximum plane load across all planes. If data is unevenly distributed across planes, some planes become idle earlier while oth-ers continue reading, reducing plane-level parallelism and bandwidth utilization. The resulting bandwidth loss directly prolongs memory-bound decode. This issue is particularly evident in weight access. For an HBF of 4.8 TB/s bandwidth, its hyper page size is about 19MB. However, Figure 4(a) shows that a weight matrix in Qwen3-235B is only 12MB. As a result, accessing a single weight tensor activates only a subset of planes, leading to insufficient parallelism and bandwidth utilization. Although accessing multiple weight tensors consecutively increases data size beyond a hyper page, its benefit still strongly de-pends on data layout. Existing inference frameworks usually store weights in the order of variable initialization in the source code, rather than the order they are actually accessed during inference. In case ① of Figure 4(b), when weights A, B, and C are accessed consecutively, the source-code decla-ration order places D between A and B, yielding a maximum plane load of 3. In contrast, case ② of Figure 4(b) achieves a maximum plane load of 2. 3.3 System Resource Management 

The third challenge is system-level support for HBF. Unlike HBM, HBF is non-volatile, so the system must support persis-tent data organization inside HBF for objects such as model weights and KV cache. It also has to coordinate HBF, HBM, and SRAM during execution. Existing approaches do not fit the management of HBF well. Using an HBM-style paging mechanism for data stored in HBF provides direct and low-latency access, but it does not support persistence, because page table mappings are discarded when the program ter-minates. Conventional Flash stacks support persistence but incur metadata and latency overhead from layered address translation [ 1, 10 ]. Moreover, as HBF bandwidth approaches that of HBM, treating Flash as a lower tier behind HBM be-comes increasingly inefficient because it forces unnecessary staging through HBM. Instead, HBF should be exposed as a directly accessible resource for GPUs, while SRAM is used to hide Flash access latency. This requires a storage manage-ment layer for persistent data in HBF and a programming abstraction for accessing heterogeneous memory resources. 

## 4 Hardware Design 

FlashAccel combines HBF, HBM, and GPU cores into a het-erogeneous accelerator for LLM inference. FlashAccel can be realized using either co-located integration (CLI) [ 46 ] or cascaded integration (CSI) [ 23 ], as illustrated in Figure 6(a). CLI places HBF alongside the GPU, partially replacing HBM to increase overall capacity and lower HBM cost. CSI con-nects HBF to the GPU in a daisy-chain manner through the HBM base die, preserving more HBM stacks and thereby maximizing HBM capacity and bandwidth. In this heteroge-neous memory system, HBM and HBF serve complementary roles. HBM handles small, frequently updated intermedi-ate data for its low access latency, while HBF stores large, read-dominated data such as model weights and KV cache. Figure 6(b) shows the HBF stack architecture. Each stack contains 8 Flash dies above a base die, interconnected by TSVs. Each Flash die integrates an array die and a circuit die through hybrid bonding. Following [ 52 ] and [ 46 ], FlashAccel increases plane-level parallelism by scaling each Flash die to 96 planes. Figure 6(c) shows that HBF integrates SRAM buffers in different dies as caches for data prefetch to miti-gate Flash latency. According to [ 52 ], the circuit die contains substantial unused silicon area. FlashAccel places SRAM in this unused area, providing each plane with an SRAM cache. For the base die, in addition to the NAND controller and PHY, we also integrate SRAM cache, increasing the overall SRAM capacity. Each stack has a total SRAM capacity greater than

## 5 Data Layout Design 

Data layout design is the core of FlashAccel co-design, be-cause achieving high HBF bandwidth utilization depends not only on hardware capability, but also on whether data accesses can be arranged to match Flash parallelism. Efficient data layout enables data accesses in HBF to approach peak bandwidth. 

5.1 Weights in HBF 

To expose HBF parallelism, FlashAccel places static weights across all planes in execution order, balancing load and ap-proaching peak bandwidth. Figure 7 illustrates the weight layout strategy. For a given model architecture, the compu-tation graph of each inference step is fixed, which enables offline determination of the execution order. FlashAccel par-titions each operator’s static weights into page-sized units (e.g., A0, A1, and A2) and maps them across all planes and channels in round-robin order. After mapping the current operator, the system continues with subsequent operators according to the execution order, starting from the plane immediately following the last assigned plane of the cur-rent operator. 5.2 KV Cache in HBF 

The layout of the KV cache is more complicated than that of static weights because the active request set changes at every inference step. FlashAccel organizes per-request KV blocks as a unified KV cache. This abstraction enables write, offload, and read policies that balance plane load and preserve HBF bandwidth. At the beginning of a decode step, the unified KV cache can be divided into two types of KV blocks. Resident KV blocks are already stored in HBF from previous steps and have not been evicted. Nonresident KV blocks must be written into HBF from a remote KV cache pool or from newly generated prefill outputs produced on prefill GPUs. As shown in Fig-ure 8 ❶, FlashAccel aggregates nonresident KV blocks into as many full hyper pages as possible and writes them to HBF to improve inter-plane balance. Residual blocks that do not fill a hyper page are placed randomly to avoid hotspots. Balancing incoming writes is insufficient because resident KV blocks may already be skewed across planes. This imbalance reduces bandwidth utilization in the memory-bound decode phase. To address this issue, FlashAccel rebalances plane load after Maximum Plane Load

We first com-pute the average plane load of the unified KV cache and then offload excess pages from overloaded planes to HBM until no plane exceeds the average load (Figure 8 ❷). Benefiting from HBM’s low read latency, the offloaded data can effectively utilize HBM bandwidth. This selective offloading avoids ex-tra HBF writes and thereby preserves Flash endurance. Since the same unified KV cache is typically reused across multiple subsequent decoding steps, the extra offloading overhead can be amortized. During attention computation, conventional kernels load KV cache in parallel across requests. This may trigger concur-rent reads to different requests whose KV blocks happen to reside in the same plane, causing plane conflicts that reduce parallelism. Figure 8 ❸ illustrates the proposed HBF-friendly loading scheme. The GPU performs accesses to HBF at hyper-page granularity to maximize effective bandwidth, and then processes all KV blocks within each hyper page in parallel. A small fraction of KV blocks offloaded to HBM is fetched from HBM in the same step. Using FlashAttention [ 14 ], we com-pute partial attention results per KV block and then reduce them by request to obtain the final result. Each decode step also produces new KV cache for every request. We temporarily buffer them in HBM (Figure 8 ❹). At the end of each step, once the filled blocks are sufficient to form one or more full hyper pages, we flush them back to HBF using the same hyper-page-based write policy (Fig-ure 8 ❺). Since the KV cache generated at each decode step is too small to form a hyper page, directly flushing it to HBF would underutilize bandwidth. The proposed strategy avoids inefficiency and improves bandwidth utilization for KV cache writes and future reads. Besides the above strategies, increasing the KV block size, i.e., storing more tokens in every KV block, can further miti-gate inter-plane load imbalance. Under random plane map-ping, increasing the KV block size significantly reduces the maximum plane load, as shown in Figure 9. A larger KV block spans multiple NAND pages and is therefore distributed across a group of adjacent planes rather than confined to a single plane. This spreads load over multiple planes, weak-ens local hotspots and reduces the amount of rebalancing required. However, the KV block size cannot be arbitrarily large. Since a KV block is typically written to HBF only after it is filled, a larger block size leaves more partially filled data buffered in HBM and increases HBM pressure. For the Qwen3-235B model, a KV block size of 512KB with batch size 256 requires at least 8GB of HBM, imposing high HBM pressure on the CLI architecture. Larger block sizes also in-crease internal fragmentation. FlashAccel uses 256KB KV blocks to balance plane-level skew and HBM pressure. 

## 6 System Design 

The above data layout and access optimizations require sys-tem support at two levels. First, the system has to coordinate HBM, HBF, and SRAM during inference. Second, it must or-ganize persistent data stored in HBF. Accordingly, FlashAccel provides a programming model for heterogeneous resource coordination and an HBF-aware storage management layer for persistent data organization. The programming model ex-poses a unified virtual address space to programs, while the storage management layer maintains the metadata needed to locate persistent objects in HBF. 

6.1 HBF-aware Storage Management 

FlashAccel uses a lightweight HBF-aware storage layer for persistent data placement. It treats model weights and per-request KV cache as persistent objects, partitions them into data units, and maintains per-object indexing metadata to locate them. In conventional Flash storage stacks, locating data typically requires two levels of indirection. The program first uses file-system metadata to obtain a logical address, which is then translated by the Flash translation layer (FTL) in hardware (e.g., SSD controller) into a physical Flash ad-dress. Since Flash does not support in-place updates, the two-level indirection is necessary. When data is modified, the new data must be written to a different physical address, and the FTL is responsible for tracking these remapped ad-dresses. However, the FTL is costly for HBF, as it demands a GB-scale logical-to-physical mapping table [ 22 ] stored in low latency SRAM or DRAM, and incurs extra address trans-lation overhead [ 1 ]. Our key observation is that both model weights and KV cache follow append-only write patterns. FlashAccel therefore eliminates the FTL and directly records the physical address of each data unit in the object’s index-ing metadata. As a result, locating data in HBF requires only the indexing metadata, avoiding the overheads of FTL. By storing the metadata in Flash, we ensure data persistence. To match the block-level erase semantics of Flash, the stor-age layer adopts an isolated block allocation policy. Weight objects and each request’s KV cache object are placed in separate blocks. Because KV cache eviction occurs at request granularity, erasing the KV cache of one request does not affect weight objects or the KV cache objects of other re-quests, thereby avoiding write amplification and reducing Flash wear [11]. -

6.2 Programming Model 

FlashAccel exposes HBF through a programming model com-prising an interface set and a runtime. After the program invokes these interfaces, the runtime maintains page table mappings from logical addresses to the physical addresses of HBM, HBF, or SRAM. This design exposes heterogeneous memory through a unified virtual address space. The pro-gramming model provides different interfaces for accessing weights and KV cache stored in HBF, accounting for distinct access characteristics. To address the HBF access latency challenge, it also provides interfaces that leverage SRAM to hide data access latency. 

6.2.1 Weight Access Interface. Weights are the simplest object in the model because they are static and read-only. FlashAccel introduces a primitive NandMmap interface to ex-pose weight objects stored in HBF to the virtual address space. NandMmap maps a weight object in HBF into the vir-tual address space and returns a logical base address. The program can subsequently access the mapped weights using ordinary load operations on the returned logical address. Upon a NandMmap call, the runtime first reserves a contigu-ous free range in the virtual address space, as shown in Fig-ure 10(a). It then retrieves the metadata of the target object from the storage management layer to obtain the physical page addresses of the mapped data in HBF. Finally, it updates the page table to associate the returned logical address range with the corresponding physical addresses in HBF. Since weights are static, these mappings can be established once during model initialization and reused throughout inference. , Xinyu Wang, Yalong Xue, Xiaotian Sun, Xiaoyu Zhang, Chunmeng Dou, Xueqi Li, and Xiaoming Chen 

6.2.2 KV Cache Access Interface. Unlike model weights, the KV cache is maintained separately for each request rather than shared across requests. FlashAccel stores each request’s KV cache as an independent persistent object in the HBF storage management layer. However, during each inference step, the system must jointly read and write the KV caches of all active requests. To support this access pattern, we in-troduce a group object abstraction. GroupCreate logically combines the KV-cache objects of active requests in the same inference step into a single group object. Based on this ab-straction, FlashAccel introduces three interfaces: GroupMmap ,

GroupWrite , and GroupArrange , to support grouped reads, append-only writes, and offloading, respectively. 

GroupMmap maps a group object into a contiguous logical address space and returns the base address and a read table. The runtime first reserves a contiguous virtual address re-gion, and then builds a plane-distribution table (Figure 11) for all objects in the group and uses it to pack data from different objects into hyper pages whenever possible. These hyper pages are mapped contiguously into the virtual region at hyper-page granularity. The runtime also returns the read table to resolve each offset of the logical base address to its corresponding object identifier. This design allows the program to access the group object through one logical ad-dress range, while transparently serving HBF accesses at hyper-page granularity for high bandwidth utilization. 

GroupArrange performs the offloading mechanism de-scribed in Section 5.2. The runtime computes the load of the group object across different planes and derives the aver-age load. It then migrates data from overloaded planes in HBF to HBM. Finally, it updates the page table so that the physical addresses of the offloaded data point to their new addresses in HBM (Figure 11). This interface alleviates load imbalance across planes and improves overall read bandwidth. 

GroupWrite handles append-only writes to the KV cache, flushing data from HBM to Flash. GroupWrite takes a pointer to data in HBM together with a write table that specifies which object each portion of the data belongs to. Follow-ing the write policy described in Section 5.2, the runtime splits data in HBM into hyper pages whenever possible and distributes them across HBF in a plane-balanced manner. For data smaller than a hyper page, the runtime randomly selects planes for placement. The runtime updates the object metadata in the storage management layer, the page table, and the read table of GroupMmap according to the write table and the plane addresses determined during placement, so that the newly written data remains accessible.

7.4 Write Issues 

Flash suffers from write-side limitations in both bandwidth and endurance. Table 2 shows that the write latency of pro-gram operation ( 𝑡 𝑃𝑟𝑜𝑔 ) is significantly higher than the latency of read ( 𝑡 𝑅 ), which limits HBF write bandwidth. While the read bandwidth reaches 4.6 TB/s for CSI, the peak write band-width is only 245.8 GB/s. The endurance is another concern. Prior works [ 12 , 37 ] demonstrate that reducing retention time from 3 years to 3 days can extend P/E cycles by up to 50 ×. Conservatively, we assume that reducing the retention time can extend the endurance by up to 10 ×, increasing the P/E cycles from 100K to 1M. Even so, it still cannot match the nearly unlimited write endurance of DRAM. However, the wide adoption of new attention mechanisms, like GQA and MLA, reduces write volume compared to multi-head attention (MHA), making it possible to store KV cache in Flash. Across all evaluated configurations, each HBF-based GPU generates and writes at most 276MB of new KV cache per second in the decode node (8 ×CSI running Qwen3-480B under a 100ms SLO, 1138 tokens/s). In addition, based on the input/output length ratio (i.e., ∼2.58:1) in agent work-loads [ 58 ], each HBF-based GPU writes another 712MB of KV cache per second from the prefill node. In total, each HBF-based GPU writes 988MB of KV cache per second, tak-ing only 3.9ms and incurring an additional overhead of 4%. Over a 5-year deployment period, this amounts to 148,570TB of total KV cache writes. With 1152GB capacity and 1M P/E cycles, CSI provides 1,125,000 TBW, sufficient for KV cache storage.

7.6 Energy and Area Analysis 

Flash memory suffers from high read energy consumption. While hybrid bonding can reduce read power, measurements from a real hybrid-bonded Flash prototype still show a read energy of 8 pJ/bit [ 62 ], which is 2.7 × higher than that of HBM3e (2.99 pJ/bit [ 4 ]). After integrating HBF, the overall TDP of CSI and CLI architectures increases 1.31 × and 1.23 ×,respectively. Nevertheless, thanks to the throughput advan-tages of HBF, we observe improvements in tokens per Joule. Figure 17 illustrates the energy efficiency (tokens/J) of dif-ferent accelerators under a 100ms SLO. On average, 8 ×CSI and 8 ×CLI deliver 1.93 × and 1.66 × higher energy efficiency than 8 ×H200, respectively. Our evaluation shows that HBF occupies an area of 149mm 2,which is 1.23 × larger than that of standard HBM3e (121mm 2). This overhead stems from our conservative assumptions based on the design in [ 32 ], which uses only 96 wordline layers. Recent technologies [ 13 , 54 , 61 ] have achieved over 300 wordline layers, significantly improving storage density and reducing area overhead for a given capacity. Using array dies with more wordline layers allows HBF to achieve area parity with the HBM stack. Even with only 96 wordline lay-ers, our HBF design still achieves 6.5 × higher density than HBM3e. 

## 8 Related Works 

Flash Offloading Strategy: Prior works [ 3, 21 , 26 , 50 ] of-fload model weights or KV cache to SSDs to mitigate the DRAM capacity bottleneck, enabling LLM execution without out-of-memory failures. However, these designs are funda-mentally constrained by the low SSD bandwidth, leading to , Xinyu Wang, Yalong Xue, Xiaotian Sun, Xiaoyu Zhang, Chunmeng Dou, Xueqi Li, and Xiaoming Chen 

extremely high decode latency, which limits their practical-ity in datacenter deployment. FlashAccel addresses this issue by increasing plane-level parallelism in Flash and employing dedicated layout optimizations to achieve high bandwidth. 

In-storage Processing Accelerators: These accelera-tors [ 35 , 52 , 68 ] place compute units inside Flash to exploit its internal bandwidth for on-device inference. However, the limited silicon area inside Flash restricts computational ca-pacity, resulting in low throughput. Moreover, they do not support storing KV cache in Flash, leaving capacity chal-lenges unaddressed. FlashAccel instead integrates HBF with GPUs to provide high computational capacity, and enables KV cache storage via SLC Flash and system support for append-only KV cache writes. 

Processing-in-memory Accelerators: Some works [ 19 ,24 , 42 , 47 ] accelerate attention execution using DRAM-based processing-in-memory (PIM) which provides high internal bandwidth but limited computational capacity. They mainly target MHA that has low arithmetic intensity. Recent MLA and GQA impose higher computational demands that are difficult for PIM to sustain. In addition, PIM does not address the inherent low density of DRAM which leads to high cost. By contrast, FlashAccel improves density with Flash and, through GPU integration, supports attention mechanisms requiring more computation. 

## 9 Conclusion 

This work points out the key obstacles that prevent HBF from translating its capacity advantages into LLM-serving performance, and addresses them through co-design across architecture, data layout, and system software. FlashAccel introduces an SRAM cache at the architectural level and a prefetch interface at the system level to mitigate the high access latency of Flash. A specialized data layout fully lever-ages plane-level parallelism for weights and KV cache to improve bandwidth utilization. An HBF management layer and a programming model address the management and usage of heterogeneous memory resources. Experimental re-sults show that FlashAccel achieves 2.54 × higher throughput than HBM-based GPU.
