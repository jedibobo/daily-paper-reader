# GelNeuro: A Sensing-Computing Integrated Neuromorphic Tactile System for Texture Recognition 

Luoyang Bian, Xinpan Meng, Zhenghua Ma, Houcheng Li and Long Cheng, Fellow, IEEE 

Abstract —Neuromorphic visuo-tactile sensing offers a promis-ing paradigm for low-latency and low-power robotic perception. However, existing systems still rely heavily on a host computer for event readout, preprocessing, or relaying prior to chip infer-ence. This paper presents GelNeuro, a fully integrated sensing-computing visuo-tactile system that directly pairs a GelSight Mini-based optical tactile front end with the Speck2f neuro-morphic system-on-chip (SoC). Contact-induced marker motions are captured as dynamic vision sensor (DVS) events and routed through the on-chip network to a spiking convolutional neural network (SCNN) classifier. To mitigate accuracy degradation during 8-bit deployment, a hardware-aware weight clamping strategy is introduced. Evaluated on a 15-class natural texture recognition task, hardware-in-the-loop testing on the physical chip achieves a 96.3% accuracy within an 80 ms inference window. Notably, the system consumes only 19.6 mW of board-level active power-over three orders of magnitude lower than conventional CPU/GPU baselines on the same benchmark. Gel-Neuro also exhibits robust generalization across unseen contact depths, demonstrating the viability of direct sensor-to-chip tactile recognition on edge neuromorphic hardware. 

Index Terms —Neuromorphic tactile perception, in-sensor com-puting, spiking convolutional neural network (SCNN), visuo-tactile sensor, texture recognition. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

# TACTILE sensing provides high-fidelity local contact in-formation, including fine surface geometry and incipient slip, that is inherently difficult to infer from vision alone yet remains essential for dexterous robotic manipulation in cluttered or occluded environments [1]. Texture recognition serves as a rigorous benchmark for tactile systems because it simultaneously demands high spatial resolution to capture intricate surface profiles and exceptional temporal resolution to resolve transient contact dynamics [2]. When incorporating tactile feedback into closed-loop control, both latency and operating power directly dictate the available reaction window and the viability of edge deployment on resource-constrained platforms [3]. Dynamic vision sensors (DVS), or event cameras, offer a paradigm shift by asynchronously reporting pixel-level bright-ness changes with sub-millisecond temporal resolution and minimal data redundancy [6]. Integrating a DVS into a visuo-tactile front end allows the resulting event stream to preserve the sparse, high-frequency spatiotemporal structure of contact-induced marker motions, which can be naturally decoded by spiking neural networks (SNNs) through bio-inspired, spike-based representations [7]. Neuromorphic processors are purpose-built to exploit this sparse spike traffic for ultra-low-power computation, where event-driven neuron updates eliminate repeated, power-hungry dense-frame operations [8], [9]. These compelling advantages have motivated pioneering neuromorphic visuo-tactile sensors, such as NeuroTac [10], alongside recent exploratory studies evaluating tactile tex-ture recognition on Loihi2 and Speck platforms [11], [12]. R ELATED WORK 

A. Visuo-Tactile Sensing Hardware 

Vision-based tactile sensors infer contact deformation by imaging markers or surface patterns in compliant elastomers, and have become a common route to high-spatial-resolution tactile sensing. Representative systems include the GelSight series [14] and the TacTip series [16]. Compact designs such as DIGIT [17] and GelSlim [18] have further improved integra-tion with robotic hands. Most of these systems, however, use conventional frame-based cameras, whose fixed frame rates limit their ability to resolve fast contact events such as slip onset and high-frequency friction. Event-camera tactile sen-sors have therefore been developed to preserve asynchronous temporal information at the sensing front end. Systems such as Evetac [19] and GelEvent [20] demonstrate that event-based optical tactile sensing can capture high-frequency contact dynamics. However, in many pipelines, the event stream is still accumulated into frames or pseudo-frames and processed by conventional convolutional neural networks (CNNs) [21]. This frame-based treatment discards part of the sparsity and timing structure that make event data attractive for low-latency tactile processing. 

B. Neuromorphic Tactile Perception 

Several studies have applied SNNs to tactile perception, motivated by the compatibility between spike-based represen-tations, event-driven sensing, and neuromorphic processors. Early work mainly used non-optical tactile arrays. Taunyazov et al. achieved fast texture classification with piezoresistive arrays and SNNs [22], [23]. Rongala et al. developed neu-romorphic fingertips inspired by human mechanoreceptors [24], Lee et al. demonstrated a neuro-inspired artificial pe-ripheral nervous system for scalable electronic skins [25], and Rasouli et al. reduced power consumption by combining tactile sensing with extreme learning machines [26]. Although these systems exploit temporal spike features, electrode-array implementations remain limited by spatial resolution and wiring complexity as the array scale increases. In optical tactile sensing, an event-driven perceptual framework was used for slip detection with the SLAYER algorithm [27]. NeuroTac was later used to compare spike coding strategies for texture recognition [10]. More recently, real-time texture classification was demonstrated with NeuroTac on a Loihi2 simulation platform [11], and neuromorphic tactile inference was explored on the physical Speck2f chip [12]. These systems advance neuromorphic tactile processing, but the sensing front end is still generally connected to the neuromorphic processor through a host computer for event readout, preprocessing, or spatiotemporal relay. The resulting sensor-host-chip pipeline leaves the direct sensor-to-chip tactile architecture largely unaddressed.

A. GelNeuro Design and Operation 

The proposed GelNeuro combines an elastomeric optical tactile front end with the Speck2f near-sensor neuromorphic SoC in a directly interfaced visuo-tactile module. Following the optical layout of Evetac [19], the tactile front end uses a GelSight Mini marker-gel module, lateral illumination from a monochrome COB LED strip, and the built-in 128 × 128 

-pixel DVS of Speck2f as the imaging core [29], as shown in Fig 2(a). The focal setting and camera distance were selected so that the DVS field of view covers the gel surface and each internal marker occupies sufficient pixels for event generation. A custom 3D printed housing fixes the camera, gel, and light source. During contact, microscopic marker motion produces local brightness changes, from which the DVS outputs asynchronous events containing spatial location, polarity, and timestamp information ( x, y, p, t ). The hardware components of GelNeuro are detailed in Table I. During inference, the DVS output is not relayed through a host computer before reaching the neuromorphic processor. The raw asynchronous event stream first passes through an on-chip preprocessing module for spatial region of interest (ROI) cropping. The resulting sparse event stream is then routed through the network-on-chip (NoC) to the on-chip spiking con-volutional processing cores without frame buffering. Training is still performed offline on a personal computer (PC), but the network and deployment steps are constrained by the on-chip event representation and fixed-point arithmetic. In hardware inference, the front-end event stream is processed directly by the chip, forming a sensor-to-SCNN tactile pipeline without host-computer preprocessing. 

B. Texture Dataset Construction 

The dataset contains 15 natural texture materials, including several smooth textures with similar surface appearances, to assess discrimination of fine texture cues, as shown in Fig. 2(d). For data collection, the GelNeuro sensor was mounted on the end-effector flange of a Franka Emika robotic arm, as illustrated in Fig. 2(b). For each sample, the robot pressed the sensor normal to the texture surface to a prescribed depth and then slid at 20 mm/s. Each sliding motion lasted 1000 ms and was treated as one sample. In preliminary trials, we observed that indentation depths above 0.5 mm tended to damage the gel; the maximum depth was therefore limited to 0.5 mm. The training set was collected at three indentation depths (0.1 mm, 0.3 mm, and 0.5 mm) and three sliding angles relative to the texture axis (0 ◦, 45 ◦, and 90 ◦). For each texture, 15 samples were recorded for each depth-angle combination, giving 135 training samples per class and 2025 samples in total. The test set was collected independently and divided into two groups. The in-distribution (ID) test set used the same three depths as the training set to evaluate baseline recognition performance, with 27 samples per class and 405 samples in total. The out-of-distribution (OOD) test set used two intermediate depths, 0.2 mm and 0.4 mm, which were excluded from training to evaluate generalization to unseen contact depths, with 18 samples per class and 270 samples in total. 

C. Offline Training and Deployment 1) SCNN Topology and Offline Training: For SCNN train-ing, the recorded tactile events are converted into a compact spatiotemporal tensor before being passed to the network. The event stream is cropped to a 100 × 100 region of interest (ROI) centered on the marker deformation area, excluding peripheral events outside the active contact region. For offline optimization, the cropped events are accumulated in 1 ms bins to form an input tensor of size (2, T , 100, 100), where the 2 channels denote event polarity and T is the selected temporal window. This binning step provides the discrete time steps required for training with surrogate gradients [31] and backpropagation through time (BPTT) [32], but it is not used to reconstruct image frames. During on-chip inference, events passing the same ROI are delivered asynchronously through the Speck2f pipeline to the processing cores. The network uses non-leaky integrate-and-fire (IAF) neu-rons [33], which retain membrane potential over the short inference window and are suitable for sparse tactile events. To fit the nine physical processing cores on Speck2f, we used a compact topology with 5 convolutional layers and 1 fully connected (FC) readout layer, and removed bias terms to match the chip computational graph and routing constraints. At each time step, the two polarity channels form two 100 × 100 

event maps. These maps pass through 5 convolutional layers with kernel size 4 × 4, stride 2, and padding 1. The channel dimensions are 16, 32, 64, 64, and 64, while the spatial resolution is downsampled from 100 × 100 to 3 × 3. To improve robustness to frictional jitter and thermal noise during sliding, a 10% random input mask is applied to the binned event tensor during training. The network was implemented in Sinabs and optimized with the surrogate-gradient BPTT formulation described above, using 5-fold cross-validation to reduce overfitting. Standard Kaiming ini-tialization can produce little firing activity in early SCNN training, which weakens gradient flow through deeper layers. Therefore, we use an over-driven initialization strategy, in which Kaiming-initialized weights are multiplied by 2 to increase the initial membrane drive and firing activity. A dropout layer (rate = 0.3) is used before the FC readout. Classification is performed by rate coding [34], selecting the output neuron with the highest accumulated spike count. 

2) Quantized Deployment and On-Chip Inference: For de-ployment on Speck2f, the 32-bit floating-point weights learned offline must be converted to the chip’s signed 8-bit range, [-128, 127]. Direct post-training conversion is sensitive to large-magnitude weights, since a small number of outliers can enlarge the scaling range and reduce the effective resolution of the remaining weights. Therefore, we apply hardware-aware synaptic weight clamping during training. After each BPTT update, every trainable weight is symmetrically clipped to 

[−c, c ]. Before deployment, the clipped weights are discretized by the Sinabs/DynapCNN toolchain to the integer representa-tion used by the chip. This deployment-time mapping can be written as

After quantization, the network is loaded onto Speck2f for hardware-in-the-loop inference. The on-chip inference window can be configured independently and is denoted as Tinfer . Within this window, the readout layer continuously integrates spikes from the 15 output neurons, and the neuron with the largest spike count determines the predicted texture class. 

D. Evaluation Metrics and Protocols 1) Temporal Performance Metrics: Preliminary analysis showed that tactile deformation events caused by sliding friction are concentrated within the first 100 ms of contact. We therefore evaluated temporal performance in two settings. In the matched-window setting, the input samples were tem-porally cropped to 8 lengths from 20 ms to 150 ms, and separate models were trained and tested at each length. This experiment measures the effect of available event duration on texture classification. In the early-inference setting, the model trained with Ttrain = 80 ms was deployed on Speck2f. Since the on-chip SCNN updates its membrane states and output spike counts as events arrive, the readout can be stopped at a prescribed Tinfer without waiting for the full training window. We therefore shortened Tinfer from 80 ms to 20 ms, forcing the classifier to make a decision from only the initial segment of contact. For very short windows, some samples do not generate any readout-layer spike. We therefore report the silent rate ,defined as the proportion of samples with no output spike at the readout layer, together with classification accuracy. 

2) Cross-Depth Generalization: To evaluate generalization across contact depth, the test set is divided according to whether the indentation depth was used during training. The in-distribution (ID) group contains samples at 0.1 mm, 0.3 mm, and 0.5 mm, while the out-of-distribution (OOD) group contains the held-out intermediate depths of 0.2 mm and 0.4 mm. Because depth changes the contact patch and the density of deformation-induced events, this split evaluates a physical domain shift rather than a random resampling of the same contact condition. Accuracy is reported separately for ID and OOD depths to test whether the trained SCNN preserves texture-discriminative information under unseen indentation depths. 

3) Power Consumption Metrics: To compare the energy performance of GelNeuro with conventional computing plat-forms, we measured power consumption under the 80 ms texture-classification task. Power on the Speck2f board was recorded and compared with an Intel i9-14900KF CPU and an NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU, both while idling and during continuous inference. The active-inference power is reported together with the idle power, and their difference is used as the dynamic inference component. IV. E XPERIMENTAL RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 

A. Effect of Training and Inference Windows 

The impact of matched training and testing windows rang-ing from 20 ms to 150 ms was initially evaluated in both PC software simulation and physical Speck hardware deployment. At 20-40 ms, the contact-induced event stream is still sparse, and the deployed SCNN has limited time to accumulate mem-brane potential across layers; the on-board accuracy therefore remains low. The PC model already reaches higher accuracy in the same short windows, suggesting that early tactile cues are present, while the fixed-point hardware mapping and finite on-chip integration time make early readout more difficult. Increasing the window provides more spatiotemporal evidence and raises the Speck accuracy to 96.3% at Ttrain = 80 

ms. Further extending the window to 120 ms or 150 ms gives only marginal improvement, with accuracy fluctuating around 97%. We therefore use 80 ms as the baseline window for the following on-chip evaluation, since it captures most discriminative events while preserving a short response time. Subsequently, early inference capabilities were investigated by applying the model trained at the 80 ms baseline while systematically shortening the actual on-board inference win-dow (Tinfer ). As illustrated in Fig. 3, on-device accuracy increases with the available integration time, while the silent rate decreases. With only 20 ms of input, the stimulus is often too short for the output layer to generate a decision, resulting in a silent rate of 19.8% and an accuracy of 27.9%. At 60 ms, accuracy increases to 85.4% and the silent rate decreases to 4.4%. With the full 80 ms inference window, the system reaches 96.3% accuracy and a silent rate of 0.2%. This trend reflects the progressive accumulation of tactile evidence on the chip: the network can produce early, coarse decisions from short contact segments, and a modest extension of the window is sufficient to obtain stable texture recognition.

D. Edge Power Consumption 

Board-level active power consumption was measured under the baseline 80 ms inference task and systematically compared against standard CPU and GPU baselines. As illustrated in Fig. 6, the Speck2f platform draws merely 1.4 mW in the idle state, whereas the Intel i9-14900KF CPU and NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU exhibit idle power draws of 7.7 W and 25.9 W, respectively. During continuous tactile spike-stream inference, the total active power of the Speck2f board rises to 19.6 mW, representing a dynamic power increment of 18.2 mW above its idle baseline. Evaluated on the identical texture classification task, the CPU and GPU baselines incur substantial power overheads of 74.7 W and 87.4 W, respectively. Consequently, the operating power of the integrated GelNeuro system is over three orders of magnitude lower than conventional digital com-puting architectures, delivering an approximate 3800 × energy reduction relative to the high-performance CPU baseline. This reduction comes from the event-driven operation of both the tactile front end and the spiking processor. When no tactile events are generated, the Speck2f board remains close to its idle state. During sliding contact, incoming events trigger updates only in the routed on-chip neurons, so computation scales with tactile activity rather than with a fixed image frame rate. By directly interfacing an elastomeric optical tactile front end with the Speck2f neuromorphic SoC, the proposed architecture effectively bridges the traditional separation be-tween sensing and computation in visuo-tactile perception. Within this tightly integrated configuration, contact-induced tactile events are routed directly through the on-chip network to the SCNN classifier, completely bypassing host-computer preprocessing during active inference. Physical hardware-in-the-loop evaluations demonstrate that the complete system achieves a 96.3% texture classification accuracy within a brief 80 ms inference window, while drawing only 19.6 mW of board-level active power. As summarized in Table II, these at-tributes position GelNeuro in a distinctly superior deployment regime relative to prior texture-recognition pipelines, which remain fundamentally constrained by host-side preprocessing overheads or sensor-to-processor hardware relays. Further-more, compared with standard CPU/GPU platforms, GelNeuro suppresses active inference power by more than three orders of magnitude. These quantitative findings substantiate that direct, event-driven near-sensor processing can simultaneously optimize processing latency and power efficiency without compromising texture-recognition fidelity. The hardware evaluations also elucidate several fundamen-tal challenges that persist when migrating an offline-trained SCNN onto physical neuromorphic substrates. Although the Speck2f SoC processes asynchronous events continuously, standard backpropagation through time (BPTT) training inher-ently requires the continuous event stream to be discretized into uniform temporal bins. In preliminary evaluations con-ducted at a single 0.5 mm indentation depth, refining the bin width from 1 ms to 0.5 ms elevated the accuracy within a tight 20 ms window from 93.4% to 96.1%. However, shrinking the bin width heavily extends the temporal unrolling length during BPTT, thereby exponentially driving up the training memory overhead. Consequently, the 1 ms temporal discretization adopted in this work represents a pragmatic trade-off bound by the current software optimization framework, rather than an intrinsic constraint of the asynchronous chip architecture. Fixed-point hardware quantization serves as another primary source of accuracy degradation. While the proposed hardware-aware weight clamping strategy mitigates quantization drift when mapping the 32-bit floating-point software model to the 8-bit fixed-point (INT8) hardware representation, it cannot completely close the precision gap induced by hardware vir-tualization. Furthermore, continuous deployment on physical silicon is inevitably susceptible to circuit-level crosstalk and thermal fluctuations. The 10% random input masking deployed during training effectively bolsters the network’s resilience against such environmental perturbations, yet real-world hard-ware occasionally exhibits complex non-ideal noise distribu-tions that are not fully spanned by offline synthetic data. These experimental insights directly illuminate subsequent optimization trajectories. Future endeavors will focus on inves-tigating advanced compilation strategies to further suppress the remaining 8-bit mapping loss, specifically exploring whether quantization-aware training (QAT) can yield performance su-perior to the current post-training weight-clamping paradigm. Additionally, lightweight surrogate-gradient or proxy-gradient algorithms will be explored to accommodate finer temporal discretizations without incurring the severe memory penalties of standard BPTT. V. C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK 

This work demonstrates GelNeuro as a sensing-computing integrated neuromorphic tactile system, using texture recog-nition as a validation task for direct sensor-to-chip event processing. By routing tactile events from the optical gel front end to the on-chip SCNN processor without host-side preprocessing, GelNeuro preserves the sparse temporal struc-ture of contact-induced signals through to on-chip inference. On 15 natural textures, the deployed system achieved 96.3% accuracy within an 80 ms inference window while consuming 19.6 mW of board-level active power, and retained useful performance at unseen contact depths. These results support direct neuromorphic tactile processing as a practical route toward low-latency, low-power perception on robotic edge platforms. In future investigations, beyond tactile texture recognition, the core architecture of GelNeuro is well-suited for extension to transient slip detection and real-time, closed-loop slip-preventive control. In these time-critical scenarios, the ultra-low-latency characteristics inherited from the direct sensor-to-chip perception pathway are expected to provide a more pronounced and definitive operational advantage.
